# Crypto roadmap

**Purpose:** Document how cryptocurrency could eventually support `companion-self`, `grace-mar`, and `work-build-ai` without turning crypto into the product itself. Crypto is relevant here when it strengthens sovereignty, portability, provenance, access control, or settlement independence.

**See also:** [PORTABILITY](portability.md), [OPENCLAW-INTEGRATION](openclaw-integration.md), [WORK-BUILD-AI](skill-work/work-build-ai/README.md).

---

## 1. Core Principle

**Crypto is not the product layer.** The canonical Record remains human-governed and primarily off-chain. Cryptocurrency and related cryptographic primitives may help prove authority, secure transfer, control access, and move value, but they must not become the place where canonical identity truth lives.

Use crypto where it reinforces:

- human approval
- provenance
- portability
- access control
- settlement independence

Do not use crypto where it weakens clarity, increases coercion, or turns identity into a speculative asset.

---

## 2. Why This Matters

As AI systems become more persistent and more agentic, the scarce asset is no longer raw intelligence. The scarce asset is governed continuity:

- who the system serves
- what may become true
- how authority is proven
- how state moves across runtimes
- how value moves without surrendering control

Crypto primitives fit naturally at the boundaries of authority, transfer, and settlement. They are most useful here as a **sovereignty layer**, not as a replacement for the gate.

---

## 3. Near-term Uses

### 3.1 Payment rail

Use BTC or stablecoins for:

- `work-build-ai` diagnostics and implementation sprints
- bounded `WORK`-territory deliverables
- international payments
- partner or contractor payouts later

**Why this fits:** lower platform dependence, cleaner cross-border settlement, and alignment with sovereignty themes.

**Guardrail:** crypto payments should remain optional and operationally simple.

### 3.2 Signed receipts

Use cryptographic signing or hash-anchored receipts for:

- approved merges
- export bundles
- client deliverables
- major handoffs

**Why this fits:** it strengthens provenance, creates tamper evidence, and proves what was approved and when.

### 3.3 Crypto accounting discipline

If crypto revenue is received:

- issue a receipt
- note payment rail
- record fiat equivalent at time of receipt
- keep accounting legible

**Guardrail:** the payment rail must not distort the underlying governance model.

---

## 4. Medium-term Uses

### 4.1 Signed export bundles

Exports may eventually include:

- bundle hash
- signer identity
- timestamp
- intended recipient or runtime
- optional scope or expiration

**Why this fits:** it improves portability, makes handoffs more trustworthy, and reinforces client-owned authority over downstream runtimes.

### 4.2 Signed handoff records

When a Record-derived bundle moves to another school, runtime, or system, a signed handoff could prove:

- who authorized the transfer
- what was transferred
- when it happened
- under what scope

This is especially aligned with the portability thesis.

### 4.3 Wallet-based access control

A wallet or keypair could eventually help control:

- who can open a private export
- which runtime may consume a bundle
- whether access can be revoked or rotated
- who is recognized as the authority over a private asset

**Guardrail:** begin as optional access infrastructure, not as the default user experience.

### 4.4 Encrypted private bundle sharing

A future export flow could support:

- generate bundle
- encrypt for recipient
- authorize access with keys or wallet-linked identity
- revoke or rotate if needed

Good fit for private handoffs, schools, therapists/coaches, partner runtimes, and governed client delivery.

---

## 5. Long-term Uses

### 5.1 Human-controlled agent spending

As agents become more action-taking, crypto rails could support:

- bounded budgets
- spend policies
- auditable machine transactions
- human-defined financial constraints

**Why this fits:** it mirrors the same logic as the identity gate. AI may act within bounds; the human remains the authority.

### 5.2 Portable authority layer

Long term, `companion-self` could support:

- signed approval events
- signed Record snapshots
- signed continuity state
- signed access grants to downstream runtimes

This would strengthen the idea that the human's authority is portable across systems, not trapped in one platform.

### 5.3 Company-level trust infrastructure

If `work-build-ai` becomes a real firm, crypto primitives could support:

- signed proofs of delivery
- signed implementation receipts
- partner payout flows
- milestone release models
- client-owned authorization over deployed AI surfaces

---

## 7. Best First Build

The best first crypto-adjacent implementation candidate is:

### Signed merge receipts and signed export bundles

**Why first:**

- strongly aligned with the thesis
- useful without introducing token economics
- directly improves provenance and portability
- reinforces human authority
- can be introduced incrementally

This should come before any tokenized or on-chain experiment.

---

## 8. Guardrails

### Do not do these early

- do not put canonical Record truth on-chain
- do not tokenize the self
- do not make wallet use mandatory for ordinary operation
- do not introduce speculative token economics
- do not let crypto become the narrative instead of sovereignty

### Prefer these instead

- signatures over speculation
- receipts over tokens
- access control over hype
- bounded financial authority over autonomous spend
- optional rails over mandatory complexity

---

## 9. Phased Roadmap

### Phase 1 — Practical rails

- accept BTC or stablecoins for selected services
- issue receipts
- define accounting discipline

### Phase 2 — Signed artifacts

- signed merge receipts
- signed export bundles
- signed handoff records

### Phase 3 — Access control

- encrypted exports
- recipient-scoped sharing
- optional wallet-based authorization

### Phase 4 — Agent finance

- bounded agent budgets
- auditable machine spending
- human-defined spend policies
