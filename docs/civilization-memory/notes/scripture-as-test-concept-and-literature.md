# Scripture as test: concept and literature search

**Purpose:** State the claim that the prophetic/violent scriptures function as a **test** — the way to win is to choose not to act out the script (reason and love vs. the opposite). Document a search of academic literature for precedents and apply the concept to Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism. This note supports the Simple Condition and One Subject Many Tongues.

**Related:** [THE-SIMPLE-CONDITION](../essays/THE-SIMPLE-CONDITION.md) (seam, blade, face not category), [ONE-SUBJECT-MANY-TONGUES](../essays/ONE-SUBJECT-MANY-TONGUES.md) (Christianity and Islam, shared reference).

---

## The concept

**The prophetic scriptures are a test. The way to win is to choose not to act out the script.**

- The violent or “prophetic” passages in sacred texts (commands to destroy, verses of the sword, holy war) are not there to be obeyed. They are there as a **test**.
- **Winning** the test = refusing to perform the script. Choosing **reason and love** instead of the opposite (obedience to the violent command, category over face).
- The tradition stays intact; the move is to **read** those passages as test, not as script to be enacted. Making the seam visible is showing that the violent script is a trial — and the right response is to refuse to act it out.

**Unifying Christianity and Islam:** If both traditions adopt the same reading — “these passages are a test; we refuse to act them out; we choose reason and love” — they share one **action** (refusing to perform the violent script) while each keeps its own **language** and theology. Same referent: the script is a test; we don’t play that part. That is “one subject, many tongues” in practice: coordination on the condition (stop, see the face) without doctrinal merger.

---

## Application to Hinduism and Buddhism

The same move applies. Each tradition has a seam: beauty (compassion, unity, the sacred) and blade (passages or teachings that have been used to justify violence). Reading the blade as **test** — and winning by refusing to act it out — works in each tradition's own terms.

**Hinduism.** The tradition carries both the vision of unity (*tat tvam asi*; the Self in all beings) and *ahimsa* (non-harm) and, in the same corpus, texts that have been read as commanding or justifying violence: the Bhagavad Gita (Krishna's exhortation to Arjuna to fight; the duty of the warrior), the Mahabharata and Ramayana (righteous war, destruction of the enemy), and strands of *dharma* that have been invoked for caste or communal violence. The seam is visible: "every life is the same Self" and "do not harm" versus "under certain conditions, violence is dharmic duty." **Scripture as test:** The passages that seem to command or glorify violence are a test. The way to win is to choose not to act out that script — to choose *ahimsa*, reason, and the recognition that the other is the same Self (*tat tvam asi*) over the opposite. The tradition can name it in its own language: the Gita's "battle" as inner struggle (a reading Gandhi and others have advanced); the violent narrative as *trial* of whether we will treat the other as category (enemy, *asura*, other) or as the one Self. No need to discard the texts; read them as test. Refuse to perform the script.

**Buddhism.** The tradition is built on the first precept (do not kill), *karuna* (compassion), and the view that all beings are worthy of liberation. It also has histories and texts that have been used to justify violence: warrior monks in some cultures, nationalist or ethnic readings that have turned the *sangha* or the dharma into a banner for conflict, and strands of tantric or "skillful means" argument that have sometimes been stretched to excuse harm. The seam: the Buddha's teaching of non-harm and compassion versus any script that invites us to treat the other as enemy or obstacle. **Scripture as test:** Any passage or interpretation that invites violence is a test. Winning is refusing to act it out — choosing the precepts, *karuna*, and the recognition of the other as suffering being over the opposite. The tradition can name it in its own language: the Buddha did not teach killing; this is a test of whether we cling to the script of conflict (aversion, hatred) or choose the path of release. One subject, many tongues: Buddhism says it in Pali, Sanskrit, or the vernacular of its schools; the referent is the same. Refuse to perform the script.

**One subject, four (and more) tongues.** Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism need not agree on God, revelation, rebirth, or the nature of the self. They can agree on one move: the violent or prophetic script in our texts is a test; we refuse to act it out; we choose reason and love (or *ahimsa*, *karuna*, face, *dharma* as non-harm). Same referent — the condition (stop, see the face) — each in its own grammar. That is coordination without creedal merger.

**Tension with prophecy-fulfillment readings (e.g. Christian and Jewish Zionism).** Christian Zionism and Jewish religious Zionism, in the forms that treat prophecy as a script to be fulfilled in history, are **inherently opposed** to the scripture-as-test idea. Scripture-as-test says: the prophetic script is a trial; winning = refusing to perform it. Prophecy-fulfillment says: the prophetic script is a program; winning = helping to bring it about (ingathering, restoration, Temple, end-times). In the latter, the text is to be **enacted**, not refused — so the same passages that scripture-as-test reads as "do not act this out" are read as "we must act this out." Not every Zionist reading is prophecy-fulfillment; secular or non-messianic Zionism can be about safety or self-determination without treating scripture as script. But **Christian Zionism** (e.g. support for Israel as a step toward prophesied scenarios) and **Jewish religious Zionism** in its prophecy-fulfilling form are structurally opposed: they are committed to carrying out the prophecy, and thus to treating the script as mandate rather than test.

## Literature search summary (academic precedents)

A search of existing academic literature was conducted to determine whether this concept is original or has clear precedents. Summary below.

### 1. Levinas and the Akedah (Binding of Isaac)

**Close precedent for a single story.**

- Genesis 22 explicitly presents the episode as a **test** (God tests Abraham). The traditional reading: test of obedience; Abraham obeys; God stops him. Kierkegaard (*Fear and Trembling*): Abraham obeys — “teleological suspension of the ethical.”
- **Emmanuel Levinas** reverses the valence: the “test” is whether we put the **concrete other** (Isaac) before the divine command. For Levinas, the right response is **refusing** to sacrifice — “ethics before religion.” Abraham’s obligation to Isaac takes precedence; the test is passed by *not* sacrificing, by returning to the ethical (the face of the other).
- **Relevance:** The “scripture as test, win by refusing to act” structure appears clearly in Levinas’s reading of the Akedah. The concept in this note **generalizes** that move from one story to all violent/prophetic scripture and makes “refuse to act out the script” the unifying rule.

**Sources (representative):** Levinas on the Binding of Isaac; “The Binding of Abraham: Levinas’s Moment in Kierkegaard’s Fear and Trembling” (Springer); “Levinas and Kierkegaard: The Akedah, the Dao, and Aporetic Ethics” (Wiley); MDPI “Alterity, Alacrity, and Excess” (Rosenzweig, Levinas, Derrida, Marion on Abraham).

### 2. Violent scripture: hermeneutics and “don’t act out”

**Partial precedent; different framing.**

- Scholarship on “divine violence,” “texts of terror,” and the Old Testament (e.g. Eric Seibert, *The Violence of Scripture*; L. Daniel Hawk; Rabbi Jonathan Sacks, *Not in God’s Name*) stresses: (1) do not use these texts to justify violence; (2) read in context; (3) prefer non‑violent or peace‑oriented interpretations; (4) “ethically responsible reading.”
- Rabbi Sacks: “Fundamentalism—text without interpretation—is an act of violence against tradition.” The move is **reinterpretation** (context, trajectory, love) rather than literal obedience.
- **Relevance:** The field already says “don’t act out the violent script” and “choose the trajectory of love.” It does **not** typically say “these passages are *there as a test*; the point is to refuse to perform them.” The emphasis is on *how to interpret*, not on “test + refusal to act” as the *nature* of the passage.

**Sources (representative):** Seibert, *The Violence of Scripture: Overcoming the Old Testament’s Troubling Legacy*; “Recent Research on Divine Violence in the Old Testament” (SAGE); “Hard Texts and Interfaith Peace: Rabbi Jonathan Sacks’s Not in God’s Name” (Public Discourse); “Violence in the Bible: Towards a Non-Violent Reading” (Bible Interp).

### 3. Satan’s use of scripture (temptation narratives)

**Structural parallel.**

- In the temptation narratives (e.g. Luke 4), Satan **uses** scripture to tempt Jesus. So scripture can be the *vehicle* of a test: the test is whether you do what the quoted script seems to ask. “Winning” is not doing it (Jesus refuses).
- **Relevance:** Structurally similar to “the script is put in front of you as a test; winning is not acting it out.” The tradition usually frames this as “Satan **misuses** scripture,” not “the violent verses are themselves the test.”

**Sources (representative):** “Interpreting Scripture with Satan?: The Devil’s Use of Scripture in Luke’s Temptation Narrative” (Tyndale Bulletin).

### 4. Christian–Muslim dialogue on violent scripture

**Dialogue exists; the specific “test + refuse” unifying hermeneutic was not found.**

- **A Common Word** and the **Scripture & Violence** project (Cambridge, etc.) focus on: shared responsibility in interpreting one’s own and others’ texts; “transformative” or non‑violent readings; dialogue so “texts of terror” are not weaponized.
- **Relevance:** The shared move in the literature is “responsible interpretation” and “dialogue.” The explicit formulation — “We both treat these verses as a **test**; we both **refuse to act out the script**; that is our shared move” — did not appear as a stated common hermeneutic in the sources consulted.

**Sources (representative):** A Common Word (acommonword.com); “Transformative Readings of Sacred Scriptures: Christians and Muslims in Dialogue” (Lutheran World Federation); The Scripture & Violence Project (scriptureandviolence.org, interfaith.cam.ac.uk).

### 5. Refusing immoral orders (Jewish tradition)

- Jewish sources (e.g. midwives in Exodus; Talmud on illegal orders; Rabbi Sacks on “not obeying immoral orders”) stress: moral law can override human or apparently divine commands; refuse to carry out genocide or clearly immoral acts.
- **Relevance:** Supports “refuse to act out the script” as a **moral option** within tradition. Does not frame the violent *text itself* as “a test.”

---

## Conclusion on originality

- **Not original for one story:** The “test + refuse to act” structure is present in Levinas’s reading of the Akedah.
- **Original as a general hermeneutic:** Treating **all** (or all violent/prophetic) scripture as *test*, and “winning” as *choosing not to act out the script* (reason and love vs. the opposite), does not appear as a named, general position in the literature searched.
- **Original as unifying move:** Using that same “script as test; we refuse to perform it” as the **shared** way Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism (and thus the traditions to each other) relate to their violent texts also did not appear in the sources found.

The **components** (Levinas on the Akedah; “don’t act out the violent script”; Christian–Muslim dialogue on violent texts) exist. The **synthesis** — violent/prophetic scripture as test, winning = not acting it out, reason and love vs. the opposite, and that as the unifying relation across Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism — appears to be **original** or at least not clearly stated in the academic material consulted. Further specialist search (e.g. in Islamic hermeneutics, comparative theology, or peace studies) could refine this.
