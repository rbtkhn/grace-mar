# Exercise: See the Face — For School Children

**Purpose:** Derive a classroom exercise from the [face–category–blade](face-category-blade.md) idea so children learn: we sometimes see a **category** (a label) instead of a **person**; we can ask "who is the person behind the label?"; and when we see the specific person, the label can soften. No graphic violence; no required religious content. Suitable for secular and faith-based settings.

**Source:** [face-category-blade.md](face-category-blade.md); [THE-SIMPLE-CONDITION](../essays/THE-SIMPLE-CONDITION.md) §IX (the children's question).

**Suggested age range:** Grades 4–5 through 9–10. Simplify language for younger; add reflection prompts for older.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end of the exercise, students will:

1. **Notice** that we sometimes think about people as "a category" (a label) instead of as a specific person.
2. **Practice** asking "Who is the person behind the label?" and naming one specific thing about that person that isn't the category.
3. **Name** that "seeing the face" means letting the specific person show up — not just the label.

---

## Core idea (for the teacher)

When we replace a **specific person** (a face, a name, a story) with a **category** ("the new kid," "the bully," "that group"), we stop seeing them as a full person. The exercise teaches children to **find the face inside the category** — to ask who the person is behind the label — and to notice how that can change how we feel and act. No one has to be blamed; the focus is on a habit of attention: see the person, not only the category.

---

## Materials

- None required. Optional: a short story or book excerpt where a character is first seen as a "type" and later as a person (e.g. a newcomer, an outsider, a rival). Or use hypotheticals only.

---

## Part 1: What is a category? (5–8 minutes)

**Teacher says (in your own words):**

"Sometimes we talk about people using a single word or phrase: 'the new kid,' 'the one who always wins,' 'someone from that school,' 'the teacher.' When we do that, we're using a **category** — a label. The label might be true in a way, but it's not the whole person. There's always a specific person behind the label — with a name, a face, things they care about, things they're good at, things they're afraid of. Today we're going to practice **finding the person behind the label**."

**Discussion prompt:**  
"Can you think of a time when someone thought about you using only a label or category? How did that feel? What's something about you that the label didn't capture?"

(No one has to answer out loud; they can think, pair-share, or volunteer.)

---

## Part 2: Find the face inside the category (10–12 minutes)

**Activity:**

1. **Name a category** that sometimes gets used at school or in the world (e.g. "the new kid," "the troublemaker," "someone from the other team," "the person who doesn't speak our language"). Teacher can suggest one or take one from the class. Use a category that is **not** tied to a real, named student in the room to avoid embarrassment or targeting.

2. **Ask:** "If we could see **one specific person** who sometimes gets that label — not the whole group, just one person — what might be true about them that the label doesn't tell us?"  
   Examples: a name, something they like to do, something they're proud of, someone they love, something they're worried about, something they're good at.

3. **Say:** "When we do that, we're **finding the face inside the category**. We're not throwing away the label; we're adding the person. The person was always there. We're just paying attention."

**Optional for older students:**  
"If we only ever see the category and never the person, we can end up acting in ways we might not if we saw the person. So the habit we're practicing is: **see the face**. When you notice you're thinking about someone only as a label, ask: who is the person? What's one thing that's true about them that isn't the category?"

---

## Part 3: The question for stories (5–8 minutes)

**Teacher says (in your own words):**

"There's a question we can carry when we hear stories — in books, in the news, in history. The question is: **Does anyone have to be hurt for this to be true?**  
When the answer is *no* — when the story is about kindness, or fairness, or people helping each other — that's something we can keep. When the answer is *yes* — when the story says someone *has* to be hurt or put down for the story to work — we can notice that. We don't have to agree. We can see the seam: the part that cares for people, and the part that treats someone as only a category or an enemy. Seeing the seam helps us choose."

**Discussion (optional):**  
"Can you think of a story or a situation where someone was treated as a category instead of as a person? What would it mean to 'see the face' in that story?"

---

## Closing (2–3 minutes)

**Teacher says:**

"Today we practiced **seeing the face** — finding the person behind the label. When you notice yourself thinking about someone only as a category, you can ask: who is the person? What's one thing that's true about them that the label doesn't tell? That habit is something you can use anywhere: at school, at home, in the world."

---

## Notes for the teacher

- **Safety:** Do not ask children to name real peers as "the bully" or "the new kid" in a way that singles them out. Use "someone who might get that label" or a character from a book. The goal is the habit (find the person), not public confession or blame.
- **Religion:** The exercise is secular. In a faith-based setting, you can add: "In many traditions, every person is said to have a face — to be someone, not only a category. Seeing the face is a way to respect that."
- **Connection to the Simple Condition:** The full letter to religious leaders is for adults. This exercise is the same idea in child-appropriate form: the blade = seeing only the category; the expulsion = see the face again. The children's question ("Does anyone have to be hurt for this to be true?") is from the letter and can be used with stories without teaching the letter itself.
- **Time:** Total about 25–35 minutes. Can be one session or split (Part 1–2 one day, Part 3 another day).
