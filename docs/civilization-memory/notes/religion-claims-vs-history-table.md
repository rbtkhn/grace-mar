# Religion: claims/teachings vs. history and present

**Purpose:** Table comparing what each major tradition claims or teaches (ideals) with what it has actually done in history and in the present. Supports the seam/beauty-and-blade framing: every tradition carries both.

**Integrated:** The table appears in **Applied Theology** (Part 1 — The Call, after section II "What Each of You Carries") under the heading "Teachings vs. practice."

## Table: teachings vs. practice

| Tradition | What it claims / teaches | What it has done in history | What it does in the present |
|-----------|--------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Christianity** | Love God and neighbour; love your enemy; every person in the image of God; beatitudes; mercy, forgiveness, peace; “turn the other cheek”; kingdom of peace, swords into plowshares. | Crusades, religious wars, Inquisition, witch trials, conquest and colonisation blessed by clergy; pogroms; wars of religion; blessing of armies and rulers; slavery justified by scripture. | Both: extensive charity, hospitals, education, peacebuilding, abolitionist and civil-rights witness; and ongoing blessing of war, nationalist fusion with state violence, persecution of minorities in some regions, culture wars and dehumanising rhetoric. |
| **Islam** | Mercy and compassion (Rahman, Rahim); justice; sacredness of life; care for orphan and stranger; no compulsion in religion; peace (salam); vision of a world free of oppression. | Conquests; jihad as armed struggle; sectarian wars; empire and caliphate; persecution of dissidents and minorities; slavery; verses of the sword invoked for war. | Both: charity (zakat), education, relief work, reform and peace movements; and sectarian violence, terrorism invoked in religious terms, blasphemy laws and persecution, authoritarian fusion of religion and state in some countries. |
| **Judaism** | Sanctity of life; “choose life”; tikkun olam (repair of the world); prophetic justice for the vulnerable; Sabbath rest; covenant and law as path to flourishing. | Conquest of Canaan (as narrated); holy war in scripture; occasional zealot violence; later diaspora often victim rather than perpetrator of religious violence; messianic and nationalist strands. | Both: strong emphasis on education, charity, social justice in many streams; and in some forms: religious nationalism, settlement and occupation justified by scripture, prophecy-fulfillment readings that treat land/state as mandate; also targeted by antisemitic violence. |
| **Hinduism** | Unity of self (tat tvam asi); ahimsa (non-harm); dharma as order and duty; compassion; moksha as release from suffering; many paths to the divine. | Righteous war in epics (Mahabharata, Ramayana); caste hierarchy and violence justified by dharma; communal violence; destruction of temples and sects; warrior and kingly duty to fight. | Both: nonviolent movements (e.g. Gandhi’s drawing on ahimsa); pluralism and philosophy; and Hindu nationalism, communal violence, caste-based oppression and violence, lynching and vigilante action in the name of cow/dharma. |
| **Buddhism** | First precept: do not kill; karuna (compassion); all beings worthy of liberation; end of dukkha; middle way; non-harm as core. | Warrior monks in some cultures; Buddhist kings and armies; sectarian and ethnic violence (e.g. Sri Lanka, Japan, Tibet); nationalist use of sangha; tantric “skillful means” invoked to excuse harm. | Both: global peace and mindfulness movements, humanitarian work, dialogue; and in some regions: Buddhist nationalism, violence against Muslims and others (e.g. Myanmar, Sri Lanka), fusion with state and ethnic identity. |
| **Zoroastrianism** | Asha (truth, order) vs. Druj (lie, chaos); two primal spirits; kingship conditional on advancing order; endurance as ethical resistance. | Enforcement of orthodoxy; persecution of "Druj" (heretics, enemies of order); conquest and state religion used to justify violence. | Small surviving communities; ethical-structural logic absorbed into Persian Islam and statecraft; seam visible where "order" justifies coercion. |

---

## Notes on use

- **“Claims/teaches”** = central ideals and doctrines that point toward love, peace, compassion, or reduction of harm. Not exhaustive; each tradition has more.
- **History and present** = well-attested institutional and collective behaviour, not every believer. Many adherents in every tradition have lived the ideals; the table highlights the gap between teachings and collective record.
- **Both** in the present = traditions are not monolithic; they contain peacebuilding and violence-justifying strands at once. The seam (beauty and blade) is visible in current as well as historical practice.
- This table supports the thesis that **all religions carry the seam**: the same traditions that teach love and peace have also authorised or practised war and suffering. Making the seam visible is the first step toward choosing the beauty over the blade.
