# Research brief: "Condition" in the traditions

**Purpose:** Map where Christianity, Islam, Judaism, Hinduism, and Buddhism **explicitly or implicitly** say that something must hold **before** the awaited future (return, restoration, judgment, moksha, nirvana, etc.) — and whether that "something" can be read as **peace or the cessation of killing**. Supports the book's central claim (peace as **condition** for what the traditions await, not the reward after) and Act I (the letter to religious leaders).

**Related:** [Beauty and the Blade manuscript](../book/beauty-and-the-blade-manuscript.md) (Terms: "Condition"; Act I: The Call, "What if the cessation of killing is not the consequence of the return, but the condition for it?"); [THE-SIMPLE-CONDITION](../essays/THE-SIMPLE-CONDITION.md); [ONE-SUBJECT-MANY-TONGUES](../essays/ONE-SUBJECT-MANY-TONGUES.md).

---

## Research questions

1. **Christianity:** Where do scripture or tradition say that X must hold before the Second Coming, the Kingdom, or the restoration of all things? E.g. "until the full number …" (Romans?), "this gospel … then the end" (Matthew 24); repentance, peace, or "love one another" as prerequisite. Map: (a) explicit conditional language; (b) passages that can be read as "peace as condition"; (c) dominant readings (e.g. divine initiative vs human preparation). One-page summary per tradition.
2. **Islam:** Where do Qur'an or hadith say that something must hold before the Hour, justice, or the world made right? E.g. conditions for the Day of Judgment; *salam* (peace) as prerequisite or as characteristic of the awaited state; "no compulsion in religion" and order. Map explicit and implicit condition; note where peace/cessation of killing can be read as condition. One-page summary.
3. **Judaism:** Where do Tanakh, Talmud, or tradition say that X must hold before the messianic age, *tikkun olam*, or the ingathering? E.g. "choose life"; Sabbath and peace; repentance (*teshuvah*) and the coming of the Messiah; land and covenant. Map condition; note peace or cessation of violence as possible condition. One-page summary.
4. **Hinduism:** Where do texts or tradition say that X must hold before *moksha*, *dharma* fully restored, or the end of the age? E.g. *dharma* and order; *ahimsa* and the path; conditions for the *avatara* or the end of *Kali Yuga*. Map; note whether peace/non-harm can be read as condition. One-page summary.
5. **Buddhism:** Where do sutras or tradition say that X must hold before awakening, the Pure Land, or the end of suffering? E.g. precepts, *karuna*; conditions for the *Buddha* or *Bodhisattva* to appear; peace as path or as result. Map; note peace/non-harm as condition. One-page summary.
6. **Synthesis:** One table: Tradition | Awaited future | Explicit or implicit "X must hold" | Can "peace / cessation of killing" be read as that X? | Key passages. One-page narrative: "The condition across five traditions" — for the book's claim that **killing delays what all of them say they are waiting for**.

---

## Key sources

**Christianity:**
- NT: Matthew 24, Romans 9–11, Revelation; "until …" and "then …" constructions; commentaries on Second Coming and conditions (e.g. N.T. Wright, or traditional eschatology).
- Patristic or medieval: Augustine on peace (*City of God*); any "preparation" or "disposition" for the Kingdom.

**Islam:**
- Qur'an: suras on the Hour, judgment, peace (*salam*), and "no compulsion"; hadith on signs of the Hour and on peace.
- Secondary: Firestone, Sachedina, or others on Islamic eschatology and ethics.

**Judaism:**
- Tanakh: prophetic passages on peace, "swords into plowshares," and conditions for the messianic age; Talmud or midrash on *teshuvah* and the coming of the Messiah.
- Secondary: Jewish eschatology and *tikkun olam*; one or two standard surveys.

**Hinduism:**
- Key texts: *Bhagavad Gita*, *Dharma*-related passages; *Puranas* or *Manusmriti* on *dharma* and ages; *ahimsa* and the path to liberation.
- Secondary: survey of Hindu eschatology and ethics (e.g. *dharma* as condition).

**Buddhism:**
- Sutras: conditions for awakening or for the *Buddha* to appear; precepts and *karuna*; Pure Land conditions if relevant.
- Secondary: Buddhist ethics and "conditions for the end of suffering."

**Comparative:**
- Any comparative theology or comparative eschatology that addresses "conditions for the awaited future" across traditions.

## Method / outline

1. **Per tradition:** (a) List 3–7 key passages where "X must hold before Y" (awaited future) is stated or implied. (b) For each, note: explicit conditional language (yes/no); can "peace" or "cessation of killing" be read as X? (c) Summarize in one page: "In [tradition], the awaited future is …; the tradition says or implies that … must hold; peace / non-harm can [or cannot] be read as that condition because …."
2. **Table:** Tradition | Awaited future | "X must hold" (quote or paraphrase) | Peace as condition? (yes / partial / no) | Key passages.
3. **Synthesis:** One page: "The condition in five traditions." (a) The book's claim: killing delays what they all await. (b) For each tradition, one sentence: "[Tradition] can [or can with the following reading] support the claim that peace is the condition because …." (c) Note tensions: e.g. divine initiative vs human preparation; literal vs ethical readings. (d) Conclusion: sufficient support (or clear limits) for the letter to religious leaders.
4. **Applied Theology output:** (a) Draft or refine 1–2 sentences for Act I (The Call) with **citation** for each tradition: "In [tradition], [passage or concept] can be read as saying that [peace / X] must hold before [awaited future]." (b) Optional: short footnote or endnote per tradition with key passages. (c) If a tradition does not support the claim clearly, say so and suggest how the letter can still invite (e.g. "we ask you to consider whether …").
