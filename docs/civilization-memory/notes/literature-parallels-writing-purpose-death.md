# Literature and philosophy: parallels to this corpus

**Purpose:** Catalog concepts in literature and philosophy that resemble or support the theses in this book: writing (making-with) as peak activity; purpose as creating memories; no need to fear death or seek eternal life; prime objective to keep the species writing; non-zero-sum good; be fruitful and multiply as flourishing.

---

## Writing, art, and humanity's purpose

**Hannah Arendt**, *The Human Condition* and related work:
- Art is "the highest kind of activity" that *homo faber* is capable of; it gives mortals a "taste of immortality" because works endure beyond the maker.
- Works of art achieve durability that transcends decay — "something immortal achieved by mortal hands."
- Thought transforms feeling into communicable form; inner experience "becomes reified" and enters the world as shareable, permanent.
- **Natality:** each person has the capacity to begin something new; action is a "second birth." The common world is built through word and deed; the polis is "an immortal home for otherwise mortal beings."
- *Parallel:* Writing/art as peak activity; value in what is made and endures, not in the maker's duration; species-level "immortality" through what we build together.

**Jean-Paul Sartre**, *What Is Literature?*:
- Writing is "engaged" with human freedom and responsibility; literature confronts readers with choice and existence.
- Prose is utilitarian and communicative — it expresses perspective and meaning about the human experience.
- Writing as "the self's immanent act of self-transcendence" — liberation from ego toward contributing to universal human good.
- *Parallel:* Writing as meaningful, world-directed activity; the good is in the doing and what is communicated.

**George Orwell**, "Why I Write":
- Writing creates "a sort of private world" where experience and meaning are processed; writing as the activity in which meaning is made.
- *Parallel:* Writing as the locus of meaning-making; the activity itself is central.

**Raymond Tallis** and "the fourth hunger":
- Art answers a basic human hunger beyond physical needs; great art is "far more profound and beautiful than circumstances require."
- *Parallel:* The drive to create (write, make) as constitutive of human purpose.

## Death, immortality, and meaning in the doing

**Epicurus**, *Letter to Menoeceus*:
- "Death is nothing to us" — when we exist, death is not present; when death comes, we do not exist. Fear of death is irrational.
- The wise person "does not deprecate life nor does he fear the cessation of life."
- The key is not length of life but enjoying "the time which is most pleasant"; the same practice teaches how to live well and how to die well.
- *Parallel:* No need to fear death; the good is in the quality of the doing, not in extension of the doer.

**Bernard Williams**, "The Makropulos Case: Reflections on the Tedium of Immortality" (1973):
- Uses the story of Elina Makropulos (300 years of life, then chooses death): immortality would become tedious and meaningless.
- Meaning in life depends on "categorical desires" — projects and commitments that give direction. An immortal life would exhaust such desires; "everything is joyless."
- "Death gives the meaning to life"; mortality makes our commitments and values significant.
- *Parallel:* We need not seek eternal life; endless repetition of the same existence can empty it of meaning; the shape of a finite life (arc, project, "this book") is what makes the activity matter.

**Death and meaning in life (contemporary philosophy):**
- Accepting death's inevitability can free us to focus on meaningful action in the present; the good exists in the doing itself, not in an immortal future.
- *Parallel:* Prime objective is not personal immortality but the continuation of the activity (keep the species writing); the good is now.

## Non-zero-sum and abundance

**Islamic theology** (e.g. Fountain Magazine, contemporary scholars):
- Divine provision is limitless; "abundance cognition" — if all requested gifts, it would not diminish divine dominion.
- Paradise as vastly expansive, not scarce; room for multiple paths.
- Recognizing another's virtue does not diminish one's own; "generous recognition" rather than zero-sum competition.
- *Parallel:* God is non-zero-sum; the good grows with participation; face vs category.

**Christian theology** (e.g. "Beyond Zero-Sum Thinking"):
- Divine abundance as antidote to zero-sum thinking; biblical vision of abundance recovers a moral imagination centered on common good.
- When flourishing is imagined as scarce, outsiders become threats; abundance reframes neighbor and cooperation.
- *Parallel:* Be fruitful and multiply as non-zero-sum; the more who write (or recognise), the more the field grows.

**Game theory and religion:**
- Faith and love can convert prisoner's dilemma into cooperative equilibria; religious practices activate neurochemistry associated with generosity; non-zero-sum framing supports creativity (e.g. oxytocin vs cortisol).
- *Parallel:* Coordination on one subject, many tongues; cooperation as non-zero-sum.

## Species-level purpose and continuation

**Arendt (again):** The polis as "immortal home for otherwise mortal beings"; action and work produce a common world that outlasts the actor. The species continues through what we build and record.

**Implicit in Williams and Epicurus:** The significance of our projects depends on their being *our* projects in a finite span; the continuation of meaning-making across generations is a different question (species continues to have new categorical desires, new projects).

*Parallel:* Prime objective = keep the species writing; secure the conditions so that the activity never stops; multiply the activity, and the good multiplies with it. Non-zero-sum: no one has to lose for another to gain.
