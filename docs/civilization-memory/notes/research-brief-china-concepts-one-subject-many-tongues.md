# Research brief: China concepts — "one subject, many tongues"

**Purpose:** Trace and map Chinese concepts that **name** the book's "one subject, many tongues" and coordination without creedal merger: 和而不同 *he er butong*, 天下 *tianxia*, 夷夏 *yi–xia*. Distinguish **inclusive** readings (one horizon, many rites) from **category-erasing** readings (Sino-centrism, "barbarian" as type). Output supports Applied Theology Act I–II and MEM–CHINA–TIANXIA and related entries.

**Related:** [Beauty and the Blade manuscript](../book/beauty-and-the-blade-manuscript.md) (tianxia, one subject many tongues); [ONE-SUBJECT-MANY-TONGUES](../essays/ONE-SUBJECT-MANY-TONGUES.md); [face-category-blade](face-category-blade.md).

---

## Research questions

1. **和而不同 *he er butong* ("harmonize without uniformity"):** Where does it appear in the *Analects* and later commentaries? How does it express "same subject (harmony, coordination), many expressions (difference)"? Tie explicitly to the book's "one subject, many tongues" and to coordination without creedal merger.
2. **天下 *tianxia* ("all under heaven") in political thought:** Classical and imperial usage vs contemporary (e.g. Zhao Tingyang). Map: (a) **inclusive** reading — one shared horizon, many kingdoms/rites; (b) **blade** reading — Sino-centrism, "barbarian" subordinated. When does *tianxia* support "one frame, many tongues" vs "one name that all must speak"?
3. **夷夏 *yi–xia* (barbarian–civilized):** Where does the distinction function as **cultural difference** (many tongues) vs where does it become a **category** that erases the face of the other? Use 2–3 canonical moments (e.g. debates on 以夏變夷 "using Xia to change Yi," or tributary discourse).

---

## Key sources

**Primary:**
- *Analects* 13.23 and related: 君子和而不同 *junzi he er butong* (and 小人同而不和). Commentaries: Zhu Xi, Cheng Yi; modern exegesis (Slingerland, etc.).
- Classical and imperial uses of 天下: *Mencius*, *Xunzi*, *Lüshi chunqiu*, *Gongyang* tradition; imperial edicts and memorials (e.g. Tang, Song, Ming) where *tianxia* appears.
- 夷夏 discourse: *Gongyang zhuan*, *Spring and Autumn* tradition; Han Yu 原道; Song/Ming debates on "transforming" or "excluding" the other.

**Secondary:**
- Zhao Tingyang, *Tianxia tixi* (天下體系) and related essays; critiques (e.g. "hierarchy," "Sino-centrism") and defences ("inclusive order").
- Callahan, "Chinese Visions of World Order" (and responses).
- Ge Zhaoguang and others on *tianxia* as concept history.
- Papers on "harmony" (和) in Confucian political thought (和而不同, 和諧).
- Tang, Song, or Ming scholarship on tributary system and 華夷 *hua–yi* (civilized–barbarian) as ritual vs category.

## Method / outline

1. ***He er butong*:** (a) Locate *Analects* passage(s) and standard translation. (b) Survey 2–3 major commentaries: how is "harmonize without uniformity" explained? (c) Write one page: how this phrase is the **Chinese name** for "one subject, many tongues" — same referent (coordination, condition), many expressions; no doctrinal merger. (d) Draft one sentence for Applied Theology: e.g. "The Analects name it: 和而不同 — harmonize without uniformity; one subject, many tongues."
2. ***Tianxia*:** (a) List 5–10 classical/imperial uses (with citation): inclusive vs hierarchical. (b) Summarize Zhao Tingyang's *tianxia* and one major critique. (c) Seam map: beauty reading (one horizon, many politics/rites) vs blade reading (Sino-centrism, other as barbarian). (d) Draft or refine MEM–CHINA–TIANXIA paragraph for the book.
3. ***Yi–xia*:** (a) Choose 2–3 moments: e.g. "using Xia to change Yi" (transformation) vs "do not let Yi change Xia" (exclusion). (b) Where does 夷 function as **cultural difference** (many tongues) vs **category** that erases face? (c) One short paragraph for the book: seam visible where the same vocabulary includes or excludes.
4. **Synthesis:** One page: "Three Chinese concepts for one subject, many tongues" — *he er butong*, *tianxia* (inclusive reading), and the *yi–xia* seam. How the book can cite them in Act I or II without claiming one creed.
