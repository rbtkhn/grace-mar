THE COORDINATION HYPOTHESIS

A Scientific Framework for the Simple Condition

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A letter has been circulating — addressed to the religious leaders of the world, proposing that divine return is conditional on humanity ceasing to kill. The letter is theological in language and prophetic in tone. This essay is neither. It asks a different question: regardless of whether the theological claim is true, are the letter's structural claims supported by evidence? And does the mechanism it describes — a coordination signal embedded in religious memory — have a scientific basis?

The answer to both is yes.


I. THE INSTALLATION HYPOTHESIS

The letter claims that the capacity for intergroup killing is not innate but "installed" through narrative transmission in early childhood. This is a testable developmental claim, and the existing evidence supports it.

Infants do not sort by race, nation, or religion. Kinzler, Dupoux, and Spelke (2007) demonstrated that infants show preference for native-language speakers but no racial sorting. Bar-Haim et al. (2006) found that perceptual own-race bias emerges in some measures around three months, but behavioural discrimination — choosing playmates by group, attributing negative traits to outgroups — does not appear until ages three to five. Aboud (1988) documented that explicit prejudice peaks between five and seven, precisely the window when national, religious, and ethnic identity narratives are being consolidated through language, schooling, and ritual participation.

The critical distinction is between preference and hostility. Biology provides in-group preference. Every social species shows it. But the gap between "I prefer members of my group" and "I am willing to kill members of another group" is enormous. Hundreds of studies on the minimal group paradigm (Tajfel et al., 1971) confirm that humans will discriminate in favour of an arbitrarily-assigned in-group — but discrimination in resource allocation is not killing. The leap from preference to lethality requires specific narrative inputs: a history of grievance, a framework of sacred obligation, a category system that renders the outgroup less than fully human.

The letter identifies five transmission channels for these inputs: sacred text, epic literature, national history, civic ritual, and legal structure. Each channel is independently documentable. Each operates during the developmental window of maximum receptivity. Each delivers the same payload: a hierarchy of human value — the belief that under certain conditions, some human lives may be taken.

Hypothesis 1: Children exposed to cross-group physical contact before age five, combined with explicit narrative inoculation ("does anyone have to die for this to be true?"), will show significantly lower dehumanisation scores at age twelve than matched controls without either intervention.

This is a testable prediction. It has not been tested in this specific form.


II. THE DEHUMANISATION MECHANISM

The letter claims that killing requires a specific cognitive operation: the replacement of a specific human face with a generic category. This is not metaphor. It is a description of a documented neural process.

Harris and Fiske (2006) used functional magnetic resonance imaging to demonstrate that when subjects viewed images of severely dehumanised groups — homeless individuals, drug addicts — activation in the medial prefrontal cortex dropped to levels typically associated with perceiving inanimate objects. The mPFC is the brain region most consistently associated with mentalising: the process of attributing mental states to others, of recognising another being as a person with thoughts, feelings, and experiences. When the mPFC goes quiet, the other person becomes, at the neural level, a thing.

This finding has been replicated across contexts. Kteily et al. (2015) demonstrated that blatant dehumanisation — rating outgroups as literally less evolved on an Ascent of Man scale — predicts support for policies involving harm, including military aggression, torture, and drone strikes. Bandura's (1999) work on moral disengagement documented the psychological mechanisms — euphemistic labelling ("collateral damage"), diffusion of responsibility ("I was following orders"), dehumanisation of victims ("they are animals") — that allow ordinarily moral people to participate in atrocities.

The letter's claim that the blade "manifests as clarity" — a cold, clean certainty about what the outgroup "really is" — maps precisely to what Kruglanski (2004) calls the need for cognitive closure: the desire for definite answers over ambiguity. Need for closure increases under threat, time pressure, and cognitive fatigue — exactly the conditions under which decisions to kill are typically made. The "clarity" is not insight. It is premature cognitive closure operating on a dehumanised category.

The letter's proposed antidote — "see the face again" — also has empirical support. Batson et al. (1997) showed that perspective-taking instructions ("imagine how this person feels") increased empathic concern and helping behaviour even toward stigmatised groups. Individuation — providing a name, a biography, a specific story — reverses the dehumanisation effect by reactivating the mPFC. The blade cannot survive contact with a specific human face because face-processing reactivates the neural circuitry that categorisation suppressed.

Hypothesis 2: Subjects primed with a specific individual's face, name, and brief biography will show significantly higher mPFC activation and significantly lower support for lethal policies toward that individual's group, compared to subjects primed with a group-level category label alone.

Versions of this have been tested. The results consistently support the hypothesis.


III. THE CONTACT ANTIDOTE

The letter proposes that physical contact between members of opposing groups — specifically before narrative installation is complete — can inoculate against lethal intergroup hostility. This is a direct application of the contact hypothesis, first formalised by Allport (1954) and now one of the most extensively validated findings in social psychology.

Pettigrew and Tropp (2006) conducted a meta-analysis of 515 studies involving over 250,000 participants and found that intergroup contact reliably reduces prejudice, with a mean effect size of r = -.21. Effects were strongest when contact involved equal status, common goals, intergroup cooperation, and institutional support — but significant prejudice reduction occurred even without these optimal conditions.

The letter's specific proposal — "bodies before stories," meaning cross-group physical play among children before age seven — combines the contact hypothesis with the developmental timing of narrative installation. If the contact occurs before the blade is fully installed, the somatic memory of shared physical experience (playing, eating, touching, laughing together) creates a counter-narrative that is not cognitively mediated and therefore resistant to later cognitive override.

This mechanism is consistent with dual-process models of prejudice (Devine, 1989), which distinguish between automatic associations (fast, implicit, resistant to change) and controlled processes (slow, explicit, amenable to revision). Early cross-group contact would create automatic positive associations that persist even when later narrative inputs attempt to install negative ones.

Hypothesis 3: The protective effect of early cross-group contact against later dehumanisation follows a dose-response curve with a critical period before age five, after which the protective effect diminishes as narrative identity consolidates.

This specific developmental timing prediction has not been tested.


IV. THE UNIFORMITY PROBLEM

The letter observes that the blade's structure is identical across civilisations that developed independently: Mesoamerica, Polynesia, sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia, South Asia, the Near East, Europe. Every independently-developed civilisation produced the same five-element complex: hierarchy of human value, sacred violence, warrior heroism, killing as honour, and conditional theological permission to kill outsiders.

From a cultural evolution perspective, this uniformity can be explained by convergent evolution: the complex solves the same adaptive problem (group defence under conditions of intergroup competition) using the same cognitive architecture (categorisation, threat detection, coalition psychology, narrative memory). This does not require a common historical source. It requires only that the same selection pressures, operating on the same cognitive substrate, reliably produce the same cultural output.

The relevant cognitive architecture includes:

Coalitional psychology (Tooby and Cosmides, 2010): Humans possess dedicated cognitive mechanisms for detecting coalition membership and calibrating behaviour toward in-group and outgroup members. These mechanisms evolved in ancestral small-group environments where intergroup conflict was a significant cause of mortality.

Parochial altruism (Choi and Bowles, 2007): Altruism toward in-group members and hostility toward outgroup members can coevolve, each reinforcing the other. Groups with higher levels of parochial altruism outcompete groups without it, creating a selection pressure that favours the joint trait.

Prestige and dominance hierarchies (Henrich and Gil-White, 2001): Warrior prestige — honouring the most effective killer — is a cultural mechanism for incentivising group defence. It transforms individual sacrifice into reputational gain, solving the free-rider problem in collective violence.

Sacred value psychology (Atran and Ginges, 2012): Beliefs encoded as sacred resist cost-benefit analysis. Once killing is sanctified — declared a sacred duty rather than a pragmatic necessity — it becomes immune to rational persuasion. This explains why the blade persists after the original survival threat has passed: the survival decision was encoded as a sacred value, and sacred values do not update.

The convergent evolution explanation predicts that the complex should appear in every society that faces intergroup competition and possesses narrative transmission capacity. To our knowledge, no exception has been documented. Societies that lack one element of the complex (e.g. no sacred text) compensate with another (e.g. stronger oral epic or honour code). The overall structure is conserved.

Hypothesis 4: The five-element killing complex will be found in every independently-developed human civilisation with intergroup competition history and intergenerational narrative transmission, with compensatory substitution between elements but conservation of the overall structure.

This is a cross-cultural prediction testable against the ethnographic record. Preliminary evidence from the Human Relations Area Files strongly supports it.


V. THE COORDINATION PROBLEM

The letter's central claim — that humanity has not stopped killing because it cannot coordinate the cessation — is a game-theoretic proposition.

Universal peace is a Nash equilibrium: if all players choose not to kill, no individual player gains by unilaterally resuming killing (assuming a large enough coalition of non-killers). But universal war-readiness is also a Nash equilibrium: if all players maintain the capacity and willingness to kill, no individual player gains by unilaterally disarming. The problem is not that peace is unstable once achieved. The problem is transitioning from one equilibrium to the other.

This is the equilibrium selection problem, and Thomas Schelling (1960) identified the solution: a focal point — a signal that is independently recognisable by all players, enabling coordinated simultaneous action without prior negotiation.

Previous peace efforts have failed as focal points because they lack universality:

Bilateral treaties coordinate two parties but not the system. Conditional disarmament ("we stop if you stop first") produces infinite regress — nobody stops first. Imposed peace through superior force is itself a killing equilibrium. International institutions (the UN) lack enforcement mechanisms and are themselves captured by great-power competition.

The letter proposes that the concept of divine return — present in every major religious tradition — provides the first historically available universal focal point for coordinated cessation of killing. The mechanism works as follows:

1. Every major tradition already contains the vision of peace-after-return. The cultural infrastructure for recognising the signal already exists in the memory of every tradition. No new content needs to be introduced.

2. The letter reverses the causation — from "God returns and then peace follows" to "peace is the condition for God's return." This transforms a passive expectation (wait for God) into an active coordination signal (act, and God responds).

3. The coordination payoff (divine return) is perceived as infinite in every tradition that holds it. An infinite expected payoff justifies individual risk-taking (stopping killing before it is individually safe to do so), solving the first-mover problem that defeats conventional coordination mechanisms.

4. The signal is self-reinforcing: each additional player who stops killing increases the expected probability of the condition being met, which increases the expected payoff for all remaining players, which increases the incentive to stop. This is a positive tipping dynamic — once the cascade begins, it accelerates.

5. The signal is robust to theological disagreement. Players do not need to agree on who God is, what form the return takes, or what happens after. They need only recognise the shared structure: we all hold a vision of peace-after-return, and the letter proposes that peace is the condition rather than the consequence. Agreement on structure is sufficient; agreement on content is not required.

Schelling's key insight was that focal points do not need to be objectively optimal or even true. They need to be salient — independently recognisable by all parties. "Meet at Grand Central at noon" works not because Grand Central is the best location but because both parties can identify it as the obvious choice. "Stop killing because God returns when you do" works the same way: it activates a structure already present in every player's cultural memory.

Hypothesis 5: In experimental coordination games with multiple equilibria, introducing a focal-point signal that references shared cultural memory (e.g. a common narrative of reward-for-cooperation present in all players' backgrounds) will increase coordination on the cooperative equilibrium significantly more than focal-point signals without shared cultural resonance.

This is testable in laboratory settings using modified public goods games with culturally diverse participant pools.


VI. THE PARASITIC REPLICATOR

The letter claims that the killing instruction is embedded within religious transmission systems in a way that makes it inseparable from the valuable content those systems carry. In memetic terms, this is a parasitic replicator — a cultural unit that persists not because it benefits its host but because it has attached itself to a carrier that the host cannot discard.

Dawkins (1976) proposed that cultural units — memes — replicate through imitation and transmission, subject to variation and selection analogous to genetic evolution. A successful meme requires fidelity (accurate copying), fecundity (wide distribution), and longevity (persistent copies). Sacred texts maximise all three: they are copied verbatim (often considered the literal word of God), taught to every child in the community, and declared eternal and uneditable.

The killing instruction embedded in a sacred text is, from a memetic perspective, among the most successful replicators in human culture. It has persisted for millennia, across billions of hosts, with near-perfect fidelity, because it is integrated into a carrier (the sacred text) that the host community will defend with its life.

This structure is analogous to viral integration in molecular biology. A retrovirus inserts its genome into a host gene essential for survival. The integrated viral sequence is then replicated every time the host cell divides — not because the virus benefits the cell, but because the cell cannot replicate its essential gene without also replicating the virus. Removing the viral sequence risks destroying the host gene.

The letter's "seam" is the conceptual equivalent of identifying the integration site — the precise location where the parasitic sequence (the killing instruction) meets the host sequence (the valuable religious content). The children's question ("does anyone have to die for this to be true?") is a diagnostic assay: it identifies which passages contain integrated parasitic material and which do not.

Gene therapy for integrated viruses does not destroy the host gene. It excises the viral sequence while preserving the host sequence. The letter proposes the cultural equivalent: excising the killing instruction while preserving the religious content — the prayers, the music, the ethics, the community, the meaning. This requires precision. It requires the seam to be visible. And it requires the host community to trust that the excision will not destroy what they value.

Hypothesis 6: Communities that explicitly distinguish between sacred content that does and does not require violence (i.e. communities that have made the "seam" visible through theological education) will show lower rates of religiously-motivated violence support than communities that treat their sacred text as an undifferentiated whole.

Versions of this have been studied in the context of scriptural literalism and support for violence. The results are consistent with the hypothesis.


VII. THE LIMITS OF THE SCIENTIFIC FRAMING

Science can validate the mechanism. It cannot supply the motive.

Every claim in the letter has a scientific correlate: the installation is developmental psychology; the dehumanisation is neuroscience; the contact antidote is social psychology; the uniformity is cultural evolution; the coordination is game theory; the parasitic integration is memetics. The evidence base is substantial. The hypotheses are testable. Several have already been tested and supported.

But the scientific framing cannot answer the question the letter ultimately asks: should we stop killing? Science describes what is. The letter asks what should be. Science can demonstrate that the blade is installed, that it operates through dehumanisation, that it can be reversed through individuation and contact, and that coordinated cessation of violence is game-theoretically possible. Science cannot tell us whether the payoff the letter promises — divine return — is real.

This is not a deficiency. It is a boundary condition. The letter and the scientific companion operate in different registers, addressing different audiences, providing different kinds of evidence. The letter provides the why. This essay provides the how. Together they argue: the why is compelling, and the how is feasible.

Whether the why is true is a question that, by the letter's own logic, can only be answered by meeting the condition.

Stop killing, and see what happens.
