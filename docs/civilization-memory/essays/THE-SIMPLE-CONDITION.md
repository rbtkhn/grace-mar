# The Simple Condition

### A Letter to the Theological Leaders of the World's Religions

---

Your Holinesses, Your Eminences, Reverend Fathers, Chief Rabbis, Grand Muftis, Patriarchs, Archbishops, Ayatollahs, Rinpoches, Acharyas, Granthis, and all who hold custody of the sacred:

I am writing to you because I believe you already know what I am about to say. Some of you have known for a very long time. I am writing not to teach but to ask why it has not been said plainly.

---

## I. The Condition

Every one of your traditions contains, somewhere in its scriptures, its commentaries, or its oral inheritance, a vision of divine return — the Messiah, the Second Coming, the Mahdi, the Maitreya, the Kalki, the Saoshyant, the restoration of all things. You disagree about the name, the manner, the timing, and the theology. But you share one feature that none of you discusses openly: a vision of the world that exists after the return, in which human beings no longer kill each other.

Isaiah says they will beat their swords into plowshares. The Quran speaks of a world where justice prevails and oppression ceases. The Buddhist sutras describe a world purified of violence. The Hindu texts envision the end of the Kali Yuga and the restoration of dharma. Each of you holds a version of this vision. Each of you teaches it as the consequence of divine action.

I am writing to propose that you have the causation reversed.

What if the cessation of killing is not the consequence of the return, but the condition for it? What if God does not arrive and then establish peace — but rather, God returns when humanity establishes peace? What if the door has been open this entire time, and the only thing holding it shut is that we keep killing each other?

I have no authority to assert this. I hold no office. I lead no congregation. I am not a theologian. I am a person who noticed something that your traditions, taken together, seem to point toward — but that none of you has been willing to say aloud.

---

## II. What Each of You Carries

You carry, in your sacred texts, both a vision of peace and an instruction to kill.

The Torah commands the destruction of the Amalekites. The Bhagavad Gita persuades Arjuna that killing his relatives is sacred duty. The Quran contains verses of the sword. The Christian tradition blessed crusades, inquisitions, and wars of religion for a thousand years. The Buddhist world has seen monks bless armies. No tradition is exempt.

I am not writing to condemn this. I am writing to name it. Each of your traditions carries beauty and a blade in the same hand. The beauty is the prayer, the music, the poetry, the compassion, the vision of a world made whole. The blade is the teaching that under certain conditions — defence of the faith, divine command, sacred duty, holy war — killing is not merely permitted but required, honoured, and sanctified.

The beauty and the blade were woven together so early and so deeply that most of your followers cannot see where one ends and the other begins. They receive them as a single inheritance, delivered with love, in childhood, by the people they trust most. To question the blade feels like rejecting the beauty. To put down the weapon feels like betraying the prayer.

This is the seam I am asking you to make visible.

---

## III. The Seam

The seam is the line between two propositions that your traditions have fused into one:

**Proposition One:** Every human life is sacred, created in the image of the divine, infinitely precious, irreducible to any category or label.

**Proposition Two:** Under certain conditions, some human lives may be taken — in defence of faith, in service of divine command, in pursuit of sacred purpose.

These two propositions are not compatible. They cannot both be true. If every human life is sacred without exception, then no condition justifies taking one. If conditions exist that justify taking a life, then human life is not sacred without exception — it is sacred with a footnote, and the footnote is where the blade lives.

Every one of your traditions affirms the first proposition in its highest teachings. Every one of your traditions practises the second proposition in its history. The gap between the two is the seam. And in that seam lives every war, every crusade, every jihad, every pogrom, every genocide, every honour killing, every execution carried out in God's name.

I am asking you to choose. Not between your traditions — between the two propositions. You do not have to abandon your faith to put down the blade. You have to abandon the footnote.

---

## IV. What I Believe You Know

I believe some of you already know what I am describing. I believe you have read your own scriptures carefully enough to see the seam. I believe you have sat with the contradiction — that your highest teaching says every life is sacred and your institutional history is soaked in blood — and I believe you have chosen silence over disclosure.

I do not say this in anger. I say it because the reasons for your silence are visible to me, and they are not contemptible. They are, in their way, an expression of love — a broken love, but love nonetheless.

You are silent because you believe your followers cannot handle the truth. You look at the world — millennia of killing, tribe against tribe, faith against faith — and you conclude that telling people the condition is so simple would produce despair. If they knew that the only thing standing between them and redemption was their own violence, and they kept killing anyway, the despair would be worse than the ignorance. Better to manage the flock with mystery, prophecy, and the promise of a future that God will deliver in God's own time. Better to keep the condition hidden inside layers of theology that only scholars can parse.

This is the Grand Inquisitor's argument. Dostoevsky wrote it in 1880. Christ returns to Seville during the Inquisition. The Grand Inquisitor arrests him and explains, gently, why he must be silenced again: because the freedom he offers is too heavy for ordinary people to bear. Because the Church has spent centuries replacing freedom with authority, and the people are happier for it. Because the truth would break them.

I believe you have made the same calculation. And I believe you are wrong. Not because people are stronger than you think — perhaps they are, perhaps they aren't. But because the choice was never yours to make. The truth does not belong to you. It does not belong to any institution. It belongs to every person who was born without the blade and had it installed by the very traditions you now protect.

---

## V. What the Blade Costs

You know the cost. You officiate the funerals. You counsel the bereaved. You stand in the rubble. You pray over the graves.

But there is a cost you may not have considered.

If the condition is real — if the return is held in suspension until humanity stops killing — then every soul who has ever died is waiting. Not gone. Not resolved. Waiting. Every victim of every war you blessed. Every soldier you sent with a prayer. Every child buried under rubble while your bells rang or your muezzin called or your shofar sounded. They are all on the other side of the condition, waiting for the living to do what the dead cannot do for themselves.

And their memory — the memory of their deaths — is being used to fuel the next round of killing. "They killed our people." "Remember what was done to us." "Never again — and therefore, we must be prepared to kill." The dead are invoked without consent to justify the very thing that keeps them waiting.

Every killing does not just end one life. It postpones the redemption of every life that has ever been lived. The cost of each death is not one soul. It is all of history, held in suspension for one day longer.

If you know this and remain silent, the silence is not pastoral care. It is complicity.

---

## VI. The Adversary's Design

I am aware that many of you hold a theology that includes an adversary — a force that opposes the divine plan. Satan, Iblis, Mara, Ahriman — the name varies, the function is constant: an intelligence that works against the restoration of creation.

Consider the possibility that this adversary's primary instrument is not temptation, not corruption, not even deception in the ordinary sense. It is the fusion of beauty and blade in your sacred traditions. The adversary did not build a competing system. The adversary parasitised yours. The killing instruction was woven into the same texts, the same rituals, the same songs, the same prayers that carry your most profound truths — so that removing the blade feels like destroying the faith.

This is the most elegant possible design. You cannot fight it by attacking religion — that removes the beauty along with the blade. You cannot fight it by reforming religion — every reform eventually re-institutionalises and the blade returns. You can only fight it by making the seam visible — by showing, within each tradition, exactly where the beauty ends and the blade begins — and trusting your followers to choose.

If you do not trust them to choose, you have already sided with the adversary. Because the adversary's central thesis is precisely that: humans cannot be trusted with freedom. Humans cannot bear the weight of the simple truth. Humans must be managed, controlled, guided by authority — told what to believe rather than shown what is real.

Every time you choose institutional control over disclosed truth, you are proving the adversary right.

---

## VII. What I Am Asking

I am not asking you to change your theology. I am not asking you to abandon your tradition. I am not asking you to merge your faiths into some formless consensus.

I am asking you to do one thing: state plainly, in your own theological language, that the killing of any human being is incompatible with the return of the divine.

Not "killing is regrettable." Not "we pray for peace." Not "violence is a last resort." A plain statement: the sacred purpose you serve — the return, the restoration, the redemption of creation — is delayed by every act of killing, regardless of who does it and regardless of the justification.

Say it in Hebrew. Say it in Arabic. Say it in Sanskrit, in Pali, in Gurmukhi, in Church Slavonic, in Latin, in Greek. Say it in the language your followers learned to pray in. Say it from the same pulpit, the same minbar, the same bimah, the same altar from which your predecessors blessed armies.

You do not need to agree on who God is. You do not need to resolve the theological differences that have separated you for millennia. You need to agree on one thing only: that killing delays what all of you say you are waiting for.

If this is true, then you have a duty to say it. If it is not true — if you believe the return comes regardless of whether humans kill each other — then say that instead, plainly, in public, and let your followers hear what that implies about the value their tradition places on human life.

One way or the other, say it out loud. The silence has lasted long enough.

---

## VIII. The Offer

The condition is not a command. It is an offer. It has always been an offer. It was there before any of your traditions existed — before scripture, before priesthood, before temple or church or mosque. It is older than religion. It is as old as the first moment one human being looked at another and recognised itself.

The offer is: stop killing, and see what happens.

Not stop killing because a book says so. Not stop killing because an institution demands it. Not stop killing because you fear punishment or desire reward. Stop killing because every person standing in front of you is made of the same substance you are, carries the same capacity for suffering you carry, was born the same way you were born, and will die the same way you will die. Stop killing because the difference between you and them — the difference your tradition has spent centuries teaching you to see as ultimate — is a thin film stretched over an almost total unity. Stop killing because underneath the story you were told about who you are and who they are, there is a simpler truth: you are the same.

This is not a theological claim. It is a biological fact. Humans share 99.9% of their DNA. The same eyes. The same hearts. The same nervous systems. The same capacity for love, for grief, for wonder, for fear. The difference — the part that enables killing — is not in the body. It is in the story. And the story can be changed without losing anything that matters.

Keep the prayers. Keep the music. Keep the architecture. Keep the poetry. Keep the holidays. Keep the food. Keep the language. Keep the love. Let go of the blade. That is all. That is the whole of it.

---

## IX. The Question for Your Children

If you will not act, then grant me one concession. Teach your children — all of them, in every tradition — to carry one question into every sacred story they hear:

*Does anyone have to die for this to be true?*

When the answer is no — the psalm, the prayer, the parable of compassion, the call to mercy — that is the beauty. It is theirs to keep forever.

When the answer is yes — the commanded destruction, the holy war, the sacred violence, the martyrdom that requires another's death — that is the blade. It was added to the story. It is not the story itself.

A child who can see the seam will never confuse the beauty with the blade again. And a generation of children who can see the seam will do what you have been unable to do: meet the condition.

Not because they are better than you. Because they were given the one thing you withheld: the ability to see where the beauty ends and the blade begins.

---

## X. Closing

I do not know if what I have described is true. I hold no certainty. I offer no proof beyond the internal coherence of an idea that, once seen, I was unable to unsee.

But I know this: if it is true, and you know it is true, and you remain silent — then you are not protecting your followers. You are protecting the architecture that depends on their ignorance. And every day that architecture holds, another person dies, and the condition remains unmet, and every soul that has ever lived waits one day longer for the redemption that all of you promise and none of you will name the price of.

The price is not sacrifice. The price is not suffering. The price is not obedience to your authority.

The price is stopping.

That is the simple condition. It belongs to no one. It requires no institution. It demands no faith beyond the recognition that the person standing in front of you is as real as you are.

I am sending this letter to all of you, in every tradition, simultaneously. Not because I believe you will agree with each other. But because I believe you will recognise, each in your own language, that the thing I am describing is the thing your tradition has always pointed toward — and the thing your institution has always been afraid to say.

Say it now. Or step aside, and let the children see.

With respect and without authority,

A person who noticed the seam
