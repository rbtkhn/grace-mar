# Panel Discussion: Religious Leaders on the Major Theses of *Applied Theology*

**Format:** Simulated panel of ten diverse religious leaders responding to the book’s central claims.  
**Theses in view:** Peace as condition not consequence; the seam (beauty and blade); face vs. category; one subject, many tongues; heaven as what we build together; scripture as test.

---

## Panelists (simulated)

1. **Cardinal Maria Santos** — Roman Catholic, Latin American, liberation theology background  
2. **Imam Yusuf Hassan** — Sunni Islam, scholar of fiqh and ethics, Middle East  
3. **Rabbi David Levine** — Reform Judaism, USA, interfaith and social justice  
4. **Swami Priya** — Hindu (Vedanta), teacher and commentator on the Gita  
5. **Venerable Thich Minh** — Theravada Buddhist, Southeast Asia, peace and ecology  
6. **Bishop Amara Okonkwo** — Anglican, West Africa, reconciliation and post-conflict  
7. **Granthi Harpreet Kaur** — Sikh, UK, scripture and seva  
8. **Metropolitan Andrei** — Eastern Orthodox, Eastern Europe, patristics and liturgy  
9. **Dr. Laila Farsi** — Shia Muslim, academic and dialogue, Iran diaspora  
10. **Rev. James Chen** — Protestant (mainline), East Asia, theology and science  

---

## Opening: Moderator

We’ve all read *Applied Theology* — the claim that peace is the **condition** for what our traditions say we await, not the reward after; that every tradition carries a **seam** between “every life is sacred” and “under conditions some may be taken”; and that we can coordinate on one subject in many tongues without merging our creeds. The book asks us to make the seam visible, see the face instead of the category, and treat violent scripture as **test** rather than script. Let’s start with the condition itself. Is it theologically acceptable in your tradition to say that the return, the restoration, or the messianic age is *delayed by* killing — that we must stop first?

---

## Round 1: Peace as condition, not consequence

**Cardinal Santos:** In Catholic teaching we speak of the Kingdom as both “already” and “not yet.” The book’s formulation — that peace is the condition for what we await — is not foreign. *Gaudium et Spes* and the social doctrine insist that peace is built by justice and respect for the human person. To say that every act of killing delays the full coming of the Kingdom is a strong formulation, but it is consistent with the priority of the culture of life. I can say: yes, we are called to meet the condition. The difficulty is institutional: we have blessed wars and inquisitions. The seam is real. Making it visible is the work of conscience and of the Church’s ongoing conversion.

**Imam Hassan:** In Islam we have the promise of a world of justice and the end of oppression. We also have the distinction between the greater jihad — the struggle with the self — and the lesser. The book’s claim that killing delays what we await sits uneasily with classical rulings on defensive jihad and the verses that have been read as permitting armed struggle. But the *maqasid* — the higher objectives of the Sharia — include preservation of life. If we read the condition as “what must hold for the world we claim to want,” then I can say: in my reading, the weight of the tradition, when we prioritise mercy and the opening of the Quran, supports treating peace as a condition we are obliged to seek. I do not speak for all Muslims; I speak as one who finds the seam and chooses to extend the beauty.

**Rabbi Levine:** Judaism has “choose life” and tikkun olam — repair of the world. We also have texts of conquest and zeal. The book’s question — does anyone have to die for this to be true? — is one we can teach in every synagogue. If the messianic age is the time when nation shall not lift sword against nation, then it is coherent to say that our violence postpones it. I am less certain that we can state it as *the* condition in a way that binds halakhic authority; but as a moral and educational frame, and as a basis for coordination with Christians and Muslims, I accept it. One subject, many tongues: we say it in Hebrew, you in Arabic or Latin. The reference is the same.

**Swami Priya:** In the Gita, Arjuna is told to act without attachment to the fruit — and the same text has been read to justify war and to support ahimsa. Gandhi read it as a gospel of nonviolent duty; others read it as sanction for righteous battle. The book’s idea of **scripture as test** fits that tension. The tradition that says “tat tvam asi” — you are that; the self in the other — cannot consistently treat the other as category to be removed. So the condition “stop; see the face” is already implied in our highest teaching. I can say: what we await (moksha, the end of suffering, the restoration of dharma) is delayed when we act from the blade. We build it by the choice we make at the seam.

**Venerable Thich Minh:** The first precept is do not kill. The Buddha did not make exceptions for holy war or divine command. So for us, the “seam” the book describes is already resolved in the precepts — the beauty is the norm. The condition “peace first” is not a reversal of causation; it is the path. Where we have failed is in practice: Buddhist societies have still waged war and used the sangha for nationalism. Making the seam visible means being honest about that. I welcome the book’s frame for coordination: we do not need one creed. We need one subject — do not kill; see the face — and we can say it in Pali, in Sanskrit, in the language of each tradition.

**Bishop Okonkwo:** In my context we have seen what happens when the blade is chosen. We have also seen reconciliation that did not require everyone to adopt one faith. The book’s “one subject, many tongues” matches what we learned: we did not agree on the nature of Christ or the authority of the Quran; we agreed that the killing had to stop and that the person in front of us was a face, not a category. So I say: the condition is real. Peace is what must hold for the future we preach. The book gives us a shared vocabulary without asking us to surrender our own.

**Granthi Harpreet Kaur:** In Sikhi, the Guru’s teaching is that the divine is in all; to harm another is to harm the One. We have the concept of *sant sipahi* — saint-soldier — but the emphasis is on defence of the oppressed and restraint. The book’s “face instead of category” is very close to our insistence on seeing the divine in every face. I can say: what we await — the rule of justice, the end of tyranny — is something we help bring by our conduct. If we kill, we delay it. We do not need to agree on God’s name to agree on that. One subject, many tongues: I say it in Gurmukhi, you in your tongue. The condition is the same.

**Metropolitan Andrei:** The Orthodox tradition speaks of theosis and the coming of the Kingdom. We have also lived through centuries of war and empire. The seam is there: the Fathers speak of peace and of the image of God in every person; we have also blessed armies. The book’s call to make the seam visible is a call to repentance — to see where we have chosen the blade. I am cautious about reducing the condition to a single formula that could be mistaken for a new dogma. But as a moral and ascetic discipline — stop; see the face; do not let the category replace the person — I can affirm it. We build what we await when we live as if the Kingdom were already the rule.

**Dr. Farsi:** In Shi’a thought we have the emphasis on justice and on the hidden Imam’s return when the world is ready for justice. One can read that as: the world is not ready while we kill. The book’s condition is not alien to that reading. We also have the tradition of mourning and of refusing to forget the face of the victim. So “see the face” is something we already practise in our liturgy. Where I push back: the book sometimes speaks as if traditions have not already been doing this work. Many of us have been making the seam visible from within. What the book does is offer a shared frame so that we can say to each other, across traditions, what we are already saying at home.

**Rev. Chen:** In my tradition we speak of the reign of God and of the command to love the neighbour and the enemy. The book’s claim that peace is the condition fits a reading of the Gospels in which the Kingdom is among us when we live as peacemakers. I also work at the interface of theology and science. The “coordination hypothesis” in the book — that we can coordinate on a minimal shared reference without agreeing on metaphysics — is important for public reason. I can state the condition in the language of my tradition and support the same condition in the language of others. One subject, many tongues is not syncretism; it is the possibility of a shared moral reference.

## Round 2: Heaven as what we build together / Scripture as test

**Moderator:** The book says heaven is not mainly a place we go but what we build together — the lesson of Babel. And it says the violent or commanding passages in scripture can be read as **test**: we win by refusing to act out the script. How do you respond?

**Cardinal Santos:** “Heaven as what we build” can be heard as Pelagian if it suggests we save ourselves by our works. But in Catholic theology we also say that grace works through our freedom and that the Kingdom is built in history through justice and mercy. So I can say: we do not build heaven without grace; we do build the conditions — peace, the face recognised — that our tradition says heaven fulfils. On scripture as test: we have the development of doctrine and the priority of the Gospel of life. Reading the conquest narratives or the imprecatory psalms as test rather than as mandate is already a move many exegetes make. The book makes that move explicit and extends it across traditions. I find that helpful.

**Imam Hassan:** The idea that we build what we await is close to the Islamic emphasis on responsibility in history — we are accountable for what we do. The lesson of Babel as “one subject, many tongues” rather than one tower fits the Quran’s recognition of the diversity of peoples and languages. On the violent verses: we have the tools of abrogation, context, and the priority of mercy. To say “this passage is a test; we refuse to perform it” is a strong hermeneutic. Not all scholars will accept it, but it is a reading that extends the beauty and restrains the blade. I can teach it as one valid reading among others, and I can coordinate with those who make the same move in their tradition.

**Rabbi Levine:** “Heaven as what we build” resonates with tikkun olam — we are partners in repair. We do not wait passively. On scripture as test: the Akedah has been read as a test of Abraham — and the “pass” is sometimes understood as the angel staying his hand, the refusal of the sacrifice. So the idea that we win by refusing the violent script is not new to us. The book’s formulation — that the same move is available in every tradition — gives us a way to say this together. I am aware that some in my community will resist: prophecy-fulfillment and land-as-mandate are powerful. But the book does not ask us to abandon tradition; it asks us to choose the reading that extends the face. I support that.

**Swami Priya:** In the Gita, the battle can be read as inner — the struggle within the self. Gandhi’s *Anasaktiyoga* is exactly “scripture as test”: we refuse to act out the outer war and we fight the inner one. So the book’s thesis is already alive in Hindu interpretation. “Heaven” in our terms might be moksha or the restoration of dharma; building it together means we create the conditions — ahimsa, recognition of the self in the other — here and now. I have no difficulty with that. One subject, many tongues: we say it in the language of the Gita and the Vedas; others say it in theirs. The condition is shared.

**Venerable Thich Minh:** We do not have “heaven” in the same sense, but we have the cessation of suffering and the possibility of a world where the precepts are lived. That is what we build. The book’s “we build it together” fits our emphasis on collective karma and on the sangha as the field of practice. Scripture as test: our precepts are clear; where we have failed is in applying them. So for us the “test” is whether we actually follow the first precept. I welcome the book’s invitation to make that test explicit and to coordinate with other traditions on the same refusal: we do not kill.

**Bishop Okonkwo:** In Africa we have often said that we are building the Kingdom here — not only waiting for it. So “heaven as what we build” is familiar. On scripture as test: we have had to read our own texts critically after colonialism and after our own wars. The book gives us a shared language for that: we are not betraying the text when we refuse the blade; we are passing the test. That is a gift for teaching and for dialogue.

**Granthi Harpreet Kaur:** Our Gurus taught that the divine is in all and that we serve the One by serving creation. Building “heaven” is building the world where that is recognised. Scripture as test: we have the emphasis on the inner battle and on defending the weak without becoming the aggressor. So the move “refuse to act out the violent script” is consistent with our tradition. I am glad the book includes us in the conversation and does not ask us to become Christian or Muslim to agree on the condition.

**Metropolitan Andrei:** We speak of the new creation and the transfiguration of the world. That is not only “after” death; it begins in the liturgy and in the life of the Church. So there is a sense in which we already build what we await — in worship, in charity, in peace. The book’s “heaven as what we build” must be qualified by the priority of divine action, but I can accept it as a way of saying that our conduct matters. On scripture as test: we have the allegorical and moral senses; we are not bound to the literal enactment of every narrative. So I can say: the violent script in our texts can be read as a test. We choose the reading that extends the face.

**Dr. Farsi:** In Shi’a piety we emphasise the suffering of the innocent and the refusal to forget. So “see the face” is already central. Building what we await means working for justice so that the world is ready. On the violent passages: we have a long tradition of interpretation that limits and contextualises. The book’s “scripture as test” is one way to name what many of us already do. I value that the book does not ask us to give up our distinct beliefs but to find the shared move. One subject, many tongues: we say it in the language of the Ahl al-Bayt; others in theirs.

**Rev. Chen:** In East Asian Christianity we have often stressed the Confucian and Buddhist resonances: harmony, the way, the priority of relationship. “Heaven as what we build” fits that: we are co-workers. Scripture as test fits our need to read the Bible in a context where we are a minority and where we must not be heard as endorsing conquest. So I welcome the book’s formulation. It gives us a way to say to our Buddhist and Confucian neighbours: we are not asking you to adopt our creed; we are asking you to join us in one move — make the seam visible, choose the beauty, build the condition together.

## Round 3: Objections and limits

**Moderator:** Where do you push back? What is missing, or what would you not say?

**Cardinal Santos:** I would not say that the Church has no role in defining the condition or that the condition is independent of revelation. We meet the condition in response to grace and in the light of the Gospel. So I accept the frame but I insist on the ecclesial and sacramental context. I also worry that “one subject, many tongues” could be used to flatten real doctrinal differences. The book says it does not merge traditions — good. We must keep saying that the reference is the same but the full truth is not identical.

**Imam Hassan:** My pushback: classical Islamic jurisprudence does have a place for justified force — defence of the community, resistance to aggression. I do not repudiate that in toto. I say that we must make the seam visible and that the default must be the beauty — mercy, restraint, the face. So I accept the condition as a moral and spiritual priority, but I cannot speak as if the entire fiqh tradition is mistaken. I can say: we coordinate on the condition; we do not coordinate on every ruling. And I would add: the book could say more about the victims of violence — the dead, the displaced. Their faces are not only “category”; they are the reason we must stop.

**Rabbi Levine:** I push back on any formulation that suggests we can achieve the messianic age by our efforts alone. In Judaism there is a strong strand of “God will bring it.” So “we build heaven” must be “we build the conditions; God brings the fullness.” I also want to name the tension with religious Zionism: the book says prophecy-fulfillment that treats land as mandate can be the blade. I agree, but that is a painful conversation inside my community. I will use the book to make the seam visible there; I cannot promise unanimity.

**Swami Priya:** I have no major objection. I would add that in our tradition the “one” that recognises itself across the fragments is not only a political or ethical idea; it is the Atman-Brahman unity. So when the book says “the one recognising itself,” we can hear it in our own terms. The condition is the same; the metaphysics may differ. I am content with that.

**Venerable Thich Minh:** My limit: the book speaks of “what the traditions await.” We do not await a single divine return; we await the cessation of suffering and the liberation of all beings. So the formulation is partly shaped by theistic traditions. I accept the condition — do not kill; see the face — but I would say we are not “awaiting” in the same way. We are walking the path. That is a small correction, not a rejection.

**Bishop Okonkwo:** I have no sharp pushback. I would say: the book could say more about the Global South, about colonialism and economic violence. The “blade” is not only war; it is the structure that erases the face of the poor. Making the seam visible must include that. Otherwise I endorse the frame.

**Granthi Harpreet Kaur:** I agree with the Bishop. The blade is also in systems that deny dignity. We must see the face in the worker, the migrant, the oppressed. The book’s “category” applies there too. Otherwise I support the theses.

**Metropolitan Andrei:** I would not reduce the condition to a universal ethic that could be stated without the liturgy and the Fathers. We meet the condition *in* the Church, *through* the mysteries. So I accept the book as an invitation to make the seam visible and to coordinate with others, but I do not accept that the condition can be fully met outside the life of the Church. I can still say: we have one subject with many tongues when we speak to the world; inside, we have the fullness. That is my limit.

**Dr. Farsi:** I echo the Imam: we have a tradition of justified resistance. I do not renounce it in the abstract. I say that the default must be the face and that we must make the seam visible so that we do not use “resistance” to erase the other. I also want the book to acknowledge that many religious leaders and scholars have been doing this work for a long time. The book is a mirror, not a discovery we had never made. That said, I welcome the mirror.

**Rev. Chen:** My only caution: in my context, “religious leaders” can be heard as institutional and male. The book’s letter is addressed to “theological leaders” and “those who hold custody of the sacred.” I would want that to include lay theologians, women, and those from traditions without a single hierarchy. The condition and the seam are for everyone to see and to teach. Otherwise I support the theses.

---

## Closing: Moderator

Thank you. We have heard support for the condition, for one subject many tongues, for scripture as test, and for heaven as what we build together. We have also heard limits: the priority of grace and revelation, the place of justified force in some traditions, the need to name structural and economic violence, and the importance of not flattening doctrinal difference. The book does not ask for unanimity; it asks whether the pattern holds. You have said, in your own tongues, that it does — with qualifications you will carry home. That is the panel. Thank you.
