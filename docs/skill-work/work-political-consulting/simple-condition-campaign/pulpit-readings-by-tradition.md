# Pulpit Readings by Tradition — Top 10 US Denominations and Religions

Tradition-specific versions of the [universal pulpit reading](pulpit-reading-universal.md). Same core message (sacredness of life, seam, see the face, peace as condition, demand the vote); vocabulary, scripture touchstones, and closing adapted per tradition. **Human approval required** before distribution. ~90 seconds each.

**Order:** By approximate US adherence (Pew / ARDA style). 1–9 Christian denominations; 10 Judaism. Islam and other traditions can be added from the same template.

---

## 1. Catholic

Our faith teaches that every person is made in the image and likeness of God. That the stranger is Christ in disguise. That peace is not a luxury but the condition to which the Gospel calls us — the peace that the world cannot give.

Yet in our tradition too there is a seam: teachings and history that have been used to justify violence — crusade, inquisition, war in the name of Christ. Beauty and a blade, woven so tightly that to question one can feel like betraying the other. The Church herself has said: we must make the seam visible. We can hold the beauty — the Mass, the saints, the option for the poor — and we can put down the blade. We do not have to abandon our faith to refuse to bless killing.

Right now, people are dying in a war that most of us did not choose. We are told they are enemies, targets, faceless. But they have names. They have families. They have faces. Every life is imago Dei. See the face. Do not reduce a human being to a category.

There is a door. It has been open all along. Peace is not the reward after we win. Peace is the condition. We are the ones holding the door shut — or we are the ones who can let it open.

I ask you to do two things. First: see the face. When you hear about this conflict, remember that on every side there are people who did not choose to be targets. Second: if you live in a country whose leaders can vote on war, demand the vote. Let your representatives be on record. The vote is ours.

The Lord does not need more killing. He needs us to stop. To see the face again. To demand that those who speak in our name do not make war without answering to us.

May we choose the beauty. May we put down the blade. And may we act before one more soul is asked to wait.

Amen.

## 10. Judaism (cross‑denominational)

Our tradition teaches that every human being is created in the image of God — b’tzelem Elohim. That we are to love the stranger, for we were strangers in the land of Egypt. That pikuach nefesh, saving a life, overrides almost every other law. That the prophets dreamed of a day when nations would beat their swords into plowshares.

Yet in our heritage too there is a seam: texts and history that have been used to justify violence — in the name of survival, land, or faith. Beauty and a blade, woven together so tightly that to question one can feel like betraying the other. I am not here to condemn our tradition. I am here to name the seam. We can hold the beauty — the Torah, the Sabbath, the vision of shalom — and we can put down the blade. We do not have to abandon our faith to refuse to bless killing.

Right now, people are dying in a war that most of us did not choose. We are told they are enemies, targets, faceless. But they have names. They have families. They have faces. Every life is b’tzelem Elohim. See the face. Do not reduce a human being to a category.

There is a door. It has been open all along. Peace is not the reward we get after we win. Peace is the condition. We are the ones holding the door shut — or we are the ones who can let it open.

I ask you to do two things. First: see the face. When you hear about this conflict, remember that on every side there are people who did not choose to be targets. Second: if you live in a country whose leaders can vote on war, demand the vote. Let your representatives be on record. The vote is ours.

The Holy One, blessed be He, does not need more killing. He needs us to stop. To see the face again. To demand that those who speak in our name do not make war without answering to us.

May we choose the beauty. May we put down the blade. And may we act before one more soul is asked to wait.

Ken yihi ratzon. So may it be.

## Notes for clergy

- **Length:** Each reading ~260–300 words; about 90 seconds at a steady pace.
- **Adapt further:** Localize "this war" or "this conflict" if your context requires (e.g. "in the conflicts of our time"). Add your own CTA (e.g. "Contact your representative this week"; "Sign the letter").
- **Islam / other traditions:** Same structure can be used for Sunni, Shi'a, Buddhist, Hindu, or other communities; substitute that tradition’s name for the sacred, key phrases (e.g. mercy, justice, one umma), and closing (e.g. Inshallah).
