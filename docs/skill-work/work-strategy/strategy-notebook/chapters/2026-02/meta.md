## Geopolitical arc

February 2026 is dominated by the **transition from coercive diplomacy to open U.S.–Israel combat operations against Iran**. Reuters and other wires tracked a **large U.S. military buildup** in and around the Gulf while talks stalled on enrichment caps, sanctions relief, and missile-related proposals [1]. For the strategy notebook, that stall is not merely “news noise”: it is the month where **the bargaining space** (what could still be traded without war) and **the escalation path** (what force would buy) became **simultaneously visible** to operators weaving **`pape`** and **`davis`** without merging their tiers. By **19 February**, reporting described President Trump warning Tehran to reach a “meaningful” nuclear deal within a **10–15 day** window or face severe consequences, alongside Russian warnings about escalation risk as deployments continued [2]. The same news cycle carried reporting that the administration was **relaxing non-proliferation guardrails** in a proposed **civil nuclear cooperation framework with Saudi Arabia** — dropping enrichment restrictions and some inspection conditions that had previously constrained the deal’s politics [3], a development **`mearsheimer`**-style readers would map to **regional nuclear hedging** and **GCC alignment** under U.S. sponsorship.

**16 February** appears in the notebook’s **`ritter`** lane as a **long-form YouTube interview** on Iran policy and diplomacy (episode metadata dated Feb 16, 2026) — a **primary-ish ingest anchor** for the dissent register before kinetic operations [4].

The **28 February** inflection is the chapter’s hinge: open-source coverage described **U.S. and Israeli strikes** on Iranian targets, framed by U.S. leadership as **major combat operations** and tied to Iran’s nuclear and missile programs, with public calls for **regime change** in some U.S. messaging [5]. Iranian officials warned that U.S. bases could become **legitimate targets** if attacks proceeded [2]. For the strategy notebook, February therefore encodes **three simultaneous objects**: (a) **coercion timing** (deadlines vs negotiation text), (b) **alliance execution** (U.S.–Israel use of force), and (c) **regional nuclear proliferation politics** (Saudi deal structure) [3].

Energy and insurance markets begin to **price tail risk** in late February even before March’s Hormuz headlines; the notebook treats price levels cited in journalism as **hypothesis tier** until matched to exchange and customs releases in later verification passes.

**Negotiation archaeology (hypothesis tier):** Outlets published **timelines** of U.S.–Iran contacts after fighting began, summarizing earlier phases of talks and stalemates over missiles and enrichment [7]. The notebook does **not** treat those timelines as **primary** documents unless each row is pinned to a named text or official readout; they are **orientation aids** for **`parsi`** and **`freeman`**-style **process** reads when April weaves **Islamabad** and **Oman** into the same month as kinetic facts.
