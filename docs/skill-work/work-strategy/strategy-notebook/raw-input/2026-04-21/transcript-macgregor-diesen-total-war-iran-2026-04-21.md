# Douglas Macgregor × Glenn Diesen — No Peace / U.S. “total war” prep vs Iran? (operator transcript)

**Interview title (operator):** Douglas Macgregor: No Peace - U.S. Prepares for 'Total War' Against Iran

**Glenn Diesen:** Welcome back. Today is April 21st, 2026, and we are joined by none other than Colonel Douglas Macgregor, a decorated combat veteran, author, and former adviser to the US Secretary of Defense. Thank you very much for coming back on.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Sure.

**Glenn Diesen:** We seem to be in the final hours before the ceasefire expires. We were told there was going to be a meeting in Islamabad, but there's been uncertainty whether that's actually the case. It's difficult to see where we're going here. On one hand, we hear stories that the US has depleted a lot of its missiles and interceptors, so they would want to get out of this. On the other hand, it appears they're too far away for an actual peace deal. Is the best thing we can hope for an extension of the ceasefire? Will these negotiations take place, or are we going back to full-scale war?

**Douglas Macgregor:** I think the notion that we were going to hold talks again in Islamabad was always fiction. There was no evidence for any Iranian interest in doing so. The last talks were disappointing. The Iranians showed up with 70 people, large quantities of data, information, maps, and so forth, in the expectation that there was an intention to negotiate and come up with some sort of mutually agreeable solution. There wasn't.

When you have someone like Vice President Vance getting up during the meeting to go outside and take a call from Mr. Netanyahu, it suggests that these are not really negotiations. It also suggests that Mr. Netanyahu, not Mr. Trump, is in charge. For the purposes of deciding whether we will or will not fight, that's probably accurate.

President Trump was trying to calm the markets again. He's done this several times—said things that were patently false about the likelihood of the war ending sooner rather than later, and that the Iranians were desperate for talks when they weren't—conveying the illusion that we were somehow winning this conflict in the Persian Gulf. We aren't. As a result, I think there will be no talks.

The ceasefire expires at 3:00 a.m. tomorrow, Iranian time. The Iranians are preparing to be attacked again, and they're also preparing for the possibility that we will attack even sooner. At this point, we should just dismiss whatever comes out of the White House as effectively nonsense, largely designed to calm the public and manipulate the markets. But I think that's over now.

**Glenn Diesen:** This appears to be a common theme with Trump: to always declare victory no matter what happens. We saw this with Yemen and almost all instances—there's always a victory somehow, no matter what happens on the ground. How do you expect this war to be fought, though? If the news stories are correct, the amount of missiles used seems to be a very significant part of America's stockpile. To what extent is it reasonable to continue a war at this high intensity? Will the US try to do a low-intensity war? Will they focus on a ground invasion? It doesn't seem possible to sustain another 40 days of this kind of warfare.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Let me answer that question, but before doing so, let me provide a strategic overview from the vantage point of people on the outside looking in—the rest of the world watching what we are doing and what Iran is doing.

Increasingly, we're seen as this aspiring supranational authority that wants to impose its will on everyone everywhere. Iran has opted to stand up against this, and the world is very anxious to see what the outcome will be.

We have always had the ability—certainly since 1899—to project power. That was the whole purpose of President Teddy Roosevelt's Great White Fleet. We are not inherently defensive. We are a power that banks on the offense—the ability to attack beyond its normal limits in order to impose our power.

The Iranians are an inherently defensive power. They spend perhaps one or two percent of what we do on defense, and their investment has been in fundamentally defensive weapons. Contrary to unending Israeli propaganda and the propaganda by their agents that control our mainstream media, the Iranians can't really reach very far beyond their own borders. They are in a strategic defensive posture.

One side is operating 6,700 miles away from its logistical support base and manufacturing support; the other is operating essentially from within the boundaries of its own country. There is a home-court advantage. The Iranians have invested very heavily in a new form of warfare that has reduced us to a position very similar to what was happening on the Western Front in 1914 and 1915: if you try to move against Iran, you are identified, targeted, and attacked from anywhere from 500 to 1,000 miles beyond Iran's borders.

That makes it very difficult when you're dependent upon air and naval power. You have to move your support and sustain it thousands of miles. You have to periodically refuel and reload from a great distance. The Iranians don't have to deal with that, and most of their critical facilities and stores are underground.

What can we expect now? I think we have replenished our stocks in the theater and resupplied our naval and air platforms with required munitions. How long will they last? You would see something similar to what we did the last time, but with greater emphasis on an initial period of intensity. The first 48 to 96 hours will be more intense than previously—more sorties, more missiles, more bombings—concentrated on allegedly critical targets that, if sufficiently damaged, could influence the outcome.

This is an air and missile operation. Everything depends on the targets you select. You're looking for targets and portions of a targeting system that will give you a strategic advantage and convince your enemy to give up.

Since the end of the First World War, we've been trying this with massive bombing attacks, long-distance strikes, interdiction, and close air support. In World War II, we doubled down on it. Since 1945, the air power community in the United States has been trying to prove that air power could do everything.

Now you're going to see a far more intense opening salvo, designed—in President Trump's words—to be a "war operation." It's going to be so furious and so devastating that the Iranian forces and their citizenry won't recover. The objective now is to destroy the Iranian state. When you begin attacking bridges, road networks, railways, power plants, desalination plants, and oil infrastructure, that's about destruction of the state. You've moved beyond critical military targets and are focused largely on critical infrastructure.

We did that during the Second World War; its effects were mixed. The most important things were destroying the fuel distribution and manufacturing system, which opened the path for the Soviet armies in the east, and preventing the refueling of the air force.

The Iranians don't have a significant air force since the Shah left. They have very few aircraft. The navy was never a major player either—it consists largely of fast speed boats, unmanned submersibles, and unmanned surface craft designed to operate in the Persian Gulf.

Everything relies on missiles, target acquisition, and matching unmanned systems to targets. We think the Iranians have perhaps 45 to 50,000 unmanned systems remaining, maybe more, and somewhere between 15 to 20,000 missiles. That is more than adequate to sustain Iran through several weeks. They still have the ability, underground and out of reach, to manufacture missiles and drones. The Chinese and Russians have resupplied them.

On our side, we expended a thousand Joint Air-to-Surface Standoff Missiles in the first three weeks. We've probably replenished some stocks, especially anti-missile missiles like PAC-3 and THAAD. But radars that have been destroyed have not been replaced—they cost hundreds of millions and take years to manufacture. Precision-guided bombs are limited; we may rely on unguided bombs and carpet bombing with B-52s.

The Israelis have been reprovisioned as much as we could afford, but they're in a tougher position. We have more ships at sea, more carriers, more pilots, and more planes. We've replaced losses of KC-135 tankers. We're gearing up for something similar to before, only far more intense upfront.

Regarding ground troops: reports suggest President Trump has been unenthusiastic. That's a very good thing. I can't imagine any operation involving ground forces in the Persian Gulf—to seize islands—that would make any difference. It won't give control of the Gulf. I'm encouraged that he has expressed reservations.

The Iranians have made it very clear they will destroy most of what's on the west coast of the Persian Gulf. That is of great concern to the entire world. About 50% of the ingredients for fertilizer—urea, nitrogen, phosphate, etc.—comes out of the Persian Gulf. Destroying this infrastructure means famine across the Global South is unavoidable.

Liquid natural gas and petroleum are already problems. People are rationing in India, Indonesia, Thailand, and Vietnam. The cost of a barrel of jet fuel is now over $240 and rising. Britain is going to run out in about two weeks. Western European countries that went all-in on green energy—and shut down many nuclear reactors—are in terrible positions.

Governments in Europe are going to be overthrown. Recovery in the Persian Gulf, even if we stop now, will take a year or two according to some analysts; others say five to ten years. Much of this infrastructure may never be rebuilt. If anybody rebuilds it, it will probably be China. The global safe area for cash and money is increasingly China, not the United States. The petrodollar is already dead.

The Emirates, the Saudi House of Saud—all these Gulf elites have largely fled. The Turks and Egyptians have geared up to be far more involved. Israel will not fare well; whatever incentive the Iranians had for restraint no longer exists. They're ready for total war.

The lesson of history is that the side that goes to total war mobilization immediately—in the 20th century—has a better shot of winning. Hitler didn't fully mobilize until mid-1943. The Soviets, we, and the British did it quickly. We are not going to do that to defeat Iran.

General Caine suggested operations in the Strait of Malacca against Iranian vessels. I think that's a catastrophe. It's not unusual for someone losing a war to think they can expand it elsewhere. Foolish people say we'll also stop Chinese ships. That would widen the war further.

The ideal outcome at this stage would be to end this series of hostilities, admit we cannot gain control of the Strait of Hormuz with military power, and let the Iranians keep what they've got. The rest of the world should sit down with Iran and work out something similar to the Montreux Convention of 1926, which put the Turks in charge of the Dardanelles. The Turks have handled that very well. A similar convention can be set up, and the Iranians are the logical choice given their strategic position.

We don't like that. We're opposed to it. But we keep forgetting we don't live there. We live in the Western Hemisphere. Warfare has changed dramatically. With the right mix of missiles, unmanned systems, space-based surveillance, and good command and control, a much lesser power can be far more effective against a maritime-aerospace power.

**Glenn Diesen:** Bringing the fight to the Strait of Malacca would be a good way of pulling China closer into this war. That's a disaster on its own. You get the impression there's growing concern around the world—especially vocal in Asia—that the entire world order is being dismantled. International law itself is being rejected. This time it seems to be outright refuting it. What wider picture do you see? Is this a deliberate dismantling of the world order? There's massive pushback, not just economically, and remarkably, so much empathy for Iran around the world now. I haven't seen this before in my lifetime. What do you make of this?

**Douglas Macgregor:** If you look at India, India sees itself as dependent upon an extension cord that plugs into the Persian Gulf. That cord was suddenly pulled out. India does roughly $180 billion a year of business in the Persian Gulf. They are sensitive to international laws and norms and opposed to the use of force to solve anything if it can be avoided. They're shocked that the United States has opted for the use of force to solve Israel's problem with Iran.

If it were not for Israel, we would not be at war with Iran. There's no real reason for us to do so. There's a wealth of propaganda over the last three decades trying to demonize Iran and turn it into public enemy number one. That's nonsense.

After 9/11, one million Iranians poured into the streets in Tehran and elsewhere for candlelight vigils to mourn the loss of life in the United States. There was real hope for an improved relationship. They tried very hard, and we essentially spit in their faces.

They were very supportive of us in ways most people don't know. In Afghanistan and Iraq, General Soleimani helped persuade Shiite militias to stop fighting us. When we fought ISIS, those Shiite militias liberated Christians, Sunnis, Yazidis, Jews—anybody oppressed by ISIS—and fought hard with us, not against us.

All of that was swept away in favor of Israel's agenda and the Greater Israel project. The destruction in Gaza, the West Bank, Lebanon—all of it is intertwined with the desire to destroy Iran. There's no particular reason why we should be involved.

We've had a long-term agreement that enshrined the petrodollar. Now that's been destroyed. I don't think we're coming back to the Gulf, and we can't afford to. Only China can finance rebuilding. This whole war has been a strategic disaster for us.

The intelligent thing is to end it, admit we were wrong, and look for help from others to mediate—as a normal power in a multipolar system. But there's no appetite for that in Washington. We want to be the supranational authority, establish the rules, dominate, and win. Those days are passed.

**Glenn Diesen:** This adjustment from global primacy to multipolarity is probably going to bring more wars. In this war, the consequences for Asia are huge economically. In Europe, we see Zelenskyy making the point that the lack of air defenses is as bad as it could possibly be. At the same time, weapons are drying up. He's complaining that the US has been disrespectful—Witkoff visited Moscow but not Kiev. The EU is complaining that US weapons for Ukraine are delayed or diverted. Europeans are talking about a NATO without the US.

At the same time, they're escalating—sending massive drone programs to Ukraine, allowing their territory to be used to strike Russia's Baltic coast. Russia is warning that factories producing these drones are now legitimate targets. Where do you see all of this going? There seems to be delusion in Europe: obsession with defeating Russia, yet weapons are drying up, lack of capability is compensated with more belligerent language and dismissing red lines.

**Douglas Macgregor:** My perception is that the misrepresentation of Russia's true intentions—which never had anything to do with conquering anybody—will vanish in an energy crisis that the current governments in Western Europe can't master. That's the final straw. The globalist leaders who want to maintain this war against Russia will be thrown out of office.

I'm surprised we haven't seen open revolution in Germany and Great Britain yet. I think we will. The situation in England, Ireland, and Wales is really bad. The Irish have come out into the streets—they may be the canary in the coal mine.

People in Parliament are out of touch. They refused more drilling in the North Sea despite enormous deposits and overwhelming need. They're still living in an illusory world where wind and solar will replace carbon-based energy. It's not going to happen that way.

Europeans are concluding that. In Germany, you'll see a return to the status quo: restart nuclear power plants and come to terms with Moscow. All the abuse, insults, and lies hurled at Moscow have to be dismantled. The media, controlled by globalist governments, has not told the truth. They've portrayed Ukraine as luminous patriots fighting for freedom. That's nonsense.

The Ukrainians were built up by us and the Europeans to attack Russia. NATO is finished. President Putin has restrained Russian military power. He has everything needed to finish this war decisively this year. He also has the means to flatten areas in the Baltic states, Germany, Poland, or Great Britain where drones are manufactured. He has held back. The Russians want action; he doesn't.

I hope European governments will come to their senses sooner rather than later. New people will come in and behave differently. The corruption in the Zelenskyy regime—its criminal underpinnings—has to be revealed. The truth has to come out. Many supposed Russian atrocities turned out to be committed by Ukrainians.

What the Ukrainians have done is unforgivable, and this has to stop. We have to stop them. I had hoped Donald Trump would pull the rug on that, but he's been unable to. He is truly the prisoner of the Washington status quo. That extends to the Persian Gulf and Iran.

Congress has done nothing to restrain him. They're all on the payroll. The Israel lobby ensures money flows to those who support Israel. There's a vote this week on the War Powers Resolution—I hope it produces something, but I'm not optimistic. There's too much inertia.

Donald Trump cannot get past his three rules from Roy Cohn: deny, deny, attack, attack, and twist everything into a victory. When asked why he put his face on the figure of Jesus Christ, he said he thought of himself as a physician healing people. That's absurd. He should have said it was a terrible mistake, in bad taste, and he regrets it.

He can't tell the truth. The Iranians are not going to talk to us. There's no point. They watched our meeting with Putin in Anchorage—we showed up with nothing. The Iranians came to Islamabad with 70 people and substantive materials, thinking they were there to negotiate. They discovered it was all about submitting to Israel. That's not going to happen.

**Glenn Diesen:** This "deny, deny, attack, attack, and claim victory" comes with a great cost. With the picture of Trump as Jesus, it comes out so much worse. The same in Iran when the US killed all those little girls. If he had just said it was accidental—a horrible thing that happens in war—it could have been put to rest. Instead, claiming Iran launched Tomahawks at its own schools makes it so much worse and keeps the wound open. This strategy has to have some exceptions, otherwise it's very self-destructive.

**Douglas Macgregor:** There's a portion of the base that believes in "my country right or wrong, but always my country." But at this point, we have to identify mistakes to move beyond them. He's not the man to do it. He keeps saying we have the greatest military in the world. We had the greatest military designed to refight World War II. It's not the one we need—it hasn't been since 1991. Instead of recognizing that, he says he rebuilt the military in his first term. He put money into the old structure but didn't build anything new.

If anything good comes out of this, we'll see a turnover in Washington. Americans are not stupid. They see what's happening. Elections don't seem to matter—we get the same outcome. That realization is setting in. That's a good thing.

**Glenn Diesen:** Well, Colonel, thank you for taking the time and sharing your insights. As always, it's been very interesting.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Okay. Thank you, Glenn.
