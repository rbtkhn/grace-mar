# Iran defies U.S. / no Islamabad talks; ceasefire; Hormuz; Donbass; Baltics (operator transcript)

**Video title (operator):** Iran Defies US, Refuses Talks; Ceasefire About To End; Russia Warns Donbass Falling, Baltics Will Follow

**Date (voice):** Tuesday, 21 April 2026

---

Good day. Today is Tuesday, 21 April 2026.

Before I begin the program, let me first make a few points about the announcement in London of the death of Lord Robert Skidelsky. He was a great historian and a great economist, someone from whom I was privileged to receive regular commentaries. I never met him or spoke to him — we were not acquainted — but clearly he followed my work. He used to watch these programs, and of course I have followed his for many decades, basically from the time when I began to study history way back in the 1970s.

Perhaps most important of all, at least in topical political and geopolitical terms, he was the only important voice in British politics who was a consistent critic of British policy with respect to Russia and the war in Ukraine. He argued persuasively, convincingly, and repeatedly in the House of Lords for a fundamental rethinking and change in British foreign policy — for dialogue with Russia and for efforts to negotiate peace in Ukraine. He argued consistently against this open-ended, disastrous support for the war in Ukraine which, in part on the basis of what it was doing to Ukraine itself, he thought was not just wrong in geopolitical terms but wrong in moral terms as well.

His consistency in doing this — even as he was basically a single voice in the entire British Parliament arguing this case — speaks of a man of tremendous integrity and courage.

I should just say that his loss is personally deeply upsetting for me. Learning about it today, even as these tawdry events continue in Parliament with the testimony of Sir Olly Robbins, the former chief civil servant in the Foreign Office, about the Mandelson-Epstein-Starmer affair — well, the contrast between the integrity, courage, dignity, and wisdom of Lord Robert Skidelsky and the shocking disclosures and behavior in that other part of Parliament is, for me as a British person, nothing short of painful.

Anyway, there it is. Now I will proceed with the rest of the program.

Today we finally have clarity about the negotiations in Islamabad — or at least we appear to, as of the start of this program. I have just been checking the Iranian media, and they confirm that even as the United States is sending its negotiating team to Islamabad — with uncertainty surrounding Vice President JD Vance, who is in and out of favor with the President — the Iranian authorities have said straightforwardly and clearly that there is no Iranian delegation going to Islamabad today. There is nothing to talk about, nothing to discuss.

The Americans are continuing to try to apply pressure on Iran via the sea blockade. The Iranians are furious about the attack on an Iranian ship carrying oil that took place two days ago. They say that in these circumstances it is obviously wrong and inappropriate to hold further discussions with the Americans — all the more so as there has been no shift and no change in the American negotiating position.

They continue to make impossible, unreasonable demands of Iran. They continue to demand that Iran hand over the entirety of its enriched uranium stockpile to the United States — not to Russia, not to Pakistan or other neighboring countries favorable to Iran, but that Iran capitulate and humiliate itself by handing it over to the Americans. The Iranians insist they have every right to possess this stockpile as a party in good standing with the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty.

On top of that, the Iranians are not prepared to even consider American demands that Iran do away with its ballistic missile program — which, as the Iranians never cease to point out, is what provides them with deterrence against potential attack by their primary enemy, Israel, and their secondary enemies in the Gulf, not to mention the United States itself.

Given that this is the stance the Americans are taking, the Iranians say there is really no point in them turning up to Islamabad and conducting a negotiation which, to all intents and purposes, is not a negotiation at all but a dialogue of the deaf.

The Iranian media is full of commentaries and reports about how the United States is on the brink of conducting a further attack on Iran. The Iranians anticipate that attack over the next few days. They have watched the American military buildup in the Persian Gulf area. They are aware that the United States looks to be preparing some kind of additional missile offensive against Iran. They are also aware of the continued movement of ground forces to the Persian Gulf area by the United States, perhaps in anticipation of some sort of ground operation. And, of course, the sea blockade itself remains in place.

The Iranians have no trust and no confidence. They were not impressed by the fact that the President of the United States at one point appeared to sack JD Vance as chief negotiator and replace him with Jared Kushner, only for the President to reverse himself again within a few hours. We have had no explanation from the United States about these strange changes in the makeup of the negotiating team.

Whether it is the Vice President who doesn’t want to go because he thinks he’s been set up for failure and is frustrated that he’s been pushed into a negotiating process where he does not have the proper and sincere backing of the President himself, or whether the President is playing some kind of cruel mind game with his Vice President — whom many people have noticed he has taken to teasing relentlessly in ways I personally find excruciating — almost certainly a reflection of the President’s annoyance and resentment that the Vice President spoke out against the Iran operation (and has since been proved right).

Or perhaps the President himself cannot make up his mind whether to pursue a negotiation track or go for a full military solution, and is keeping his options open by sending someone who does seem to have his heart in negotiations to Islamabad. Or perhaps it is because the Iranians made clear they were not prepared to negotiate with Jared Kushner as chief negotiator, and the Pakistanis — who are desperate to get these negotiations going — made clear to the United States that it was essential to send a negotiator the Iranians would accept, which of course had to be JD Vance.

One way or the other, this instability in the American negotiating team will have reinforced Iranian doubts that the Americans are actually serious about this process at all.

So there we have it: no negotiations today, at least that appears to be the position at the moment. In a few hours the ceasefire will expire. President Trump, over the last couple of days amongst the many contradictory things he has said, has also said that 99% he does not expect to extend the ceasefire any further.

The Iranians themselves were always skeptical about the ceasefire and only agreed to it under heavy pressure from their friends in Pakistan and China. They will probably feel vindicated in their skepticism by the erratic course of US policy ever since.

It seems to me that, as of the moment of making this program, the strong likelihood is that the ceasefire will indeed end in a few hours’ time and we will be back to military action. Quite plausibly that action will initially take the form of heavy missile strikes and bombing by the United States of various Iranian targets, with counter-strikes and drone strikes by Iran — which we have seen have been repeatedly devastating and which US outlets such as Bloomberg confirm the Iranians have the capability to sustain for a very long time because of the continued size of their drone and missile arsenals.

There is also a possibility that more ambitious military action might be taken: that the United States might attempt some kind of operation using ground troops in Iran — be it against one of the islands in the Strait of Hormuz or Kharg Island, or perhaps to capture the Bushehr nuclear power plant, or conceivably this reckless, unbelievably dangerous scheme to land an infiltration force deep inside Iran close to the city of Isfahan with the intention of removing Iran’s stockpile of enriched uranium.

We are likely to see one or some of these things happen over the next few hours.

Meanwhile, I read reports that the Iranians have now begun on a systematic basis the mining of the Strait of Hormuz. Apparently they are planting large numbers of sea mines. This is not like what we heard a few weeks ago. It seems now they are doing it in earnest. That of course will severely complicate any naval operations and make it all but impossible for merchant ships to transit the Strait of Hormuz unless they receive guidance from the Iranian authorities themselves through the minefields.

The Iranians were apparently provoked to do this by the attack on the Iranian merchant ship. Previously the United States has claimed that its navy has been undertaking mine clearance operations in the Strait of Hormuz. I confess I have great doubts about that — my deep skepticism that anything of that kind has really taken place. That skepticism has been reinforced by further reports that the Americans, in the midst of these mine clearance operations, had failed to find a single Iranian mine. Perhaps they didn’t find any because they weren’t actually looking for them.

The US attorney Robert Barnes has said publicly that we have now reached the point where US military officials are misleading the President about what is going on. They are intentionally telling him what he wants to hear rather than the truth because they are so alarmed about the kind of orders he might give them if they did tell him the truth.

That is what the situation looks to be today. There remain many imponderables. If there is a return to actual fighting, then I think we are not only going to see oil prices and energy prices surge, but there is going to be a major loss of confidence in the financial markets. The markets were reassured by the ceasefire and the appearance of negotiations. If negotiations are merely postponed, some major players may take that in their stride. But if fighting actually resumes, with attacks by each side on the other, that is an entirely different matter.

If the Houthis follow through on the threats that both they and the Iranians are making and close the Red Sea — and I spoke about Ali Velayati’s comments about that — then the crisis we are going to see will be multiple times greater.

The United States continues to deploy forces to the region. The USS Gerald Ford — the carrier that had been redeployed to the Caribbean, then transferred to the Middle East, which then experienced various breakdowns (I’m being polite) in the Red Sea with stories about toilets and laundry fires — returned to US bases in Greece for some sort of refit. It was widely expected it would return to the United States and be replaced by the USS George H.W. Bush. It turns out the Gerald Ford is being redeployed back to the Iranian theater of operations again.

Once the George H.W. Bush arrives together with the Abraham Lincoln, there will be three US carrier groups gathered around Iran. This is despite the logistical difficulties I have discussed in previous programs caused by the lack of bases in close proximity to the Arabian Sea and the Persian Gulf, reports about personnel on helicopter carriers being given reduced rations, and despite the fact that the crews are probably tired and unhappy. Despite all that, the United States continues to deploy them and insists they participate in the coming battle.

This is an unreported part of the story. The United States has 11 nuclear-powered super carriers. At the time of the Iraq war in 1991, the United States was able to deploy five US Navy carriers to support the operation against Saddam Hussein’s Iraq without undue difficulty. Today, the United States is struggling to keep three Navy carriers in operation. The rest of the fleet is either under repair or on extended duty elsewhere. The force being concentrated against Iran still falls far short of the force the United States gathered to defeat Iraq in 1990–91, despite the fact that Iran is a much bigger and far more powerful country than Iraq was then.

We could be seeing a change in relative US military power — perhaps less convincing and less strong today than it was 35 years ago — while Iran is far more powerful than it was then, and the global military balance has shifted decisively against the United States since that time.

For Pakistan, this is a disastrous outcome. One of the reasons Pakistan has been so committed to trying to get the mediation process to succeed is because Pakistan’s economy has been particularly badly hit by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. There are reports that Pakistan is rapidly running out of oil and other energy products, and I suspect of funding as well, as the Gulf kingdoms and Saudis try to husband their financial reserves. That is probably going to influence Pakistani decision-making and make the Pakistanis align themselves even more closely with China, which is probably the only country at the moment that can help them get through this difficult time.

The other big player that has the potential to change the entire way the game is being played is China. China made a big diplomatic effort to support the Pakistani mediation. They are very unhappy about obstruction to navigation at any point on the world ocean. They have been the target of speculation in the United States about the US one day imposing sea blockades upon China. For that reason, the Chinese have been major advocates of unrestricted navigation through all the world’s oceans.

They are also not unaware that some US officials have been telling each other that this operation against Iran is ultimately about cutting off China from sources of oil. The Chinese have therefore been pursuing a policy of preserving the government in Iran and trying to get back to something close to the situation that existed before February 28th — to get the Americans and Iranians talking and to get the Strait of Hormuz reopened.

In my opinion, the Americans have badly damaged and changed that Chinese calculus by imposing the sea blockade on Iran. From the Chinese point of view, the issue has now shifted from Iran to the United States. It is now the United States — a far more powerful and much more dangerous country than Iran — which is imposing restrictions on global trade. The Americans are now trying to interrupt shipping from the Persian Gulf to China itself.

I remain of the view that at some point China will intervene — not because it is short of oil, but because it will almost certainly feel the need to assert itself in order to protect its right to conduct international trade. I continue to hear reports about movements of Chinese warships to the region. If Chinese warships do start to appear in the region in force, this is going to change the entire dynamic of the conflict and put immense pressure on the United States.

In a recent program I said that the widespread assumption in the West that the Chinese would never do this was complacent and misplaced. The Chinese Defense Ministry has already made statements to this effect, and they have the precedent of what their Russian friends and allies are doing by providing naval escorts for merchant ships trading with Russia.

I have recently received further information which puts the extent of Russian protection for their shipping at an entirely different level. There are reports in Russia that Russia did indeed provide a naval escort for the tanker that recently visited Cuba and delivered 730,000 barrels of oil — and that naval escort took the form of a Russian Navy nuclear submarine which was lurking in the sea close to the tanker at all times. The United States was aware it was there. Nuclear submarines are exceptionally powerful weapon systems. The US Navy was not willing to tangle with it, and that was the primary reason why that tanker was able to get through.

There is another tanker like that coming to Cuba soon, and it is plausible it will be provided with the same level of protection. I did not expect the Russians would do something on this scale, but I believe they have. The Chinese will be following it and drawing lessons from the fact that the United States was not prepared to take military action against merchant ships protected by the Russian Navy in that way. The Chinese will probably decide the United States is not going to be prepared to take action against ships protected by the Chinese Navy either. This whole affair is going to give those arguments traction in Beijing and make the Chinese more willing to send their warships to the Arabian Sea and the Persian Gulf.

Having said all this, it is also important to note that from the Chinese point of view, the announcement of the US blockade and the attack on the Iranian ship has almost certainly tilted China away from pursuing mediation and trying to close this conflict down. It must seem to the Chinese that the United States is indeed intent on unlimited escalation. In which case, the proper way for China to counter that is not by proposing mediation — which can only work when both sides are willing to come to an agreement — but on the contrary to assert Chinese power in a way that might put pressure on the United States and oblige it to alter its behavior.

My own view is that this sea blockade the United States imposed has been a profound mistake. French President Emmanuel Macron has just made a speech saying exactly the same thing. I am always uncomfortable when I find myself agreeing with Emmanuel Macron — a stopped clock is right twice a day. Macron is 90% wrong about everything. Every so often he gets it right, and he did so on this occasion.

That is the state of the Iran crisis today. I personally expect significant escalation over the next few hours. We will see whether I am right.

Meanwhile, there continue to be very significant developments in the Ukraine conflict, and it is to that I will now turn.

The Russians are continuing to maintain pressure against the Baltic states and against the Europeans. We have now had an extremely forceful comment from Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Mikhail Ulyanov. He posted on X: “We are at the stage when fundamental decisions are to be made. If the Baltic states really believe that Russia is afraid of any action aimed to protect the human rights of the Russian population, they are wrong. They will face the real situation shortly.”

I would add that Russian Foreign Ministry spokeswoman Maria Zakharova also gave a briefing yesterday which struck me as very unusual even by her standards for the uncompromising stance she was taking on this issue.

Ulyanov is talking about action by the Russians being taken shortly. Of course, we don’t know what “shortly” means — it could be tomorrow, next week, in a month, or in three months. It is a relative term.

It is worth pointing out again that the major decision-maker, Vladimir Putin, continues to maintain his stony silence about all matters related to the conflict in Ukraine, the position of the Baltic states, relations between Russia and the United States, and Russia’s relationships with the various European powers. At the moment the rhetoric in Russia is coming from other people — from Shoigu, Lavrov, Medvedev, and now Ulyanov.

Here is what I think is taking place. Contrary to some claims, there is no doubt whatsoever that we are now in the first or perhaps second week of Russia’s spring-summer-autumn offensive. The Russians are on the attack on every part of the front lines.

General Gerasimov, the overall Russian military commander and Chief of the General Staff, said in a briefing that the battle of Donbass is almost at an end. The day is close when the two remaining cities in Donbass — Slaviansk and Kramatorsk — are going to be liberated. He said the Russian army is advancing towards Slaviansk. “The day is not far off when we will liberate Slaviansk and Kramatorsk. All this is Donetsk land. Thank you for your courageous and proactive actions. You contribute to the liberation of Donbass.”

He is confident that the battle for the liberation of Donbass is coming to an end and that the battle for Slaviansk and Kramatorsk — the last parts of Donbass — is about to start.

He also spoke about intense fighting in Kostiantynivka, with assault detachments delivering strikes deep into the city from the northeast and southwest. Fighting is also taking place in the suburbs. My own understanding, heavily based on reports from Rybar, is that the Russians now control the central and western parts of Kostiantynivka. Based on what Gerasimov is saying, it looks as if they are also pushing in from the northeast. The battle there remains very intense, and it looks as if the turning point has been passed.

He spoke about successful operations by the Northern group in the Sumy and Kharkov regions. Ukrainian sources are already saying that the city of Sumy is now on the brink of becoming surrounded. Gerasimov said the encircled enemy was routed on the eastern bank of the Oskil River close to Kupiansk. So the entire territory on the east bank of the Oskil River close to Kupiansk is now under Russian control.

He said the Russians are now conducting urban warfare — street fighting — in Borova, Studenok, and Staryi Karavan. Borova is a town on the east bank of the Oskil River. The Russians have not been very active there until recently, but it looks as if they are now storming Borova and extinguishing this salient. If this happens, the Russians will be in complete control of the entirety of the east bank of the Oskil.

Sviatohirsk is a religious center located to the northwest of Lyman. The Russians reached its outskirts about two months ago, but Gerasimov says fighting is now taking place there as well. Lyman itself: about 70% of the city’s territory is under Russian control. If we believe Gerasimov — which I do — it looks as if Lyman is finally about to fall. With the Russians operating in Sviatohirsk, Studenok, and Staryi Karavan, one can see how, unless the Ukrainians withdraw from Lyman fairly soon, they are likely to be surrounded there.

Staryi Karavan is located very close to Slaviansk itself, and Lyman has always been seen as a satellite town of Slaviansk. When the Russians captured Lyman for a short time in May 2022, it caused a huge brief panic in the Ukrainian part of the internet. There were worries at that time that the Russians were going to sweep through to Slaviansk itself. If the Russians capture Staryi Karavan this time, that might actually start to happen.

Gerasimov said the Russians have expanded their control northwest of Pokrovsk in the direction of the town of Druzhkivka. He confirmed that the important village of Hrodivka has indeed been captured, as well as another nearby village called Pavlivka. He said there are still battles going on in Novodonetske and Berezivka, but he describes these as mopping-up operations. So it looks as if the battle for Velyka Novosilka at least is coming to an end.

In some ways the most interesting comments of all: Gerasimov spoke about how the Russians are creating a security belt in Dnipropetrovsk region. According to him, they now control 75% of Novo Pavlivka. This is an important fortified town in Dnipropetrovsk region. This is the first time in any of his briefings that Gerasimov has referred to it. A “security belt” is a vague concept. Other reports suggest the Russians are planning to continue their westward advance in Dnipropetrovsk region ultimately all the way to the city of Pavlohrad, an important industrial and urban area to the east of the Dnieper River. If Pavlohrad is eventually captured, that will put the Russians on the eastern bank of the Dnieper in its central regions.

Lastly, Gerasimov said that Ukrainian counterattacks in Zaporizhzhia region have been unsuccessful. He put Ukrainian losses there at 3,000 troops and 160 pieces of hardware. He said all Ukrainian claims to have captured territory in this area are untrue, and that the Russians are consolidating and pushing westwards. They have now liberated the villages of Boyove and Luhivske in the Hulyaipole area as well.

The key point to repeat is what Gerasimov said about Slaviansk and Kramatorsk: their liberation is now imminent and the battle of Donbass is indeed about to end.

The Russians, as they survey the situation at the moment, are probably assessing that things are playing out very well indeed from their point of view.

Zelensky has made further statements about how Ukraine can no longer rely on the United States. The flow of weapons and money from the United States has essentially ended. He must know that the Europeans are in no position to step in and make up the difference tomorrow. He expects the Europeans to approve the €90 billion loan. Presumably that has to be raised on the international financial markets. I have discussed the difficulties of that already. €60 billion of those €90 billion are going to be paid over to European arms manufacturers to make weapons for Ukraine, which the Ukrainians might in theory receive in three to five years’ time — or perhaps never. More realistically, €30 billion will go to Ukraine itself and will no doubt make possible many more golden toilets, though perhaps not in Kiev.

Even Zelensky now acknowledges that the flow of support from the United States is coming to an end. Even if the Democrats win back control of Congress in November, the heart has gone out of it as far as the Ukrainian war is concerned. The United States has far more important and far more pressing things to worry about than the fate of Vladimir Zelensky.

Zelensky is of course unwilling to consider any possible negotiation or any kind of concession. But the Russians know that the US is becoming exhausted with Ukraine. The Russians are achieving these advances on the battle fronts, looking within the next few months to the end of the battle of Donbass, strongly positioned to take advantage in the Kharkov and Sumy regions and decisively in Zaporizhzhia region as well, and in a global situation which from their point of view must look optimal.

The United States is bogged down in an indefinite conflict with Iran — unable to bend Iran to their will, but unable to break Iran either. Western economies are soon going to experience the full force of the energy shock. A Chinese ally that is becoming increasingly exasperated by the United States and which inevitably is going to turn to Russia increasingly in order to secure its energy supplies. And Europe — economically prostrate and now increasingly at odds with the United States, with the President of the United States and some of his senior officials becoming increasingly hostile to NATO and to Europe and questioning the point of NATO and its usefulness to the United States in ways that have never happened before.

Obviously the Russians don’t expect NATO to just collapse or the United States to walk out of NATO. But at this specific point in time, conflicts between the Europeans and the Americans must be seen in Moscow as working to Russia’s advantage.

They are even noticing that no less a person than Friedrich Merz — extremely anti-Russian historically, very pro-American, a fervid supporter of Ukraine — is finding it more and more difficult to conceal his anger and exasperation with the United States. Merz recently made comments criticizing American policy towards Cuba, basically saying that the Americans should leave Cuba alone — which of course, as it happens, is what the Russians are also saying.

With the situation on the front lines in Ukraine going their way, it is not surprising that the Russians feel that the moment has come for them to become more assertive. It is a sign of their growing self-confidence. They probably don’t expect and don’t want their army to march into the Baltic states, but they probably calculate that if they pile up the pressure, the Europeans will be forced to twist the arms of the Baltic states and get them to make significant concessions.

The leaders of the Baltic states would be well advised to take this hardening of rhetoric from Moscow seriously in exactly the way Ulyanov is telling them to. I still find it difficult to believe that the Russian army is actually going to cross the border into Estonia or do something of that kind, but this hardening is there and it is becoming unambiguously clear.

That is where we are at the present time.

Obviously in any situation of conflict there is always the possibility for sudden change, and the Russians for example have concerns of their own. Putin, as I discussed recently, is very angry with Nabiullina, the Central Bank chair, because of her interest rate policy, which entirely predictably — but contrary to Nabiullina’s assurances — led to a 1.8% contraction in the Russian economy as compared to last year in January and February. Putin has made no secret of his displeasure about this. Nabiullina, not directly but through some of her officials, has been trying publicly to defend her interest rate policy.

One way or the other, I believe we will start to see interest rates come down faster in Russia in a few months. The Economics Ministry expects growth to return to Russia mid-year and I suspect they are right. There is talk of stabilization in consumer demand in Russia this month.

The Russians do have their concerns about the economy and about other things, but from the cockpit of the Kremlin, as Putin surveys the global situation — provided things don’t escalate out of control in the Persian Gulf, which is something Putin and the Russians will not want to see at all — if things remain at their current level, well, from a Russian point of view it is just about as good as it gets.
