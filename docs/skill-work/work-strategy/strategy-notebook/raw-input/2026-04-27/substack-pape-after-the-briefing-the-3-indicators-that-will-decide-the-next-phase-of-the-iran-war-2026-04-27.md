# After the Briefing: The 3 Indicators That Will Decide the Next Phase of the Iran War

For Paid Subscribers Only
Prof Robert Pape
Apr 27, 2026

Yesterday's live briefing focused on a simple point: this conflict is not being decided by economic pressure alone. It is being shaped by a deeper structural contest over power, alignment, and escalation.

The key question now is not what leaders say.

It is what they do next.

Over the next several weeks, three indicators will tell us whether this war moves toward escalation, containment, or a broader shift in global power.

I. U.S. Military Posture: Deterrence or Preparation for Strike
The United States has now positioned a third aircraft carrier in the Gulf, creating one of the most concentrated naval strike forces in the region in decades.

The presence alone does not predict the future.

Carrier groups can signal deterrence-or they can prepare the battlefield.

The distinction will be visible in operational behavior.

Watch for:

Sustained increases in sortie rates from carrier air wings as essentially practice runs

Expanded ISR (intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance) focused on Iranian missile and drone sites, and storage areas for Iran's accumulating oil reserves

Pre-positioning of munitions and logistics assets beyond routine levels, especially at more distant bases such as in Jordan

Diplomatic/civilian evacuation advisories or force protection alerts across countries with U.S. regional bases

If these indicators intensify together, the United States is not just deterring escalation-it is positioning for renewed large-scale strikes.

If they remain limited, the current posture is likely intended to contain the conflict without expanding it.

This is the first signal: whether Washington is preparing to move up the escalation ladder.

II. Iran's External Alignment: Isolation or Strategic Breakout
Economic pressure only works if the target remains isolated.

Iran's next moves will determine whether that condition holds.

The critical question is whether Tehran can build a countervailing network that offsets the effects of the U.S. blockade and prepares for potential US escalation-not in rhetoric, but in material terms.

Watch for three concrete developments.

First, trade and energy corridors through Pakistan.
If Iran expands overland routes for fuel, goods, and financial flows, it reduces dependence on maritime channels vulnerable to interdiction.

Second, Russian involvement beyond diplomacy.
This does not require formal alliance. It would be visible in:

expanded military-technical cooperation

intelligence sharing

or any form of Russian presence tied to Gulf security arrangements

Even limited moves in this direction would signal that Iran is not strategically isolated and building a growing set of arrangements to offset US power in the region.

Third, Oman-mediated arrangements in the Strait of Hormuz.
If mechanisms emerge for regulated passage-such as coordinated tolling, deconfliction zones, or parallel shipping understandings-this would indicate movement toward a managed equilibrium rather than open disruption.

Individually, these three steps are each incremental.

Together, they would represent something larger: a shift from pressure-induced isolation to networked resilience.

That is how major states absorb economic coercion without conceding core interests.

III. Great Power Linkage: Regional Conflict or Global Bargaining Chip
The third indicator is whether this conflict remains regionally bounded-or becomes entangled in great power competition.

The focus should not be on personalities or single meetings.

It should be on geopolitical linkage.

China, in particular, holds leverage across multiple domains relevant to this conflict:

energy markets

sanctions evasion pathways

financial and trade networks that can cushion economic pressure

The question is whether Iran becomes part of a broader bargaining framework involving U.S.-China relations.

Watch for:

any linkage between Gulf stability and wider economic or security discussions

changes in Chinese enforcement-or non-enforcement-of sanctions-related constraints

signals of coordination, or deliberate non-cooperation, in international forums

The obvious focal point for these developments: Taiwan

Even modest shifts related to the decline of US material support for Taiwan matter.

If the Iran conflict becomes embedded in wider great power bargaining, the result is not faster resolution.

It is a redistribution of leverage-away from the United States and toward actors able to operate across multiple theaters simultaneously. This redistribution necessarily weakens US relative power - the only kind of power that matters over time.

What These Indicators Mean Together
These three indicators are not separate.

They answer a single question:

Is this conflict moving toward escalation, containment, or a shift in the balance of power?

If U.S. military signals point toward strike preparation, escalation risk rises sharply.

If Iran successfully builds external alignment, economic pressure loses coercive force.

If great powers become more deeply engaged, the conflict widens beyond its original frame.

None of these pathways produces quick resolution, even as the world's economy proceeds to move from rising prices to actual shortages to emerging economic contraction.

The Timeline Ahead
The expectation embedded in much of the current commentary-that time and pressure will force a decision-is not supported by the historical record.

What we are more likely to see over the next 30 days is not collapse, but the persistence of costly conflict:

continued economic disruption

periodic spikes in military tension

incremental shifts in alignment

This is what conflicts look like when core security interests are at stake and coercion by punishment fails.

They do not end quickly.

They evolve.

And the indicators above will tell us which direction that evolution is taking-before the headlines catch up.
