# What the Smart Questions Reveal About Where This War Is Going

From China's leverage to market denial, the answers point in one direction: escalation is becoming structurally harder to stop.
Prof Robert Pape
Apr 29, 2026

What stood out from Sunday's briefing was not just the volume of questions-but their structure. Many of you are now asking the right second-order questions: not "what is happening," but "what would have to be true for this to end?"

Those answers don't just explain the war-they determine what happens next in the coming weeks.

That shift matters because it changes what comes next.

The quality of this community is rising fast.

Here are five questions that get to the core of the war-and what they reveal.

1. Will China pressure Iran into a settlement?
At first glance, this seems plausible. China benefits from stable energy flows and has little interest in global economic collapse.

But this assumes China's priority is stability.

It is not. It is leverage - in the form of future relative power and wealth.

A prolonged crisis in the Gulf weakens United States credibility, strains alliances, and accelerates alternatives to the dollar system. Those are structural gains for China that do not require Iran to "win" in the sense of defeating the US in a conventional military clash-only to not lose and gain power and influence at America's expense.

So pressure will exist-but it will be bounded.

China will push Iran away from catastrophic escalation (e.g., initiating a new round of missile and drone strikes on Gulf countries and oil infrastructure - unless in retaliation for US strikes on Iran's energy infrastructure), not toward meaningful concessions on the enriched uranium or surrendering control of Hormuz.

That is a ceiling, not an off-ramp.

2. Why are markets not pricing this in?
Several of you asked versions of this. It's the right puzzle.

Markets are not ignoring the war-they are discounting its timing.

Investors are assuming either:

A near-term deal, or

A contained conflict that avoids systemic disruption, or (most likely)

Those assumptions rest on a belief that economic pressure works quickly.

Historically, it does not.

There is a lag between physical disruption (shipping, insurance, rerouting flows) and visible macro impact (inflation, unemployment, earnings). That lag can be weeks.

Markets are pricing the first stage of the war.

They are not pricing the second. When is more a question of trader psychology that economic realities, but realities do have a way of presenting themselves.

3. What is Iran's endgame-and why delay?
This is the most important question in the set.

Iran is not trying to win quickly. It is trying to change the structure of the game.

Delay serves three purposes:

It raises the global economic cost of the conflict

It tests the political limits of U.S. escalation

It creates space for parallel economic and diplomatic channels (through Pakistan, Russia, Oman)

In other words, delay is not stalling.

It is strategy:

The longer the war continues without decisive U.S. success, the more plausible a new equilibrium becomes-one in which Iran retains leverage over Hormuz and gains recognition as a durable regional, and emerging 4th global power center.

4. Why didn't Trump act on his deadline threat?
Because the next step is qualitatively different.

Moving from economic coercion to decisive military action-especially ground operations-requires a pretext to generate at least a temporary increase in:

Domestic political support

Allied backing

A credible reason why the risks are necessary

Right now, none are in place.

As Lyndon Johnson did after the 1964 Gulf of Tonkin incident, a triggering event could rapidly generate the political support needed to escalate.

This is the core of the escalation trap.

Failure to compel -> fear of losing credibility -> pressure to escalate

5. What signals would confirm a move to ground war?
This is where analysis becomes predictive.

Watch for three indicators:

Logistics shift: movement from strike assets to sustainment (fuel, engineering, medical)

Force composition: units designed for holding territory, not just hitting targets

Operational objectives: public or leaked discussion of securing physical assets (nuclear sites, chokepoints)

Absent these, we remain in Stage II: coercion and pressure.

Once they appear, the system has crossed into Stage III.

That transition is hard to reverse.

The Deeper Reality
The questions you are asking now point to a deeper reality:

This war is no longer about immediate outcomes.

It is about whether the United States can still impose outcomes at acceptable cost-or whether a new balance of power is emerging in real time.

That is why the indicators matter more than the headlines.

And right now, those indicators are not pointing toward resolution-they are pointing toward escalation of costs.

--Robert Pape
