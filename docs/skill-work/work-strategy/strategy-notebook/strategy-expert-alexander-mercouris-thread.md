**PakistanвЂ“Iran bridge, escorts, and Islamabad II**
Over the last 24 hours, the fragile ceasefires in the conflicts between the United States and Iran, and between Israel and Iran, remain in effect.
There is also an apparent ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon, agreed through discussions between the Israelis and the Lebanese government (in which Hezbollah has a significant role).
At present, the main news concerning this conflict is the arrival in Tehran of the Chief of the Army Staff of the Pakistani armed forces, Field Marshal Asim Munir.
He flew in from Pakistan and, as far as I am aware, is the first high-level foreign visitor from a government to arrive in Iran openly since hostilities began on 28 February.
There may have been covert visits by officials from countries such as China or Russia, but Field Marshal Munir arrived publicly.
His aircraft was escorted by Iranian fighter jets вЂ” MiG-29s and F-4 Phantoms вЂ” as it landed in Tehran.
President Trump has repeatedly claimed that the entire Iranian air force has been destroyed.
Clearly, that is not the case.
Iran still has operational fighter jets and pilots capable of escorting foreign aircraft.
To be clear, IranвЂ™s air force is a small factor in this conflict and cannot confront the far superior air forces of the United States and Israel head-on.
IranвЂ™s primary defensive capability in the skies remains its surface-to-air missiles and integrated air defence system, which has shown it can still inflict damage, as seen in recent events near Isfahan.
Nevertheless, the appearance of these Iranian fighters disproves claims of total destruction.
Similar claims were made about IranвЂ™s navy and about Russian-supplied Mi-28 helicopter gunships; yet at least some of the latter have been seen flying over Tehran in recent days.
Many claims made during this war have turned out to be overstated.
Both sides had expected the discussions to proceed.
The Pakistanis are clearly keen to restart the process and have been pressing for an extension of the current two-week ceasefire, which expires next Tuesday.
Yesterday, Western media (especially in Britain) briefly reported that an agreement had been reached to extend the ceasefire by another two weeks.
However, both the United States and Iran quickly denied any such deal.
President Trump has expressed little interest in extending it.
As things stand, the ceasefire appears set to end on Tuesday, with the Russians warning that fighting could then resume at an even more intense level.
The Pakistanis are doing what they can, and the Iranians say they want to work with them to restart direct talks with the Americans.
Iran is careful not to antagonize Pakistan, which coordinates closely with its main ally, China вЂ” a country Iran is eager to keep on good terms with.
For the moment, however, the Pakistanis seem to be having more success with the Iranians than with the Americans.
My overall sense is that the mood in Washington has shifted back toward greater confrontation.

**U.S. theory of victory, blockade limits, next escalation**
I have no expectation that any of this will happen.
Iran is a huge country with significant natural resources and industrial capacity.
Having lived under sanctions for decades, it has already built a high degree of self-sufficiency.
In wartime, a government can demand sacrifices from the population and impose economic controls that are impossible in peacetime.
Moreover, Iran will continue to receive economic support from China and Russia, neither of which wants to see Iran collapse.
Expectations of what the sea blockade can achieve are, in my view, greatly exaggerated.
For the record, I suspect that by next week вЂ” especially if the ceasefire ends вЂ” the United States will go beyond the blockade.
There are rumours of another major missile strike in preparation.
What more that would achieve beyond previous strikes is unclear to me.
The Russians, who have good sources, are probably correct in their anticipation.
The current two-week ceasefire appears to have been intended, at least in part, to allow the US time to concentrate forces for such an operation.
The Iranians, for their part, are using the pause to increase drone production, rebuild bridges and logistics, and prepare underground facilities.
Both sides are gearing up for the next round, which we will likely see sometime next week.
Even with additional US deployments to the Middle East, these forces still fall far short of what would be needed for a successful ground invasion of Iran вЂ” assuming such a thing is even realistically possible.

**China, blockade precedent, logistics, escalation risk**
What worries Beijing most is not just any temporary disruption of Iranian oil supplies, but the precedent of the United States imposing a naval blockade on a country with which China has active and important trade relations.
The US previously did this to Venezuela; Iran is a far more significant partner.
That is why the Chinese Defence Ministry has spoken out strongly against these moves.
Assembling such a force takes time, so it may not be visible for another two weeks or so, but a Chinese naval presence is likely.
Some analysts claim China is at a major disadvantage because it lacks nearby bases for resupply.
I believe this view is mistaken.
In fact, it is the United States that faces the greater logistical challenges in this region.
Its key naval bases in Qatar and especially Bahrain are inside the Persian Gulf and have already been targeted by Iran.
Reaching them would require US ships to transit the Strait of Hormuz under threat of Iranian drones, cruise missiles, and mines вЂ” something the US would prefer to avoid.
In the absence of secure forward bases, the US Navy would likely have to rely on resupply from Diego Garcia, some 4,000вЂ“5,000 kilometres away.
While the US has superior at-sea replenishment capabilities and experience, it does not have easily accessible local bases.
China, by contrast, has access to Pakistani ports (Karachi and others).
The China-Pakistan relationship is extremely close, dating back to the 1960s and highly valued by the Pakistani military.
If Chinese warships requested docking rights, it would be very difficult for Pakistan to refuse.
China can also reach Iran overland via new railway lines (through Pakistan) or road routes through Central Asia.
Given ChinaвЂ™s influence in the region, political refusal of transit would be extremely difficult.
If China decides to send drones, technology, economic goods, or foodstuffs to Iran, it has multiple options вЂ” by sea, rail, air, or truck.
A Chinese naval presence in the Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean would have viable ports for resupply.
The Pentagon is surely aware of these risks and will not want the situation to continue indefinitely.
One can understand why the Chinese are pushing hard to restart talks and end the blockade quickly.

**Russia, Saudi Arabia, Gulf architecture, second-order shifts**
Meanwhile, the Russians continue to warn of further intensification of the war.
Yesterday, Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov held another telephone conversation вЂ” this time with Saudi Foreign Minister Prince Faisal bin Farhan Al Saud.
The Russian readout is revealing.
The ministers exchanged views on the situation in the Strait of Hormuz following the US-Israeli attacks on Iran and TehranвЂ™s responses.
They reaffirmed the importance of an immediate termination of hostilities, which are damaging civilian infrastructure in the Persian Gulf states.
They also expressed support for continued political and diplomatic efforts between the United States and Iran, mediated by Pakistan.
This is somewhat more temperate language than the Russians have used recently when addressing other audiences.
The Russians and Saudis also called for a broader dialogue involving all interested parties to coordinate long-term stability and security in the region based on a balance of interests.
Russia and Saudi Arabia are ready to help organize such a dialogue.
The Russians have been floating the idea of a new security architecture for the Persian Gulf вЂ” one agreed between Iran and the Gulf states, with guarantees from constructive outside parties such as Russia, China, Pakistan, and Turkey.
In this call, they appear to have tested the waters with the Saudis, who responded positively.
The United States will object strongly.
However, time is not on AmericaвЂ™s side.
Recent reports indicate that some Gulf states are already facing fiscal strain due to lost oil revenues and have had to seek funds from high-net-worth individuals and companies.
While there may be diplomatic reasons, fiscal pressures cannot be ruled out.
This conflict, regardless of its immediate outcome, is already radically altering the geopolitical landscape in the Middle East.
If it drags on, diplomatic efforts by China and Russia to reduce US influence in the region could begin to bear fruit.
For the record, I suspect the Americans will eventually agree to another meeting with the Iranians вЂ” the Trump administration is not yet prepared to defy the Gulf states, especially Saudi Arabia, so openly.
However, I doubt any such negotiations would be conducted in good faith with a genuine intent to end the war quickly.
A return to escalation next week вЂ” possibly including a risky ground operation вЂ” seems more likely.
If that fails or bogs down, the prospects for the kind of regional realignment the Russians are suggesting will increase, and the appearance of the Chinese navy in the Arabian Sea could become a very real possibility.

**Ukraine / Europe coda вЂ” strikes, Naryshkin, drones, Sumy**
The underlying cause of these pressures remains the conflict in Ukraine, where the Russians continue to make notable statements.
Sergey Naryshkin, head of RussiaвЂ™s Foreign Intelligence Service (SVR), has provided another assessment.
Russia has repeatedly shown it wants to achieve peace in Ukraine.
Naryshkin highlighted an important nuance: when Russia and Ukraine reach agreement on peace terms (which he referred to as those discussed in Alaska), Europeans will realize they were deceived about RussiaвЂ™s supposed strategic defeat.
If a just peace is reached on those terms, it could trigger a вЂњpolitical tsunamiвЂќ in Europe.
Anchorage (Alaska) is now history, and the Russians know it.
NaryshkinвЂ™s statements are essentially an expression of confidence that Russia will achieve its objectives soon.
Other Russian reports indicate the Ukrainian army is critically short of manpower, with declining quality of recruits and increasing reliance on drones and robots вЂ” as seen in ZelenskyвЂ™s recent claim of recapturing a village without deploying any soldiers.
The Russian Defence Ministry and Dmitry Medvedev have also highlighted that Ukrainian drone production has largely shifted to Europe.
The Defence Ministry published locations of European factories and workshops now producing drones for Ukraine.
Medvedev warned Europeans to be careful, noting that Russia knows where these facilities are and reserves the right to take countermeasures.
In military-industrial terms, EuropeвЂ™s turn to drones represents a confession of weakness.
Earlier in the war, Europe supplied Ukraine with tanks, armored vehicles, air defense systems, and fighter jets вЂ” albeit in dwindling quantities.
Now it has shifted to lighter, cheaper drones.
Much of the recent Western narrative about drones being the вЂњdecisiveвЂќ weapon on the Ukrainian battlefield appears to stem from this production reality and the need to talk up their importance.
On the military front, the key development in the last 24 hours was another large-scale Russian strike on Ukraine involving Geran drones, Kh-101 cruise missiles, and Iskander ballistic missiles.
The Russian Defence Ministry stated that the targets were logistic and energy facilities supporting UkraineвЂ™s war effort.
Kiev, Odesa, and surrounding areas were heavily hit.
Ukrainian claims of intercepting most of the incoming weapons remain implausible, especially given extensive visible damage and lessons from recent Middle Eastern events showing lower effectiveness against ballistic missiles.
There has also been movement on the front lines, particularly in the Sumy region.
The Russians have begun leafleting the city of Sumy with political messages questioning ZelenskyвЂ™s legitimacy.
They are not yet calling for surrender or evacuation, but the leafleting strongly suggests Sumy is becoming a proximate target.
Russian forces appear to be advancing through forested areas north of the city, where they can concentrate troops with some protection from drones.
An intense battle for the city may be imminent, and Ukrainian defences there do not appear well organized.
