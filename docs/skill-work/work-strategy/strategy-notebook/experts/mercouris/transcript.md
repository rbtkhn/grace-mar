Good day. Today is Monday, 20th April 2026.
First, I want to thank everyone again for your kind words about my recent bout of ill health. Today I feel much better — not perhaps fully myself again, as I still get occasional moments of dizziness — but a great deal better. Today is the first day I generally feel essentially myself again, so from today moving forward it will be business as usual as far as videos are concerned.
Today, as it happens, is also the day when, for the umpteenth time, we are told that it is a make-or-break moment for the British Prime Minister, Keir Starmer.
I have touched on the latest twist in the Mandelson scandal in a recent programme. We have also recorded a programme with the former British diplomat Ian Proud, which should be coming out shortly. In it, we go into a forensic discussion of many of the claims made by the Prime Minister and his followers about the scandal. We do not find any of his excuses or explanations convincing.
For those who do not know the background: Keir Starmer insisted on appointing the deeply tainted and widely mistrusted former associate of Tony Blair, Lord Mandelson, as ambassador to Washington, overriding doubts about Mandelson's suitability. He ignored information that Mandelson had been a close associate of Jeffrey Epstein. When the full extent of those connections became impossible to conceal, Starmer — who had backed Mandelson fervently — was obliged to announce his dismissal.
Starmer initially claimed that Mandelson had only been appointed because he had lied about the extent of his connections to Epstein, that Starmer himself had been deceived by those lies, and that Mandelson had somehow managed to work his way through the entire appointment process.
Over the last few weeks, every one of those claims has collapsed. It now appears that Mandelson did lie about his connections, but the Prime Minister had been briefed about the extent of those connections. Moreover, Mandelson failed to pass the necessary security checks conducted by the British authorities — yet he was still appointed ambassador to Washington.
Starmer continues to insist that he was completely unaware that Mandelson had failed his security checks. I find that impossible to believe.
As I discussed in a recent programme, the Prime Minister suffers from an acute case of "blame someone else" syndrome. Whenever something goes wrong, he presents himself as the injured party and blames others for what are clearly his own mistakes.
Today he is supposed to go before the House of Commons and provide some kind of explanation. Many people are calling this a make-or-break moment for him. Perhaps it will be.
My own quick take on this extraordinary affair is that I do not understand why Keir Starmer is still Prime Minister at all.
Already by the summer of 2025 it was clear that Starmer was incapable of running a functional government. He was deeply unpopular with the British people — I believe the most unpopular Prime Minister since opinion polls began. Even before the Mandelson affair, it was obvious to me that this man is simply not up to the job of being Prime Minister.
He has only one issue that genuinely interests him: the war in Ukraine, of which he remains a fervid, obsessive supporter of both Ukraine and President Zelensky. He is not properly engaged in any other issue.
This was already obvious to me by the autumn. Then the Mandelson affair developed, and in February we saw revelations that looked absolutely damning of Starmer's conduct. The decision to appoint Mandelson was Starmer's and no one else's. If Mandelson has proved a disaster, the ultimate responsibility is his.
Yet despite all his excuses collapsing into dust, as of this morning he continues to be Prime Minister.
There are explanations for this: the lack of capable alternatives in the Labour Party, the extreme factionalism of British politics, and the unwillingness to open political space for rival parties such as Reform and the Greens. But ultimately, the fact that someone with this record continues to occupy the office points to one thing: the extreme degradation and essential collapse of the political centre in Britain.
The British political class has lost contact with the pulse of life in the country. It no longer has much understanding of or connection to the realities that ordinary people face every day. It continues to pursue its own policies and interests — among which the war in Ukraine takes a very high priority.
The result is that someone like Keir Starmer can become Prime Minister and then remain Prime Minister even as everything he touches turns to dust and all his excuses collapse.
It is difficult for me to discuss this, as I am talking about my own country. I apologise for bringing it up at the start of today's programme, but I feel I have to say it. Given that Starmer has survived so much and is still Prime Minister, I have ceased to assume — or perhaps the correct word is "hope" — that any single event will be the final breaking point.
I no longer assume he will face real judgment when he appears before the House of Commons today, or that he will be ousted over the next couple of days or weeks. If it hasn't happened now, what reason is there to think it will ever happen?
The political system in Britain is no longer functioning in the way that it should.
Let us now turn to the two great wars that are taking place: the conflict in the Persian Gulf, which is now clearly in a process of escalation, and the war in Ukraine, which, based on recent Russian statements, might be in the process of expansion beyond the territory of Ukraine itself.
At the centre of both crises is the President of the United States. He remains the key player in both wars.
I begin with a deeply troubling article in the Wall Street Journal. The paper claims that during the complex operation to rescue the navigator of an F-15 shot down by the Iranians near Isfahan — an operation involving large numbers of aircraft and special forces troops, in which the United States lost more aircraft in a single day than in any other combat operation since the end of the Vietnam War — Pentagon officials decided that President Trump should be excluded from the operations room.
The reason given is that his decisions and approach are so eccentric and erratic that the US military could not be confident he would not give instructions that made no sense and might throw the entire operation into doubt.
I find this extraordinary. I have never heard of anything like this in the modern history of the United States. It amounts to a vote of no confidence in the operational judgment of the President, who is their Commander-in-Chief. Remarkably, it appears the President himself accepted this decision and did not insist on participating.
I am not discussing here whether the operation was solely a rescue mission or whether it had other objectives, such as attempting to seize Iran's enriched uranium stockpile. I have expressed doubts about the latter theory in earlier programmes. What I want to focus on is this lack of confidence by the US military in the man who is, constitutionally, their Commander-in-Chief.
From the very origins of the American Republic, the President has been Commander-in-Chief. George Washington was a soldier who had served in that role during the War of Independence. Abraham Lincoln, a civilian, played an active and generally positive role in decision-making during the Civil War.
If the President is not trusted by the military to fulfil that function during an operation of this kind, what does that tell us about his ability to fulfil his role as President? That is a question Americans should be asking. So far I have seen no pushback from the administration against this claim.
We are in the middle of two major crises. In the Persian Gulf there is now an open-ended direct conflict between Iran and the United States. Iran has taken control of the Strait of Hormuz. The United States has mounted its own sea blockade. There are rumours that the Chinese Navy may start moving forces into the region, and we might see Chinese warships appear this summer.
If China deploys warships to break the American blockade of Iran, that would be a direct confrontation between the two superpowers.
At the same time, the Russians have issued statements at a level we have not heard since February 2022. The Russian Defence Ministry has listed drone production facilities across Europe that are assisting Ukraine in its drone offensive against Russia. Some are already calling this a list of potential targets.
Aiding attacks on the territory of another country is an act of war. One would have thought these warnings alone would be ringing alarm bells.
There have also been Western attempts to seize Russian cargoes and shadow fleet tankers on the high seas. The Russian Navy is now providing escorts for merchant ships trading with Russia. A direct confrontation between Western and Russian navies on the high seas is possible if hardline European countries try to enforce their own ineffective naval blockade.
Ukrainian drone attacks have struck northwestern Russia, including St. Petersburg and the Kola Peninsula, with some drones overflying NATO territory (the Baltic States and Finland). The Russians have given clear warnings that they are prepared to take counter-action.
Sergei Shoigu, Secretary of Russia's Security Council (Russia's highest decision-making body and Putin's national security advisor), and Foreign Minister Lavrov have both spoken of red lines. Lavrov has said Russia will enforce them. The Russian parliament has passed a law authorising Putin to order direct military action where Russians or people of Russian nationality are discriminated against or threatened — something the Russians claim is happening in the Baltic States.
We now potentially have a second crisis analogous to the Cuban Missile Crisis.
One reason we got through the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis is that John F. Kennedy and Nikita Khrushchev were individuals in full possession of their faculties, commanding authority and control in their countries, and able to impose their decisions on their militaries.
Today we have a President of the United States who, according to the Wall Street Journal story, is not fully trusted by his own military and does not have the same authority. If we find ourselves towards the end of the summer facing major crises in northwest Europe or the Persian Gulf/Arabian Sea — or both at once — with a President whom parts of the US military apparently do not think is fully up to the job, that is deeply concerning.
With all that said, let us turn to the actual events of the last few hours in these two conflicts.
First, the conflict between the United States and Iran.
There has been an extraordinary seesaw of events: moments of apparent escalation and collapse of negotiations, followed by indications that some people in Washington are looking for a way out.
The United States refused to lift its sea blockade of Iran even as, following the ceasefire in Lebanon, Iran indicated it might loosen its control of the Strait of Hormuz. The Iranians called this unacceptable and tightened their control again.
Then there were reports that an Iranian-flagged or Iranian-owned ship carrying commercial cargo had transited the Strait, defying the American blockade. The US Navy intercepted it. The USS Spruance, an Aegis-class destroyer, warned the captain to stop. The crew was ordered to vacate the engine room. The ship was fired upon, the engine room was damaged, the ship was boarded, and the President bragged about it.
The Iranians threatened retaliation. Iranian media were full of stories about drones being readied to attack American warships in the Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean.
This moment of escalation occurred even as reports circulated that the Chinese Defence Ministry had stated China would act to protect its trade with Iran, possibly including military intervention.
Then came further developments. The United States said it would send a follow-up delegation to Islamabad today to meet the Iranians. However, Vice President JD Vance, who led the previous session, would not be leading this time. Some reports suggested Jared Kushner — the President's son-in-law, who is not even an official of the US government and is almost certainly unacceptable to the Iranians — might be involved.
The Iranians said there was no point in negotiations while the sea blockade remained in place and they would not send a team to Islamabad.
Even as this happened, there was a flurry of Western media reports claiming divisions within the Iranian leadership, with hardliners reprimanding the Foreign Ministry and its minister, Abbas Araghchi. There was speculation that these divisions meant the United States had no one to negotiate with — an astounding claim given recent history.
All of this began to subside after Field Marshal Asim Munir, the powerful chief of the Pakistani military, publicly complained that the American sea blockade is the cause of the trouble and that the Americans should lift it so negotiations can resume.
The Russian news agency TASS reported that as many as 40 ships transited the Strait of Hormuz over the weekend, ignoring the American blockade. The blockade has only been enforced in the single case of the attack on this one ship. According to TASS, more shipping — presumably mostly from Iran to China — passed through the Strait over the weekend than at any point since the conflict began on 28 February.
The American blockade, as Larry Johnson and others have pointed out, has turned out to be something of a fiction.
The Americans are the ones facing logistical difficulties. Their closest naval base is Diego Garcia, 4,000 kilometres away. Reports have emerged of American servicemen on the warships receiving less and lower-quality food, with supplies short and nerves fraying due to the extended time at sea.
The Chinese, by contrast, can rest and refit in Pakistani ports.
The United States can probably maintain a presence if it becomes a strategic priority, but it will be a heavy drain on resources and will overstretch the Navy.
Just before I started this programme, reports emerged that the United States is now thinking about lifting the sea blockade.
If that happens, we are back where we were on Wednesday when the truce in Lebanon was announced and it looked as if the Iranians would loosen their grip on the Strait, with follow-up negotiations toward a general settlement.
There are also reports that JD Vance is going to Islamabad after all and is likely to lead the negotiating team again. If true, this is a major concession by the President. More and more people will conclude that he is in retreat: he failed to knock out Iran quickly, failed to achieve regime change, failed to bluff the Iranians in negotiations, and failed to break them with the sea blockade. He is now having to row back, lift the blockade, honour and perhaps extend the ceasefire beyond Tuesday, and sit down with the Iranians (still led by Ghalibaf and Araghchi) to negotiate a way out.
Such negotiations could take months, with no guarantee that shipping will transit the Strait of Hormuz freely as it did before February.
In my opinion, the next 48 hours are critical.
If the sea blockade is lifted and an American negotiating team led by JD Vance goes to Islamabad, there is a chance — I put it no higher than that — that we might move toward a negotiated resolution.
If none of these things happen, we will likely see major escalation after Tuesday, with extremely dangerous and uncertain outcomes.
The next 48 hours will decide. Either the President accepts that he is out of cards to play and that continuously raising the stakes risks strategic bankruptcy and failure — and pulls back — or we will see escalation that will be incredibly difficult to sustain. The Saudis are losing confidence, the Pakistanis are exasperated, and the Chinese are starting to move.
If escalation occurs, what is already a very bad situation will tip over into strategic disaster.
The President finds himself in zugzwang. He needs to retreat, though there will be a heavy political price. The alternative is much worse.
I have no confidence that he will do so for any sustained period, but that is the choice he faces today: political misfortune or geopolitical catastrophe. The choice should be clear, but then I am not the President of the United States — thank goodness — and it is not my decision to make.
Now to the conflict in Ukraine.
There is growing recognition in Europe of the manpower crisis Ukraine is facing. There has been huge denial about this, with endless claims that Ukrainians are killing ten Russians for every Ukrainian soldier lost. I have explained in many programmes why I am certain that is wrong. In fact, the Ukrainians are suffering far more casualties than the Russians.
Europeans are now finally facing the reality that Ukrainian losses are unsustainable. The result is a concerted — and I would say illegal — attempt across Europe to coerce Ukrainian men to return to Ukraine so that Zelensky's officials can round them up and send them to the front.
Forcing men to return to a country at war to be enlisted in this way almost certainly violates various international conventions.
It is becoming obvious that there are simply not enough Ukrainian infantry on the front lines to hold positions. Suddenly the mood has changed and there is pressure in many European countries to force Ukrainian men back.
One of the most troubling aspects of this war is the extraordinary cynicism of European governments. They happily disregard their own laws while obsessively trying to keep the war going as long as possible. Sending Ukrainian men back to die on the battlefield is a very strange way of "saving" them — which was supposedly the entire purpose of standing by Ukraine "for as long as it takes."
If you really want to save Ukrainian lives, you pick up the telephone, call the Russians and Ukrainians, and say the time has come to talk. You tell the Ukrainians that fundamental concessions need to be made. You ask the Russians what their terms are for ending the war.
Instead, the Belgian Defence Minister recently said — even as the Belgian Prime Minister has spoken of reopening contacts with Russia — that Europe fully expects to be in an indefinite armed confrontation with Russia. He said Europe needs Ukraine to go on fighting, bleeding and dying for another four years so that Europe can rearm and re-equip itself.
This is appalling cynicism. Ukraine is being sacrificed not as part of any real plan, but to lend credence to a mirage. Europe is never going to rearm to the level where it can take on Russia — especially against the backdrop of European deindustrialisation.
My own view is that none of these reasons for prolonging the war have any real basis. European leaders simply have a visceral loathing of Russia and will continue to hurt the Russians in whatever way they can, even at the risk of major crisis, by allowing drone production on their territory and drones to fly across NATO airspace to strike Russia.
Meanwhile, on the battlefronts, the story is one of Russian advances on every part of the battlefield. There is nowhere one can speak of a successful Ukrainian counter-attack any longer.
In Kupyansk, which the Ukrainians claimed to have recovered, we are now seeing more and more drone and visual footage of Russian soldiers back in southwestern and central Kupyansk — places fought over last autumn. Whether the Ukrainian counter-attack was real or largely public relations, it is now reversing.
The same is true in the Zaporizhzhia region. A well-informed Russian newspaper has confirmed there was a Ukrainian counter-attack from February through March, but now that the spring thaw has ended and the ground has hardened, the Russians are clearing the Ukrainians out again.
From Zelensky's perspective, these operations were extremely successful as presentation management. By talking them up and staging stunts outside Kupyansk, he changed the narrative away from the fall of Pokrovsk, Mirnograd, Sieversk and the grinding battle for Kostiantynivka (which now seems to be coming to an end). The Western media instead talked about "successful Ukrainian counter-attacks."
In reality, things continue to go from bad to worse. Kostiantynivka appears to be falling rapidly. There is clear evidence that the Russians are advancing into Dnipropetrovsk region, with some claims they are aiming for Pavlohrad.
The most dramatic news is coming from the north, from Sumy itself. Ukrainian reports and Russian advances suggest the city — the biggest city close to the Russian border — is now in the process of being encircled by Russian forces "North." The Russians have also destroyed the Chernihiv power station, which provided electric power for much of northeastern Ukraine.
The Sumy operation has come as something of a surprise to me, but it is starting to look as if the Russians plan to capture the city at some point this year, probably this summer. The fall of Sumy, a regional capital, would be a dramatic event that Zelensky and his Western backers could not ignore.
Nothing — not rounding up men in Europe — is going to change the strategic picture. It might slow the Russians for a few more months at the price of more dead Ukrainians, but it cannot change the direction of the war.
The escalation of using drones to attack targets inside Russia, crossing NATO airspace, is — by any objective analysis of what it achieves versus the risks — an escalation too far.
That is where I will finish today's programme.
You can find all our programmes on Locals, Rumble, X, and Substack. Please support our work via Patreon, SubscribeStar, and the shop links under this video.
If you liked this video, please hit the like button and make sure you are subscribed.
That's me for today. More from me soon. Have a very good day.
Thank you.

**Pakistan–Iran bridge, escorts, and Islamabad II**

Over the last 24 hours, the fragile ceasefires in the conflicts between the United States and Iran, and between Israel and Iran, remain in effect.

There is also an apparent ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon, agreed through discussions between the Israelis and the Lebanese government (in which Hezbollah has a significant role).

At present, the main news concerning this conflict is the arrival in Tehran of the Chief of the Army Staff of the Pakistani armed forces, Field Marshal Asim Munir.

He flew in from Pakistan and, as far as I am aware, is the first high-level foreign visitor from a government to arrive in Iran openly since hostilities began on 28 February.

There may have been covert visits by officials from countries such as China or Russia, but Field Marshal Munir arrived publicly.

His aircraft was escorted by Iranian fighter jets — MiG-29s and F-4 Phantoms — as it landed in Tehran.

President Trump has repeatedly claimed that the entire Iranian air force has been destroyed.

Clearly, that is not the case.

Iran still has operational fighter jets and pilots capable of escorting foreign aircraft.

To be clear, Iran’s air force is a small factor in this conflict and cannot confront the far superior air forces of the United States and Israel head-on.

Iran’s primary defensive capability in the skies remains its surface-to-air missiles and integrated air defence system, which has shown it can still inflict damage, as seen in recent events near Isfahan.

Nevertheless, the appearance of these Iranian fighters disproves claims of total destruction.

Similar claims were made about Iran’s navy and about Russian-supplied Mi-28 helicopter gunships; yet at least some of the latter have been seen flying over Tehran in recent days.

Many claims made during this war have turned out to be overstated.

Both sides had expected the discussions to proceed.

The Pakistanis are clearly keen to restart the process and have been pressing for an extension of the current two-week ceasefire, which expires next Tuesday.

Yesterday, Western media (especially in Britain) briefly reported that an agreement had been reached to extend the ceasefire by another two weeks.

However, both the United States and Iran quickly denied any such deal.

President Trump has expressed little interest in extending it.

As things stand, the ceasefire appears set to end on Tuesday, with the Russians warning that fighting could then resume at an even more intense level.

The Pakistanis are doing what they can, and the Iranians say they want to work with them to restart direct talks with the Americans.

Iran is careful not to antagonize Pakistan, which coordinates closely with its main ally, China — a country Iran is eager to keep on good terms with.

For the moment, however, the Pakistanis seem to be having more success with the Iranians than with the Americans.

My overall sense is that the mood in Washington has shifted back toward greater confrontation.

**U.S. theory of victory, blockade limits, next escalation**

I have no expectation that any of this will happen.

Iran is a huge country with significant natural resources and industrial capacity.
