# Hormuz Reciprocal Transit De-escalation Framework  
## v2.1 WORK draft

**HRTF** = this **Hormuz Reciprocal Transit De-escalation Framework**.  
**HSTA** = proposed **Hormuz Strait Transit Authority**.  
This is a **WORK-layer negotiation scaffold** only. It is not Record, not treaty text, and not legal advice.

## 1. Purpose

Participating states seek to unwind the current **dual maritime coercion** near the Strait of Hormuz:

- Iran’s asserted control, tolling, exemption, seizure, harassment, or denial practices over traffic near the Strait.
- U.S. and participating enforcement-partner blockade, interdiction, seizure, diversion, or denial practices against Iran-linked maritime commerce.

Each side now treats its maritime tool as remaining leverage.  
Unilateral relaxation risks domestic or international framing as defeat.  
Indefinite continuation risks energy shock, insurance disruption, shipping uncertainty, food and fertilizer price instability, renewed military escalation, and regime-change waiting games.

The HRTF creates a **synchronized, observable, reciprocal exit ramp** from this trap without settling underlying sovereignty, sanctions, nuclear, self-defense, or legal disputes.

## 2. No-Prejudice Principle

Nothing in the HRTF shall be construed as recognition, waiver, admission, concession, or prejudgment of any party’s claims regarding:

- sovereignty;
- maritime jurisdiction;
- blockade legality;
- sanctions legality;
- tolling rights;
- belligerent rights;
- self-defense;
- nuclear-related rights or obligations;
- prior military actions;
- prior seizures or interdictions;
- compensation claims;
- or future diplomatic positions.

Participation is a practical de-escalation step, not a political or legal concession.

## 3. Hormuz Strait Transit Authority

Parties establish, or provisionally authorize, a **Hormuz Strait Transit Authority** (**HSTA**) with a lightweight multilateral secretariat hosted by a neutral or regional state, such as **Oman**, **Pakistan**, **Qatar**, or another mutually agreed host.

Initial HSTA functions are limited to:

- provisional vessel and cargo registration;
- publication of anonymized weekly transit statistics;
- recording of toll, fee, escrow, or compensation arrangements;
- emergency consultation facilitation within a 6–24 hour window;
- complaint logging;
- referral of unresolved disputes to existing national, regional, IMO, UN, or other agreed fora.

The HSTA does **not** replace national authorities, the UN Security Council, the International Maritime Organization, or separate nuclear, sanctions, regional-security, or military deconfliction tracks.

The HSTA is an administrative confidence mechanism, not a sovereignty court.

## 4. Participants and Status Categories

### Core parties

Initial core parties may include:

- Iran;
- the United States;
- directly affected Gulf littoral states;
- and any state operating participating enforcement assets or directly affected transit infrastructure.

### Facilitators

Facilitators may include:

- Oman;
- Pakistan;
- Qatar;
- or another mutually accepted diplomatic channel.

Facilitators provide venue, message-passing, consultation support, and procedural continuity. They do not assume liability for compliance unless separately agreed.

### Political observer-guarantors

Political observer-guarantors may include, by agreement:

- China;
- Russia;
- India;
- the European Union;
- or other states with material leverage, transit interests, or diplomatic credibility with the parties.

Political observer-guarantor status does not imply endorsement of any party’s legal claims. It indicates willingness to observe, encourage compliance, relay concerns, and support de-escalation.

### Technical observers

Technical observers may include:

- UN Secretariat representatives;
- IMO representatives;
- port authorities;
- shipping registries;
- insurers;
- classification societies;
- energy-market or fertilizer-market technical bodies;
- and other non-political technical entities approved by the parties.

Technical observers provide expertise, data integrity, and procedural credibility. They are not guarantors.

## 5. Covered Categories

The initial phase applies only to civilian, commercially registered, non-offensive cargoes, including:

- crude oil;
- liquefied natural gas;
- refined fuels;
- fertilizer inputs;
- petrochemicals;
- food;
- medicine;
- humanitarian goods;
- emergency maritime safety traffic;
- civilian industrial goods;
- and other agreed cargo classes.

Military cargo, weapons cargo, dual-use cargo, specially restricted cargo, or cargo under unresolved legal/compliance restriction may be excluded from the initial phase or handled through separate annexes.

Covered status does not immunize a vessel from lawful inspection or emergency self-defense claims, but it creates a procedural presumption against discretionary interdiction, denial, seizure, harassment, or escalation.

## 6. Data Protection and Registration Integrity

Registration data shall be used only for clearance, verification, emergency consultation, dispute logging, and anonymized statistical reporting.

The HSTA shall not publish:

- precise routing data;
- military-sensitive information;
- commercially sensitive cargo details;
- vessel vulnerability information;
- crew identities;
- or information likely to facilitate targeting.

Registration submissions may include:

- vessel flag;
- owner/operator;
- insurer or protection-and-indemnity coverage;
- general cargo class;
- declared origin and destination ports;
- declared restrictions relevant to clearance under participating-state rules;
- emergency contact;
- proposed transit window stated in general, non-targeting terms;
- and any additional information required by annex.

False declaration, material concealment, or misuse of registration may suspend a vessel’s HSTA-covered status.

## 7. Reciprocal Implementation Sequence

Implementation uses **observable hooks** rather than trust.  
No unilateral disarmament is required.  
No party is required to concede legality, illegality, defeat, success, sovereignty, or jurisdiction as a condition of participation.

### Phase 0 — Announcement, Freeze, and No-Surprise Undertaking  
**Timing:** Day 0

Parties issue a joint, parallel, or coordinated announcement of the HRTF.

Parties freeze expansion of maritime coercive measures, including:

- no new blanket exclusion zones;
- no new blanket interdiction categories;
- no escalation of discretionary tolls;
- no expansion of seizure practices;
- no new public ultimata tied to registered civilian traffic;
- no use of framework talks, registration data, facilitation channels, or consultation windows as cover for surprise military action.

No party is required at this stage to terminate existing leverage or admit failure of prior policy.

### Phase 1 — Provisional Registration  
**Timing:** Days 1–7

The HSTA opens provisional registration for covered civilian vessels and cargoes.

Registration creates the procedural hook for later reciprocal action.  
Registration does not itself guarantee clearance.

Parties may reject registration only for stated procedural, cargo-category, safety, or compliance reasons.

### Phase 2 — Provisional Exemptions  
**Timing:** Days 7–14

Iran publishes provisional categories exempt from discretionary denial, seizure, harassment, or toll escalation.

The United States and any participating enforcement partners publish provisional categories exempt from new interdiction, seizure, diversion, or blockade-enforcement action.

Initial emphasis should be on:

- food;
- medicine;
- humanitarian cargo;
- emergency maritime safety traffic;
- fertilizer inputs;
- third-party civilian energy cargoes;
- and other systemic civilian goods agreed by the parties.

### Phase 3 — Reciprocal Pause  
**Timing:** Day 14 onward, once agreed activation threshold is reached

Once registration reaches an agreed activation threshold—such as a minimum number of vessels, cargo tonnage, or representative cargo categories sufficient to test the mechanism—the reciprocal pause begins.

For HSTA-registered vessels in covered categories:

- Iran suspends discretionary toll escalation, harassment, denial, or seizure.
- The United States and participating enforcement partners suspend interdiction, diversion, seizure, or blockade-enforcement action.
- Gulf states and port authorities treat HSTA clearance as a positive confidence signal, not a final legal determination.
- Technical observers may verify anonymized compliance patterns.

The activation threshold shall be defined by annex and should not be set so high that Phase 3 becomes unreachable.

### Phase 4 — Escrow, Fee, and Claims Mechanism  
**Timing:** Parallel with Phases 2–3

Disputed tolls, transit fees, insurance surcharges, compensation claims, or security-cost claims may be placed into HSTA-recorded escrow.

Escrow prevents payment disputes from becoming immediate sovereignty disputes.

Escrow treatment may later be resolved by:

- negotiated revenue allocation;
- compensation agreement;
- humanitarian allocation;
- maritime security cost-sharing;
- legal review;
- or mutually agreed release.

Placement into escrow does not concede the validity or invalidity of any underlying claim.

### Phase 5 — Weekly Transparency  
**Timing:** Ongoing

The HSTA publishes weekly anonymized data, including:

- number of registered vessels;
- number cleared;
- number delayed;
- number denied;
- number interdicted;
- number disputed;
- general cargo categories;
- average delay windows;
- number of emergency consultations requested;
- number of complaints logged;
- number of unresolved cases.

The HSTA shall not publish sensitive routing, military, commercial, crew, or targeting-relevant data.

### Phase 6 — Consultation Before Coercive Enforcement  
**Timing:** Core safeguard; ongoing

Before any coercive action against an HSTA-registered vessel, the enforcing party shall request emergency consultation through the HSTA unless immediate self-defense is claimed.

Emergency consultation should occur within a fixed 6–24 hour period.

If immediate self-defense is claimed, the acting party shall file a post-action notice to the HSTA within a fixed period.

Failure to request consultation or file post-action notice creates a reportable compliance concern but does not, by itself, resolve the legality of the action.

### Phase 7 — Controlled Expansion  
**Timing:** After 30–60 day confidence period

If Phases 1–6 hold during the initial confidence period, parties may expand covered categories to include broader civilian commerce.

Expansion may include:

- additional energy cargoes;
- petrochemicals;
- civilian industrial goods;
- shipping services;
- insurance services;
- port services;
- designated financial-clearing channels;
- and other categories agreed by annex.

Expansion should occur only where weekly transparency data shows measurable reduction in delay, interdiction, seizure, harassment, or escalation.

### Phase 8 — Linkage Review Without Merger  
**Timing:** 6-month and 12-month reviews

Parties may review whether the transit framework should cross-reference separate nuclear, sanctions, or regional-security talks.

Such cross-reference shall not merge the tracks unless expressly agreed.

The Hormuz track remains a maritime transit de-escalation track.  
The nuclear/sanctions track remains separate.  
The regional-security track remains separate.  
No party may condition ordinary civilian maritime transit on total resolution of all other disputes unless it expressly rejects the HRTF.

## 8. Anti-Drift and Non-Escalation Guardrails

The HRTF shall not be used as an indefinite holding pattern for regime change, permanent blockade, permanent toll escalation, permanent interdiction, or permanent coercive leverage.

If measurable maritime de-escalation does not occur within the first political review period, parties shall openly choose one of the following:

1. revise the mechanism;
2. suspend the mechanism;
3. transfer the issue to another forum;
4. narrow the covered categories;
5. or terminate participation.

During the initial term, parties should refrain from:

- attacking civilian energy infrastructure;
- attacking desalination infrastructure;
- attacking civilian port infrastructure;
- attacking registered civilian vessels;
- using HSTA data for targeting;
- targeting negotiators, facilitators, technical observers, HSTA personnel, or registered emergency contacts;
- using talks as cover for surprise attack;
- expanding the maritime crisis into unrelated theaters.

Self-defense rights are preserved, but self-defense claims should be noticed through Phase 6 procedures where feasible.

## 9. Review Cadence

The initial term shall be fixed, not indefinite.

Suggested cadence:

- emergency review: 14 days;
- operational review: 30 days;
- political review: 60 days;
- renewal review: 6 months;
- full strategic review: 12 months.

Renewal requires affirmative agreement.

Silence, delay, or procedural continuation shall not be treated as consent to indefinite blockade, indefinite toll escalation, indefinite interdiction, or indefinite coercive leverage.

## 10. Entry Into Force

Entry into force requires:

- provisional political acceptance by initial core parties;
- HSTA host or secretariat designation;
- publication of initial covered categories;
- provisional vessel-registration rules;
- emergency consultation contacts;
- initial no-prejudice declarations;
- initial data-protection rules;
- initial transparency format.

Annexes remain TBD, including:

- maps;
- transit corridors;
- activation threshold;
- cargo-category definitions;
- escrow rules;
- toll/fee handling;
- compensation procedures;
- inspection procedures;
- sanctions-interface language;
- insurance protocols;
- observer rights;
- complaint escalation;
- emergency consultation format;
- and termination procedures.

No party is bound by this v2.1 WORK draft.

## Sequence logic

The v2.1 sequence follows a simple crisis-exit ladder:

```text
Freeze escalation
→ create registration hooks
→ publish limited exemptions
→ activate reciprocal pause
→ escrow disputed money claims
→ report anonymized compliance data
→ require consultation before coercion
→ expand only after performance
→ review linkage without merging tracks
→ prevent indefinite drift
```
