## Principal points of divergence and proposed compromises (top three flashpoints)

The following outlines principal divergences on **publicly reported** lines, with **interim** compromise mechanisms intended to **stabilize** talks without requiring either side to abandon its **legal or strategic baseline** at the outset. **A kinetic pause is not yet a durable settlement**—reporting remains fluid and, in places, contradictory—so the stress here is **verifiable staging**, not rhetorical closure. Where **public** ten-point language and any **condensed working text** diverge, **table discipline** should track the instrument the parties actually negotiate.

### 1. Lebanon and the resistance axis

Iran maintains the ceasefire must cover Lebanon and associated resistance actors as **inseparable** from the agreement. The United States and Israel have treated Lebanon as **outside** the present ceasefire framework even as Israeli military operations there continue—surfacing **proxy-attribution** and **UN Charter** questions (**Articles 2(4) and 51** on threat or use of force and self-defense) that **hybrid** pressure can outlast a paper ceasefire.

**Proposed compromise:** Establish a parallel, time-bound de-escalation mechanism alongside the main negotiations. Iran commits to restraining associated armed actors from attacks from or through Lebanese territory. Israel commits to suspending offensive air and missile strikes in Lebanon except where **immediate** self-defense under **Article 51** is credibly invoked. The United States acts as mediator and compliance guarantor for the Israeli track. A neutral verification group, Pakistan co-chairing with one mutually acceptable third party, issues **weekly** compliance reports. Alleged violations trigger a graduated response: verification, consultation within 24 hours, public attribution if unresolved, **referral to the main channel** rather than automatic termination of the broader talks.

### 2. Uranium enrichment

Iran has publicly insisted on preserving enrichment on its territory within a civilian program. The United States maintains **no** enrichment on Iranian soil—a tension the **NPT** (Articles II and IV), **IAEA safeguards**, and prior **UNSC 2231**–era verification architecture were designed to reconcile when politics permit.

**Proposed compromise:** Iran transfers existing enriched uranium to a U.S.-backed international consortium at agreed valuation and **recommits** to **NPT** compliance. Iran accepts **IAEA** inspections of declared and additional agreed sites, durable monitoring of its civilian program, and a **low** civilian enrichment cap under continuous verification—**JCPOA-shaped** in rigor if not in political lineage. In exchange, the United States acknowledges Iran’s **Article IV** peaceful-use rights under the **NPT**, with **snapback** sanctions only on **verified** breach.

### 3. Strait of Hormuz

Both sides affirm **safe, monitored, non-discriminatory** passage—consistent with **UNCLOS Part III** transit-passage norms and **IMO**-compatible safety rules—yet **on-water** conditions remain disputed; **throughput and queue metrics** from **independent** maritime reporting should ground any claim of “open” or “closed.”

**Proposed compromise:** Establish a multilateral maritime coordination body (Iran, Oman, the United States, neutral facilitator), **without prejudice** to sovereignty and transit-law arguments. **Littoral** Iran and Oman jointly administer traffic coordination equitably. A **three-year** moratorium on transit tolls or fees supports recovery. **Designated** lanes stay predictable and non-discriminatory, verified by **weekly** throughput and queue data **from two independent sources** where possible. Disputes: rapid consultation and public reporting; **incident response** and **non-discriminatory** safety regulation remain explicit to avoid **chokepoint** leverage masquerading as “traffic management.”
