## 2. Neutral spine

**Timeline / facts**

- **Achaemenid:** Imperial integration with local faces retained; Cyrus Cylinder–style restoration rhetoric; Royal Road and satrap extraction; collapse after Alexander decapitates Darius III at Gaugamela — elite loyalty tied to person as well as system.
- **Parthian:** Long Roman frontier; decentralized Arsacid structure; Carrhae (53 BC) shows mobility and denial defeating Roman mass on chosen ground; neither side permanently subdues the other — parity as mutual exhaustion.
- **Sassanid:** Centralized state religion and law; heavy Byzantine wars; Shapur–Valerian episode as civilizational “validation”; fall when exhausted before Arab expansion — brittleness under decapitation vs feudal resilience.

**Patterns:** tolerance → parity → compression across regimes; endurance and doctrine densification after shock.

**Open question for voices:** Is Persian strength primarily *recognition-based legitimacy* (who may narrate sovereignty), *structural balance* with peers, or *material enforceability* of endurance under siege?

## 3. CIV-MIND passes (M → Me → B)

### Mercouris — legitimacy and narrative grammar

Persia’s civilizational story is told through inscription, epic, and martyrology more than through abstract law. Sovereignty is performed: the king as steward of cosmic or moral order (Zoroastrian *asha*; later Shi’a Karbala). Who may speak for the polity shifts across regimes, but the *grammar* repeats — restoration after humiliation, endurance as proof. The tension between Cyrus-as-liberator and Shapur-as-humiliator is not a bug; it is competing legitimacies within one civilizational brand.

### Mearsheimer — power and incentives

Rome and Persia locked into a long equilibrium: neither could cost-effectively eliminate the other on the frontier. Incentives favored denial, raiding, and occasional prestige victories over permanent conquest. When one empire overextended against the other (Sassanid exhaustion vs Byzantium), the *relative* balance shifted for third parties — here the Arab expansion — not because “values” failed but because capacity margins vanished. Parthian decentralization spread risk; Sassanid centralization concentrated it.

### Barnes — liability, mechanism, and clocks

Trace who pays: tributary flows to Persepolis; Roman lives and captives at Carrhae; attrition in decades of Roman–Persian war. “40,000 infantry” and body counts are speaker claims until tied to sources — strategic case uses *mechanism* (terrain, tempo, logistics), not ledger precision. Enforceability: satrapies and clan levies vs standing imperial armies change who bears collapse when the center fails.

## 4. Tension (unresolved)

**Legitimacy** wants continuity as moral or civilizational story (Mercouris). **Structure** says peer rivalry and exhaustion set the bargaining space regardless of that story (Mearsheimer). **Material clocks** — treasury, manpower, frontier attrition — can run out before the narrative resolves who was “right” (Barnes). Do not merge these into one closing sentence in public prose; let **Contradictions** and **proximate vs structural** lines carry the seam (as in the published chapter).
