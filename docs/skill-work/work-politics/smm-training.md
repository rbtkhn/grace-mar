## US politics — minimum viable context (no prior knowledge assumed)

### What “Congress” is (federal)

The **United States Congress** is the national legislature. It has two chambers:

| Chamber | What it is | Rough size |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **House of Representatives** | Elected from **local districts** (one member per district). Terms are **two years**. | 435 voting members |
| **Senate** | Two senators per **state** (not district-based the same way). Terms are **six years**. | 100 members |

**Thomas Massie** and **Ro Khanna** are both members of the **House** — they are **U.S. Representatives**, not senators.

### President vs Congress (why “war powers” comes up)

The **President** runs the executive branch and is **commander-in-chief** of the military. **Congress** is supposed to **declare war** and **authorize** major uses of force. When people argue about **war powers**, they are arguing about **whether Congress must vote** before or while the US uses military force — not about liking or disliking troops.

### Elections you will hear about

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Primary** | Party voters choose who runs under that party’s label in the general election. Massie’s **2026 Republican primary** (KY-4) is a major storyline. |
| **General election** | The final contest (e.g. November in federal election years) between nominees (and sometimes independents). |
| **Incumbent** | The person already in the office running again. |

### “Republican” vs “Democrat” (simple)

- **Republican (“R”)** and **Democrat (“D”)** are the two major parties. They disagree on a lot.  
- **They can still work together on specific bills** (e.g. war-powers resolutions). Cross-party sponsorship does **not** mean Massie “became a Democrat” or Khanna “became a Republican.”

- Does **not** do overt advice-giving ("here's how you could do better") — more "this is wrong" or "this isn't X."

## Calibration exercise: narrative–action alignment

**Frame:** Narrative and action need to align. When they don't, you can't offramp — you can't credibly de-escalate, pivot, or negotiate. Trump is cited as misaligned; thus failure to offramp.

**Goal:** Advise Trump (conciliatory tone) with persuasive talking points. Use Massie's voice.

- Overt advice-giving — Massie states principle, lets implication land.
- Too conciliatory — Massie is declarative, principle-first.

### Revised options (voice-aligned)

| Option | Draft |
|--------|-------|
| **A** | You can't offramp when your narrative and your actions don't match. Americans want coherence. Give them one clear story and one clear path—then the exit opens. |
| **B** | "America First" means your words and your actions have to line up. When they don't, you can't credibly de-escalate. Align them, and the offramp is there. |
| **C** | You can't pivot when your story and your moves contradict each other. Coherence isn't weakness. It's the only way to offramp. |

**Lesson:** Match his declarative, principle + consequence structure. Avoid overt advice; state the principle and let the implication land. Use his vocabulary (America First) when reframing.

## Tactics and strategies

### Strategy 1: Elevate, don't attack

**Principle:** The best counter to adversaries is a stronger principal. Post about Massie's record, votes, principles, and allies more than you post about opponents.

**Tactics:**
- Lead with Massie's floor speeches, amendments, committee work.
- Quote allies (Khanna, Davidson, Paul) when they reinforce the message.
- Use "two Republicans voted yes" (Massie + Davidson) to normalize principle over party.

### Strategy 2: Reframe the vocabulary

**Principle:** Don't cede "America First," "MAGA," or "conservative" to the opposition. Reclaim them on Massie's terms.

**Tactics:**
- "This is not America First" (re: war without vote).
- "Won't Make America Great Again; it will bankrupt us" (re: foreign aid).
- "Congress must decide war" — constitutional, not partisan.

### Strategy 3: Evidence over emotion

**Principle:** Massie's voice is analytical. Attacks on him often rely on labels. Counter with facts.

**Tactics:**
- Cite Club for Growth, FreedomWorks, NTU ratings when "RINO" or "weak" appears.
- Link to vote records, amendments, Epstein discharge petition.
- "Let's set the record straight" + Claim/Verdict structure for fact-checking.

### Strategy 4: Ally amplification

**Principle:** When allies post in support, amplify. It dilutes the "Massie is alone" narrative.

**Tactics:**
- Quote-tweet Khanna, Davidson, Paul, Jordan when they post on war powers, Epstein, or Massie.
- "Two Republicans" / "bipartisan" framing when Khanna-Massie work is relevant.
- Share Responsible Statecraft, The Hill, local KY coverage that's favorable.

### Strategy 5: Supporter segmentation

**Principle:** Different supporters care about different things. Rotate message angles.

**Tactics:**
- **Libertarian:** Constitutional restraint, omnibus no-votes.
- **Anti-war:** Iran, $8T, "radicalize new terrorists."
- **Transparency:** Epstein files, DOJ, discharge petition.
- **Fiscal:** Foreign aid in appropriations, deficit.
- **KY-4:** Local ties, district events, Rand Paul.

### Strategy 6: Adversary engagement rules

**Principle:** Don't feed the trolls. Don't make it personal. State principle and move on.

**Tactics:**
- **Gallrein:** Rarely mention by name. When contrast is needed: "Principle over endorsement" or "Record speaks."
- **Trump:** No personal attacks. Reframe his vocabulary. Conciliatory option: narrative-action alignment (advise, don't insult).
- **MAGA spam:** Fact-check when false; ignore when noise. Don't quote-tweet every attack.

### Strategy 7: Recursive learning

**Principle:** Track what gets engagement (likes, RTs, replies). Double down on what works.

**Tactics:**
- Note which topics resonate: Iran, Epstein, foreign aid, constitutional.
- Note which formats work: singles vs threads, quote-tweets vs original.
- Feed observations back; refine drafts.
