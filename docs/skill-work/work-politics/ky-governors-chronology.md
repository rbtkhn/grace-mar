# Kentucky — Governors chronology (work-politics source)

**Status:** Draft reference. **Not** Record truth. Use for WORK framing and the **jurisdiction-campaign-history** Cursor skill (Design A: `office: governor` or `stack`).

The **Governor of the Commonwealth of Kentucky** is the state’s chief executive. **Party labels** in the 19th century (Jeffersonian-Republican, Whig, American / Know Nothing) are **not** the same as modern Democratic / Republican alignment. A **1992** constitutional amendment allowed **two consecutive** terms; governors remain **term-limited** afterward (see current constitution).

**Confederate shadow government:** During the Civil War, a secessionist faction claimed a separate Kentucky governorship ([George W. Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_W._Johnson_(governor)), [Richard Hawes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Hawes)). It did **not** displace the Union government in Frankfort. Treat as a **separate** historical thread, not continuity with the state executive list below.

## Notable historical anchors (selected)

- **William Goebel (1900):** Only U.S. governor **assassinated** in office; disputed 1899 election. See [Wikipedia — William Goebel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Goebel) and scholarly sources before using in contrast ads.
- **Repeat governors (non-consecutive):** Isaac Shelby, John L. Helm, James B. McCreary, Happy Chandler—multiple **separate** terms under older rules. See [Wikipedia — List of governors of Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_governors_of_Kentucky).
- **Governors who resigned for Senate:** Pattern appears multiple times (e.g. Stanley 1919, Chandler 1945, Clements 1950, Ford 1974); use **Bioguide + NGA** to verify any specific claim.

## Campaign / framing hygiene

- **Frankfort vs D.C.:** Governor history informs **state** trust and scandal arcs; it does **not** substitute for a federal voting record unless you state the mechanism (e.g. “then left for Senate”).
- **Term limits:** Post-1992 rules differ from the 19th century; do not imply a founder-era governor “could not run again” with modern language without qualification.
