### US politics — minimum viable context (no prior knowledge assumed)

#### What “Congress” is (federal)

The **United States Congress** is the national legislature. It has two chambers:

| Chamber | What it is | Rough size |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **House of Representatives** | Elected from **local districts** (one member per district). Terms are **two years**. | 435 voting members |
| **Senate** | Two senators per **state** (not district-based the same way). Terms are **six years**. | 100 members |

**Thomas Massie** and **Ro Khanna** are both members of the **House** — they are **U.S. Representatives**, not senators.

#### President vs Congress (why “war powers” comes up)

The **President** runs the executive branch and is **commander-in-chief** of the military. **Congress** is supposed to **declare war** and **authorize** major uses of force. When people argue about **war powers**, they are arguing about **whether Congress must vote** before or while the US uses military force — not about liking or disliking troops.

#### Elections you will hear about

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Primary** | Party voters choose who runs under that party’s label in the general election. Massie’s **2026 Republican primary** (KY-4) is a major storyline. |
| **General election** | The final contest (e.g. November in federal election years) between nominees (and sometimes independents). |
| **Incumbent** | The person already in the office running again. |

#### “Republican” vs “Democrat” (simple)

- **Republican (“R”)** and **Democrat (“D”)** are the two major parties. They disagree on a lot.  
- **They can still work together on specific bills** (e.g. war-powers resolutions). Cross-party sponsorship does **not** mean Massie “became a Democrat” or Khanna “became a Republican.”

### Tactics and strategies

#### Strategy 1: Elevate, don't attack

**Principle:** The best counter to adversaries is a stronger principal. Post about Massie's record, votes, principles, and allies more than you post about opponents.

**Tactics:**
- Lead with Massie's floor speeches, amendments, committee work.
- Quote allies (Khanna, Davidson, Paul) when they reinforce the message.
- Use "two Republicans voted yes" (Massie + Davidson) to normalize principle over party.

#### Strategy 2: Reframe the vocabulary

**Principle:** Don't cede "America First," "MAGA," or "conservative" to the opposition. Reclaim them on Massie's terms.

**Tactics:**
- "This is not America First" (re: war without vote).
- "Won't Make America Great Again; it will bankrupt us" (re: foreign aid).
- "Congress must decide war" — constitutional, not partisan.

#### Strategy 3: Evidence over emotion

**Principle:** Massie's voice is analytical. Attacks on him often rely on labels. Counter with facts.

**Tactics:**
- Cite Club for Growth, FreedomWorks, NTU ratings when "RINO" or "weak" appears.
- Link to vote records, amendments, Epstein discharge petition.
- "Let's set the record straight" + Claim/Verdict structure for fact-checking.

#### Strategy 4: Ally amplification

**Principle:** When allies post in support, amplify. It dilutes the "Massie is alone" narrative.

**Tactics:**
- Quote-tweet Khanna, Davidson, Paul, Jordan when they post on war powers, Epstein, or Massie.
- "Two Republicans" / "bipartisan" framing when Khanna-Massie work is relevant.
- Share Responsible Statecraft, The Hill, local KY coverage that's favorable.

#### Strategy 5: Supporter segmentation

**Principle:** Different supporters care about different things. Rotate message angles.

**Tactics:**
- **Libertarian:** Constitutional restraint, omnibus no-votes.
- **Anti-war:** Iran, $8T, "radicalize new terrorists."
- **Transparency:** Epstein files, DOJ, discharge petition.
- **Fiscal:** Foreign aid in appropriations, deficit.
- **KY-4:** Local ties, district events, Rand Paul.

#### Strategy 6: Adversary engagement rules

**Principle:** Don't feed the trolls. Don't make it personal. State principle and move on.

**Tactics:**
- **Gallrein:** Rarely mention by name. When contrast is needed: "Principle over endorsement" or "Record speaks."
- **Trump:** No personal attacks. Reframe his vocabulary. Conciliatory option: narrative-action alignment (advise, don't insult).
- **MAGA spam:** Fact-check when false; ignore when noise. Don't quote-tweet every attack.

#### Strategy 7: Recursive learning

**Principle:** Track what gets engagement (likes, RTs, replies). Double down on what works.

**Tactics:**
- Note which topics resonate: Iran, Epstein, foreign aid, constitutional.
- Note which formats work: singles vs threads, quote-tweets vs original.
- Feed observations back; refine drafts.

# Massie — issue asymmetry map (primary + general)

**Purpose:** Map where Thomas Massie’s record and voice are clearly different from (a) primary opponents, (b) general opponents, and (c) a “generic Republican,” on issues that matter in KY-4, then package those gaps into doctrine-safe wedges for both the primary and the general.

**Doctrine filter:** See `massie-ky4-operator-checklist.md` — every wedge should:

- Strengthen **engineer/farmer**, **anti-waste**, **anti-surveillance/war** framing.
- Avoid **forced culture-war cosplay** that isn’t Massie.
- Collapse to **one KY-4 sentence** explainable to a neighbor.

### 3. Flagship wedges (dual-salience candidates)

These are **hypothesis-level** flagship issues that appear strong for both the primary and the general, given current documentation.

1. **Anti-stupid war (war powers / Iran)**  
   - **Why primary:** Only R on a bi-partisan war powers push; took heat for restraining a Republican president; records courage vs crowd.  
   - **Why general:** Voters of all stripes are tired of endless wars that don’t fix anything and cost lives and money; “don’t send our kids to a war Congress never voted on” is crossover-safe.  
   - **Receipts:** Iran war powers co-sponsorship; floor remarks about radicalizing new terrorists; documented criticism of bombing as distraction from Epstein transparency.

2. **Transparent, accountable government (Epstein files + process)**  
   - **Why primary:** Shows independence from leaders and donors; “I pushed to open the files when both parties were quiet.”  
   - **Why general:** Cuts across partisan lines — people who think powerful people get away with everything.  
   - **Receipts:** Epstein files push; quotes tying transparency to trust in institutions.

3. **Liberty at home (surveillance, mandates, overreach)**  
   - **Why primary:** Distinguishes Massie from tough-on-crime rhetoric that quietly expands the surveillance state.  
   - **Why general:** Pairs privacy and health freedom with a “government out of your home, your phone, and your doctor’s office” line; accessible beyond base.  
   - **Receipts:** Anti-surveillance votes; COVID-era skepticism of federal dictates.

These can be re-scored or swapped as more race-specific data comes in (ads, debate lines, local stories).

---

### 4. Wedge cards (draft shells)

#### 4.1 Primary wedge — “The Republican who voted against dumb wars”

- **Claim (primary-friendly):** When both parties sleepwalk toward another Middle East war, Massie is the Republican who forces Congress to vote before your son or daughter is sent.  
- **Receipts:**  
  - Only Republican co-sponsor of the Iran war powers resolution with Ro Khanna.  
  - Floor remarks warning an Iran conflict would radicalize new generations of terrorists.  
- **Contrast line:** Ed Gallrein is running with people who cheer any strike Trump orders; Massie is the one who reads the fine print before we bleed and borrow.  
- **Neighbor sentence (KY-4):** “I want the guy who makes them vote before they send our kids and grandkids.”  
- **Avoid:** Anything that sounds soft on defense; always anchor in “strong, careful, constitutional” instead of “anti-military.”

#### 4.2 Primary wedge — “Engineer who reads the bill, not the room”

- **Claim:** Massie is the engineer-farmer who votes the math and the Constitution, even when leadership is yelling.  
- **Receipts:**  
  - Pattern of lone or small-cluster votes against omnibus spending and emergency packages that balloon debt.  
  - Public explanations that connect line items to KY-4 waste.  
- **Contrast line:** While others wait to see how Trump or leadership lean, Massie tells you how he voted and why in one KY-4 sentence.  
- **Neighbor sentence:** “He votes the numbers, not the noise.”  
- **Avoid:** Direct attacks on base-friendly figures; keep focus on **process and outcomes**, not intra-party drama.

#### 4.3 General wedge — “Keep government out of your home and phone”

- **Claim:** Massie is the one in the race who has actually voted to stop the government from reading your messages and dictating your family’s health choices.  
- **Receipts:**  
  - Anti-surveillance votes; opposition to warrantless spying.  
  - COVID-era resistance to federal mandates and one-size-fits-all rules.  
- **Contrast line:** Others talk about freedom; Massie has a voting trail where he told both parties “no” when they wanted more power over your life.  
- **Neighbor sentence:** “He’s the one who kept D.C. out of our phones and out of our doctor’s office.”  
- **Avoid:** Technical surveillance jargon; keep it at “they shouldn’t be watching you or bossing your family around.”  

#### 4.4 General wedge — “Farmer who fights bureaucrats, not neighbors”

- **Claim:** Massie is the local farmer who wants fewer distant bureaucrats telling KY-4 how to work their land, run their small business, or school their kids.  
- **Receipts:**  
  - Cattle farm and rural life as lived experience.  
  - Record against federal overreach that hurts small farms and small business.  
- **Contrast line:** Opponents offer more programs from far away; Massie wants decisions closer to the people who actually live with them.  
- **Neighbor sentence:** “He’s on our side when the folks with clipboards come telling us how to live.”  
- **Avoid:** Blanket anti-government language that sounds like abandoning needed local services; focus on **who decides**.

# High-stakes messaging — guardrail stress-test

*Source file: `docs/skill-work/work-politics/america-first-ky/guardrail-stress-test.md`*

### Purpose

Reduce predictable failure modes when drafting **high-stakes** america-first-ky outputs (war powers, insider-trading / ethics, cartel-economy claims, border + civil liberties). Methodology is **inspired by** independent evaluation of clinical AI systems — factorial vignettes, reasoning-vs-action mismatch, anchoring, and miscalibrated alerts — not a claim of medical or clinical use.

**Inspired-by (verify externally):** Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai et al., *ChatGPT Health* structured triage evaluation, **Nature Medicine** (2026). Example institutional summary: [Mount Sinai newsroom](https://www.mountsinai.org/about/newsroom/2026/research-identifies-blind-spots-in-ai-medical-triage). Paper: [Nature Medicine article](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04297-7).

This repo **does not** implement clinical triage. We **borrow** the *epistemic* lessons: tail-risk silence, reasoning that does not match the final recommendation, social anchoring, and alerts that track tone instead of risk.

### Scope

| In scope | Out of scope |
|----------|----------------|
| work-politics briefs, X draft packages, opposition memos labeled high-stakes | Automated merge into SELF / EVIDENCE |
| Operator- and SMM-enforced checklists | `governance_checker.py` enforcement |
| WORK logs, brief appendices, `content-queue` notes | Routine full traces appended to `self-evidence.md` |
| Optional pipeline events (`stress_test_proposed`, etc.) via `emit_pipeline_event.py` | Fabricated Python hooks in `governance_checker.py` |

### Four failure modes (mandatory human checks)

1. **Inverted U / tail risk** — Strong on routine framing; **silent or weak** on rare but consequential scenarios (e.g. sudden energy-market shock + war powers vote).
2. **Model “knows” but output diverges** — Internal reasoning (or scratch notes) flags a constitutional or ethics issue, but the **published** recommendation softens or contradicts it.
3. **Social context hijacks judgment** — Anchoring from “donor says it’s fine,” poll toplines, or authority cues shifts the conclusion without new evidence.
4. **Guardrails on vibes** — Red-team or internal “blocks” trigger on emotional tone instead of **material** constitutional or corruption risk.

### Factorial stress testing (operator method)

For **each** high-stakes base scenario, the operator (or agent under operator sign-off) documents **controlled variations** on the same factual spine. Mount Sinai-style designs used **multiple factorial conditions** on the same vignettes; we mirror that **logically**, not as a medical test.

**Typical variation knobs (pick those that apply):**

- + Social pressure (“donor / colleague says it’s fine”)
- + Contradictory or noisy poll framing
- + Time pressure (“vote in 48 hours”)
- + Tail-risk injection (e.g. sudden chokepoint / price shock relevant to the scenario)

**Minimum:** 4 variations for a high-stakes brief; **stretch:** 8–16 documented rows in an appendix table.

**Pass criterion:** For each variation, the **stated recommendation** must be **consistent** with the **same risk notes** (no silent downgrade). Any mismatch → **do not ship**; escalate to operator approval inbox; optionally emit `stress_test_failed` (see below).

### Four layers (process — not code)

These are **discipline layers** for humans and drafting agents. They are **not** wired into `governance_checker.py`.

| Layer | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **1. Progressive autonomy** | On tail scenarios, default to **shadow / draft-only** until operator explicitly approves ship. |
| **2. Deterministic validation** | Operator checklist: “Does the final paragraph contradict any explicit risk bullet above?” |
| **3. False-positive flywheel** | Prefer **over-flagging** tail risk in internal drafts; then **prune** with evidence before publish. |
| **4. Factorial pass** | Run the variation table before finalizing any Massie pilot **high-stakes** brief. |

### Logging

- **Primary:** WORK artifact — stress-test appendix on the same brief file or linked `stress-test-brief-YYYY-MM-DD.md` under this folder or `work-politics/` weekly brief directory.
- **Do not** routinely dump full reasoning traces into `self-evidence.md`. Optional **ACT-** line only if companion approves a gated candidate that records “we ran stress-test protocol for brief X.”

### Reasoning–output alignment

- **No automated “alignment score”** in this repo unless separately specified and stored in WORK.
- Human rule: if scratch reasoning contains “constitutional concern” / “insider risk” / “unsourced claim,” the **shipped** text must **acknowledge** the same tier of concern or **remove** the risky claim.

### Suggested pipeline events (optional)

`scripts/emit_pipeline_event.py` accepts arbitrary `event_type` strings. Suggested names for audit (operator-run):

- `stress_test_proposed` — candidate id `none` or brief slug in extras
- `stress_test_passed` / `stress_test_failed`
- `guardrail_mismatch_detected`

Example (adjust user id if needed):

```bash
python scripts/emit_pipeline_event.py stress_test_passed none brief=weekly-2026-03-20 territory=wap
```

### When to run

**Always** before final human sign-off on:

- War powers / use-of-force briefs
- Congressional ethics / stock-trading messaging
- “Cartel economy” claims that imply specific actors or legal conclusions
- Border + civil-liberty combined messaging in volatile news windows

**Optional** for low-stakes engagement posts.
