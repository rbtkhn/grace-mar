## Executive summary

**Question:** Who counts among the most **active, audible pro-Israel advocates** on X, what **quantitative** descriptors exist in open sources, and what **patterns** repeat?

**Findings (high level):**

1. **No single authoritative “top 30.”** Commercial rankings (e.g. Favikon) optimize **reach and niche scores** inside a geography; academic crisis studies (e.g. UW CIP) optimize **tweet views** on keywords over a **short window**. Neither equals moral endorsement or stable “advocacy” labeling — some high-reach accounts are **general news/OSINT**, not dedicated Israel advocates.

2. **Two different “scales” of metrics:** (a) **platform-native** — followers, views, posting volume, Favikon-style composite scores; (b) **survey-based impact** — e.g. Tel Aviv Institute / *Jewish Journal* polling on **persuasion and trust** for a small panel of named influencers (not a full market census).

3. **Crisis-era pattern (CIP, Oct 7–10 2023):** A handful of **high-velocity** accounts can out-earn **legacy wire accounts** on **topic-relevant views** in the first 72 hours — driven by **posting frequency**, **visual/emotional framing**, and **algorithmic amplification** (the CIP report also notes **prior engagement from X ownership** for several “new elite” accounts). **Pro-Israel** is **not** uniform across that list; some accounts are **geopolitical aggregators** with mixed alignment.

4. **Advocacy-effectiveness pattern (TLVI survey, reported 2024):** Among surveyed English-speaking users exposed to Israel–Hamas war discourse, **Hen Mazzig** registered the strongest **individual** attitudinal impact; **network effects** appear when respondents are counted against a **broader TLVI-affiliated set** — exact percentages are **survey-dependent** and should be cited from the primary article, not rounded from memory.

5. **Structural trend:** **State and NGO-adjacent “influencer” programs** (e.g. U.S. DOJ–visible paid campaigns reported in press) sit **beside** organic creators — any **top-N list** should tag **(organic)** vs **(disclosed/paid)** when evidence exists, or abstain.

6. **Risk:** Lists that **merge** U.S. partisan commentators, Israeli-resident activists, journalists, and **outlet-style** accounts (@Visegrad24-style) confuse **audience size** with **single-issue advocacy** — use **plane tags** (scope / material facts / narrative) per [template-three-lenses](../analytical-lenses/template-three-lenses.md) when repurposing this memo.

## Patterns and trends

1. **Ranking method drives who appears “on top.”** Favikon favors **Israel-resident** creators with **consistent X engagement**; U.S. **megaphones** (Shapiro-scale) need a **parallel column** (followers) not mixed naively with Favikon scores.

2. **Survey-based “impact”** (TLVI) measures **persuasion** among users already in the war-discourse pool — **not** reach to the **unconvinced** majority.

3. **Crisis “new elites”** combine **high cadence**, **visuals**, and **platform-level attention** — **organic advocacy** accounts compete in the **same attention economy** as **neutral-ish aggregators**.

4. **Disclosure gap:** Paid or **coordinated** advocacy (press-reported **DOJ-visible** programs) means **top-N organic lists** understate **institutional** reach unless **disclosure metadata** is tracked.

5. **Long-run trend:** Traditional **wire** accounts retain **subscriber mass** but can trail **per-tweet views** in **fast-burn** conflicts — **CIP** documented **views per tweet** and **volume** effects.
