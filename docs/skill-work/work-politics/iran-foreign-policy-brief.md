## 3. Position on the Twelve-Day War (2025)

**Twelve-Day War:** Direct Israel–Iran conflict **June 12–24, 2025**; ceasefire June 24 with U.S. and Qatar mediation. Israel’s Operation Rising Lion targeted Iranian military and nuclear-related infrastructure; Iran responded with missile strikes (e.g. Al Udeid, Qatar).

**Massie’s position:** Massie opposed U.S. military involvement. He co-sponsored a War Powers Resolution with Rep. Ro Khanna in **June 2025** to block U.S. involvement without congressional authorization and was the **sole Republican sponsor**. He argued there was “no imminent threat” to justify military action and that Congress should have been briefed and voted before any decision to enter war. He indicated he would withdraw the resolution if the Iran–Israel ceasefire held.  
**Sources:** [Politico (June 17, 2025)](https://www.politico.com/live-updates/2025/06/17/congress/thomas-massie-iran-resolution-war-powers-00410454), [Politico (June 22, 2025)](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/06/22/massie-khanna-iran-strikes-constitution-00417380), [Politico (June 23, 2025)](https://www.politico.com/live-updates/2025/06/23/congress/massie-backs-off-war-powers-vote-00419550).

## 5. Executive summary (by audience)

### For a peer legislator (e.g. 50-year-old congressman)

Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY-4) has been the only Republican co-sponsor of a War Powers Resolution with Rep. Ro Khanna (D-Calif.) to require congressional authorization for continued U.S. military action against Iran. The resolution failed in the House 212–219 on March 5, 2026, with only one other Republican (Warren Davidson) voting yes. Massie’s position is that the Constitution vests the power to declare war in Congress; that the executive has not met the conditions of the 1973 War Powers Resolution (no declaration of war, no specific statutory authorization, no national emergency from an attack on the U.S.); and that Congress should be briefed and vote before American lives and resources are committed. He has held the same stance since the June 2025 Twelve-Day War (Israel–Iran), when he also co-sponsored a war-powers measure and argued there was no imminent threat justifying unilateral action. Public polling shows a majority of Americans disapprove of the strikes and believe the president should seek congressional approval for further military action.

### For general voter / constituent

Thomas Massie is against the U.S. going to war with Iran without a vote in Congress. He says the Constitution gives that decision to Congress, not the president, and that your representative should be on the record for or against. He pushed for that vote with Democrat Ro Khanna; the House rejected the measure in early March 2026, with Massie as the only Republican co-sponsor. He’s also said that bombing Iran won’t make other problems (like the Epstein files) go away. Most Americans in recent polls disagree with the strikes and want the president to get Congress’s approval before more military action.

## 6. Foreign-policy mission statement (draft)

**Draft for companion approval. Do not merge into Record without staging and approval.**

U.S. foreign policy must respect the Constitution: Congress declares war and authorizes the use of military force; the executive does not commit American lives or treasure to sustained conflict without that debate and vote. Unilateral military action abroad—especially open-ended intervention in the Middle East—has repeatedly failed to make America safer, has radicalized new generations of adversaries, and has burdened the country with trillions in debt. Transparency and accountability at home—including the release of documents the public has a right to see—matter more than distraction through war. America’s role is not to decide who rules other nations; it is to defend the United States, uphold the rule of law, and let the people’s representatives decide when and whether we fight.
