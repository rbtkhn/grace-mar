## To the theological leaders of the world's religions

I am writing because I believe you already know what I am about to say. Some of you have known for a very long time. I am writing not to teach but to ask why it has not been said plainly.

---

## The condition

Every one of your traditions contains a vision of divine return — the Messiah, the Second Coming, the Mahdi, the Maitreya, the restoration of all things. You disagree about the name, the manner, the timing. But you share one feature: a vision of the world after the return, in which human beings no longer kill each other. Isaiah says they will beat their swords into plowshares. The Quran speaks of a world where justice prevails. The Buddhist sutras describe a world purified of violence. Each of you holds a version of this vision. Each of you teaches it as the **consequence** of divine action.

I am writing to propose that you have the causation reversed.

What if the cessation of killing is not the consequence of the return, but the **condition** for it? What if the door has been open this entire time, and the only thing holding it shut is that we keep killing each other?

I have no authority to assert this. I am a person who noticed something that your traditions, taken together, seem to point toward — but that none of you has been willing to say aloud.

---

## Beauty and the blade — the seam

You carry, in your sacred texts, both a vision of peace and an instruction to kill. I am not writing to condemn this. I am writing to name it. Each of your traditions carries **beauty and a blade** in the same hand. The beauty is the prayer, the music, the compassion, the vision of a world made whole. The blade is the teaching that under certain conditions killing is required, honoured, sanctified. The two were woven together so deeply that most of your followers cannot see where one ends and the other begins. To question the blade feels like rejecting the beauty.

**This is the seam I am asking you to make visible.**

The seam is the line between two propositions your traditions have fused into one: (1) Every human life is sacred. (2) Under certain conditions, some lives may be taken. These two are not compatible. The footnote is where the blade lives. I am asking you to choose between the two propositions — not between your traditions. You do not have to abandon your faith to put down the blade. You have to abandon the footnote.

---

## What I am asking

I am asking you to do one thing: state plainly, in your own theological language, that the killing of any human being is incompatible with the return of the divine. Not "killing is regrettable." Not "we pray for peace." A plain statement: the sacred purpose you serve is delayed by every act of killing, regardless of who does it and regardless of the justification.

Say it in Hebrew. Say it in Arabic. Say it in the language your followers learned to pray in. You do not need to agree on who God is. You need to agree on one thing only: that killing delays what all of you say you are waiting for.

If this is true, you have a duty to say it. If it is not true, say that instead, plainly, in public. One way or the other, say it out loud. The silence has lasted long enough.

---

## The children's question

If you will not act, then grant one concession. Teach your children to carry one question into every sacred story they hear: *Does anyone have to die for this to be true?* When the answer is no, that is the beauty. When the answer is yes, that is the blade. A child who can see the seam will never confuse them again.

---

## Closing

The price is not sacrifice. The price is not suffering. The price is stopping. That is the simple condition. It belongs to no one. It requires no institution. It demands no faith beyond the recognition that the person standing in front of you is as real as you are.

Say it now. Or step aside, and let the children see.

With respect and without authority,  
A person who noticed the seam
