## 1. Gemology — Identification & Science

### Properties for distinguishing gems

*Source: Wade (1918)*

Stones are distinguished by their **properties**. Numerical properties (density, hardness, refractive index, dispersion) are more reliable than color alone. "It is because gem dealers so often rely upon the more obvious sort of property, such as color, that they so frequently make mistakes."

Key tests:
- **Refraction** — Each stone has a definite refractive index. Herbert-Smith refractometer (limit 1.80) excludes zircon, diamond, sphene, demantoid garnet.
- **Double refraction** — Diamond, garnet, spinel refract singly; most other stones cause light to split. Useful for identification.
- **Specific gravity** — Determined by weighing in air vs. water.
- **Hardness** — Mohs scale; diamond scratches all others.
- **Dispersion** — Separation of light into colors; produces "fire" in brilliants.
- **Color** — Often misleading; use with numerical tests.

### Synthetic vs. natural

*Source: Wade (1918)*

"Synthetic" stones (e.g., scientific rubies, sapphires) are chemically identical to natural but man-made. Imitations (glass, paste) differ in composition. Bubbles, scratches, and refractive tests distinguish them. "Only the other day a ring was brought to me that had been bought for a genuine emerald... The material was green glass."

## 3. Cutting & Lapidary

*Source: Wade (1918), Sinkankas (1962), Dake (1954)*

### Diamond cutting

Distinct trade from colored-stone lapidary. Steps:
1. **Cleaving** — Split along crystal grain (octahedral faces). Nick + steel blade + light blow.
2. **Bruting** — "Diamond cut diamond"; two stones rotate against each other to form round (or fancy) shape.
3. **Polishing** — Copper dop, iron lap charged with diamond dust, oil, ~2,000 rpm. Table first, then 32 top facets, then 24 back facets + culet.
4. **Angles** — ~35° top, ~41° back for ideal brilliancy; diamond has critical angle ~24° for total internal reflection.

### Colored stones

1. **Slitting** — Thin metal disc charged with diamond dust; slices rough.
2. **Cleaving** — For topaz, feldspars (moonstone, amazonite, labradorite); spodumene cleaves easily (durability concern).
3. **Rubbing down** — Coarse emery or carborundum on soft metal lap or vertical wheel. Diamond-charged laps for hard stones (ruby, sapphire).
4. **Faceting** — Stone on holder (ginpeg); metal lap with diamond dust or carborundum. Multiple tiers of facets.
5. **Polishing** — Fine abrasives (corundum, tripoli, pumice, putty powder). Different lap materials and speeds for different stones. Trade secrets abound.

### Cut forms

*Source: Wade (1918)*

- **Cabochon** — Oldest form; rounded top, flat or slightly convex base. From French *caboche* (bald head). Best thickness ~half the spread for total reflection. East Indian preference; saves weight.
- **Rose cut** — Facets on rounded top; 24 triangular facets; thickness ~half diameter.
- **Brilliant** — Standard for diamond; steep back angles for colored stones (39° top, 44° back) for brilliancy; may be altered to deepen or lighten color.
- **Step cut** — Rectangular facets; deeper color.

### Optical considerations

Tourmaline: cut table on *side* of prism (not perpendicular) or color too dense. Blue sapphire: cut *across* prism axis or greenish cast. Ruby: parallel to axis gives pure red but less dichroism.

### Selling & merchant education

*Source: Wade (1918)*

"Because of the rapid increase in knowledge about precious stones on the part of the buying public, it has become necessary for the gem merchant and his clerks and salesmen to know at least as much about the subject of gemology as their better informed customers." Study in spare moments; practical study of gems in-hand essential. "The frequent application of what is acquired, as opportunity offers, in connection with ordinary salesmanship, will help fix the subject and at the same time increase sales."
