## What to avoid

Do not end a paragraph (especially the **last sentence**) with a **stack** of:

1. **Accounting metaphors** — “sends a bill,” “the price,” “pays,” “tab,” “ledger,” “invoice,” **plus**
2. **Diffuse moral abstractions** — “trust,” “truth,” “noise,” “signal,” “legitimacy,” **plus**
3. **Universal inclusivity tags** — “everyone,” “all sides,” “we all,” often after an **em dash**.

**Example of the dispreferred pattern:** *“…collapses into noise, and noise in war still sends a bill everyone pays in lost trust—all sides included.”*

That pattern **sounds** weighty but **under-specifies** who loses what, and it loads several vague ideas into one line without a concrete anchor.

---

## Prefer instead

- **One** concrete consequence (actor + cost + when), **or**
- **Cut** the closer; let the prior sentence land, **or**
- A **single** image or metaphor **without** a second ledger/trust layer in the same sentence.

---

## Heuristic

If the sentence could be replaced by **“And that’s bad”** without losing specific content, it is probably **bloat** — rewrite or delete.

---

## When this rule does not apply

- **Quoted** material or deliberate **voice-matching**
- **Poetry, liturgy, satire**, or other registers where stacked abstraction is the **point**
- **Technical** writing where “bill,” “trust,” etc. have literal domain meaning (legislation, TLS, etc.) — judge by **closer pattern**, not keyword bans
