## Summary

AI is creating something we have not had since Babel: a single technical-linguistic layer through which much of the world’s communication and cognition may flow. That can support coordination across languages and cultures—or it can reproduce the conditions that, in the ancient story, led to scattering. This memo outlines a Babel-aware framework for AI policy so we harness the upside (one subject, many tongues) while avoiding the pitfalls (one tower, one name, unchecked power).

---

## The Babel Analogy

In the Babel story, humanity builds one city, one tower, and one project: “make a name for ourselves” so that “nothing they plan to do will be impossible.” The result is confusion of language and scattering. The failures are:

1. **One name** — One story, one brand, one narrative to which everyone must conform.  
2. **Refusal to fill the earth** — Concentration of power and meaning in one place instead of many.  
3. **Unbounded ambition** — No limit, no check, no counterweight to a single project.

AI can replicate these risks: one dominant stack, one optimization, one “voice” through which others are filtered. It can also support the alternative: many tongues and cultures coordinating on shared goals (one subject) without losing their distinct languages or traditions. Policy should explicitly favor the second and guard against the first.

---

## Principles for AI Policy

### 1. One subject, not one name.

**Goal:** Use AI to help the world coordinate on shared objectives (safety, resilience, fairness, accountability) without imposing one narrative, one culture, or one “name.”

**Implications:**
- Support **multilingual, multicultural AI** and local/regional models, not only a single global monoculture.
- Fund and promote **translation, alignment, and interoperability** across diverse AI systems and data sources so different communities can “speak” to each other on shared subjects.
- Reject policy that effectively mandates one technical standard, one set of values, or one “correct” way to use AI as the only option.

### 2. Fill the earth — distribute, don’t concentrate.

**Goal:** Avoid a single “tower”: one company, one country, or one platform as the sole gatekeeper of the new linguistic-cognitive layer.

**Implications:**
- Promote **plurality of models, providers, and infrastructure** (open weights, public benchmarks, competitive markets).
- Support **sovereign and regional capability** so that nations and communities can run, fine-tune, and govern AI in their own context.
- Use antitrust, procurement, and international norms to prevent one or two actors from controlling the core AI “tower.”

### 3. Preserve limits — no “nothing will be impossible.”

**Goal:** Keep the seam visible: the same technology that coordinates can dominate; the same system that translates can flatten. Ensure checks and boundaries.

**Implications:**
- **Safety and accountability** (audits, liability, transparency) so that no single system is beyond scrutiny.
- **Human-in-the-loop and democratic oversight** for high-stakes uses (e.g., critical infrastructure, defense, law enforcement).
- **Norms and red lines** (e.g., no autonomous weapons without meaningful human control, no unchecked mass manipulation) so that “what we plan to do” remains bounded by law and ethics.

### 4. Humility, not ascent.

**Goal:** Frame AI as a tool for coordination and service to many tongues and many places, not as a project to “reach heaven” under one flag.

**Implications:**
- Emphasize **AI for the common good** (health, education, climate, resilience) and for **strengthening pluralism**, not for replacing it.
- In international forums, argue for **shared subjects** (e.g., safety, non-proliferation, labor and dignity) that many nations can endorse in their own terms, rather than one universal creed.
- Avoid rhetoric or policy that suggests the US or any single actor should “own” or “define” the future of human cognition.

---

## One concrete change: Federal AI procurement

To turn the framework into a checkable rule, add a **procurement condition** for major federal AI systems used in public-facing or government-wide functions (e.g., translation, citizen-facing chatbots, benefits or decision-support): **(1) Multilingual requirement** — The system must support a defined minimum set of languages (e.g., top 10–15 by U.S. population and strategic need), with clear performance and accessibility criteria, not “English-first, others later.” **(2) Interoperability and substitutability** — Contracts must require standard interfaces (e.g., open APIs, common data formats) and the ability to switch providers within a defined period (e.g., 18–24 months) without losing core capability. No de facto permanent lock-in to a single vendor as the only “tower.”

This is a single, auditable condition: either a solicitation or contract meets it or it does not. It advances “one subject, many tongues” (many languages supported) and “fill the earth” (no single, irreplaceable provider). **Where it lives:** OMB policy memorandum or guidance on federal AI acquisition, with corresponding updates to the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) or agency supplements so that contracting officers must apply it for in-scope acquisitions.

---

## Recommended Actions

1. **Direct OMB** to issue guidance implementing the federal AI procurement condition above (multilingual + interoperability/substitutability) and to coordinate with agencies on FAR or agency-supplement updates for in-scope AI acquisitions.  
2. **Direct OSTP and relevant agencies** to incorporate the above principles into the implementation of the Executive Order on AI and into cross-government AI strategy.  
3. **Instruct State and NSC** to use the “one subject, many tongues” frame in multilateral AI discussions (e.g., safety, standards, human rights) so we seek coordination on shared goals without demanding one name or one tower.  
4. **Task Commerce, DOJ, and FTC** with assessing concentration in foundation models, cloud AI, and data, and with recommending measures to preserve plurality and “fill the earth.”  
5. **Include in the next National AI Strategy** an explicit commitment to multilingual, multicultural AI and to distributed capability, with milestones and reporting.

---

## Conclusion

AI can either repeat the mistakes of Babel—one name, one tower, unbounded ambition—or it can support a world in which many tongues and many cultures coordinate on one subject (safety, dignity, peace) without being scattered by pride or concentrated power. This framework is offered so that our policy choices explicitly favor the second path.
